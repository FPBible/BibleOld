1 Peter 5

Shepherding through the Final Transition

Hope: The Crowning Glory Context: Soon-Coming Appearing

Scripture (WEB)

1Therefore I exhort the elders among you, as a fellow elder, and a witness of the sufferings of Christ, and who will also share in the glory that will be revealed: 2shepherd the flock of God which is among you, exercising oversight, not by compulsion, but willingly; not for dishonest gain, but eagerly; 3neither as lording it over those entrusted to you, but making yourselves examples to the flock. 4When the chief Shepherd is revealed, you will receive the crown of glory that doesn’t fade away. 8Be sober and self-controlled. Be watchful. Your adversary, the devil, walks around like a roaring lion, seeking whom he may devour. 9Withstand him steadfast in your faith, knowing that your brothers who are in the world are undergoing the same sufferings. 10But may the God of all grace, who called you to his eternal glory in Christ Jesus, after you have suffered a little while, perfect, establish, strengthen, and settle you.

Fulfillment Commentary

The Glory Ready to be Revealed (v. 1)

Peter identifies himself not only as a witness of Christ's past sufferings but as a partaker of the "glory that will be revealed." In the Greek (mellein), this indicates a glory that was "about to be revealed." This echoes his statement in Chapter 1 about a salvation "ready to be revealed in the last time." Peter's expectation was centered on the imminent manifestation of Christ's kingdom power.

The Chief Shepherd's Appearing (v. 4)

The promise to the elders is that they would receive an unfading crown of glory when the "Chief Shepherd is revealed." If this appearing were thousands of years away, the specific incentive to those first-century elders would lose its immediate force. Within the context of Peter's eschatology, this appearing was the "parousia" (presence) of Christ that would finalize the transition into the New Covenant age and vindicate the faithful leaders of the early church.

The Roaring Lion (v. 8)

The "roaring lion" is often treated as a generic spiritual bogeyman, but in the first-century context, it had a very specific "bite." The adversary was the Jewish and Roman power structures that were currently "seeking whom they may devour" through intense persecution. Peter encourages them to withstand, knowing that their brethren throughout the Roman world ("in the world") were facing the same "fiery trials" (Chapter 4:12).

After a "Little While" (v. 10)

Peter concludes with a promise of restoration after they have suffered "a little while." This "little while" is the same duration used throughout the New Testament (Hebrews 10:37, Revelation 6:11) to describe the brief window of time before the AD 70 judgment would bring permanent relief and "settle" the New Creation church into its established, eternal glory.
