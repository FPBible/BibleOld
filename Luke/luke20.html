Luke 20

Jurisdictional Challenge • The Wicked Tenants • The Cornerstone's Weight

1. Immediate Context

Luke 20 captures the final week of the Messiah’s earthly presence in Jerusalem, structured as a series of legal confrontations. Within the AD 30–70 horizon, the Temple had become a contested site of authority. The religious leaders—Chief Priests, Scribes, and Sadducees—challenged the Son's jurisdiction, unaware that they were facing the very Sovereign whose arrival would legally foreclose on their administration.

The chapter is a masterclass in jurisdictional defense. Through the Parable of the Wicked Tenants, the Son delivers a definitive indictment against the guardians of the Old Covenant system. He announces that the "Vineyard" (Israel's spiritual heritage) is being taken from them and given to a new, faithful administration—the Royal Priesthood. As the AD 70 horizon approached, these debates served as the formal presentation of evidence against the old order, concluding with the warning that those who stumble against the "Cornerstone" will be broken, but those upon whom it falls will be scattered as dust.

2. Scripture (WEB)

Source (verbatim): https://ebible.org/web/LUK20.htm

1 It happened on one of those days, as he was teaching the people in the temple and preaching the Good News, that the chief priests and the scribes with the elders came to him. 2 They spoke to him, saying, “Tell us: by what authority do you do these things? Or who is the one who gave you this authority?”
3 He answered them, “I also will ask you one question. Tell me: 4 the baptism of John, was it from heaven, or from men?”
5 They reasoned with themselves, saying, “If we say, ‘From heaven,’ he will say, ‘Why didn’t you believe him?’ 6 But if we say, ‘From men,’ all the people will stone us, for they are convinced that John was a prophet.” 7 They answered that they didn’t know where it was from.
8 Jesus said to them, “Neither will I tell you by what authority I do these things.”
9 He began to tell the people this parable. “A man planted a vineyard, and leased it out to farmers, and went into another country for a long time. 10 At the proper time, he sent a servant to the farmers to give him his share of the fruit of the vineyard. But the farmers beat him, and sent him away empty. 11 He sent yet another servant, and they also beat him, and treated him shamefully, and sent him away empty. 12 He sent yet a third, and they also wounded him, and threw him out. 13 The lord of the vineyard said, ‘What shall I do? I will send my beloved son. It may be that they will respect him when they see him.’
14 “But when the farmers saw him, they reasoned among themselves, saying, ‘This is the heir. Come, let’s kill him, that the inheritance may be ours.’ 15 They threw him out of the vineyard, and killed him. What then will the lord of the vineyard do to them? 16 He will come and destroy these farmers, and will give the vineyard to others.” When they heard it, they said, “May it never be!”
17 But he looked at them, and said, “Then what is this that is written, ‘The stone which the builders rejected, the same was made the head of the corner’? 18 Everyone who falls on that stone will be broken to pieces, but on whomever it will fall, it will scatter him as dust.”
19 The chief priests and the scribes sought to lay hands on him that very hour, but they feared the people—for they perceived that he had spoken this parable against them. 20 They watched him, and sent out spies, who pretended to be righteous, that they might trap him in something he said, so as to deliver him up to the power and authority of the governor.
21 They asked him, “Teacher, we know that you say and teach what is right, and aren’t partial to anyone, but truly teach the way of God. 22 Is it lawful for us to pay taxes to Caesar, or not?”
23 But he perceived their craftiness, and said to them, “Why do you tempt me? 24 Show me a denarius. Whose image and inscription does it have?” They answered, “Caesar’s.”
25 He said to them, “Then give to Caesar the things that are Caesar’s, and to God the things that are God’s.”
26 They weren’t able to trap him in his words before the people. They marveled at his answer, and were silent.
27 Some of the Sadducees came to him, those who say that there is no resurrection. 28 They asked him, “Teacher, Moses wrote to us, ‘If a man’s brother dies having a wife, and he is childless, his brother should take the wife, and raise up children for his brother.’ 29 There were therefore seven brothers. The first took a wife, and died childless. 30 The second took her as wife, and he died childless. 31 The third took her, and likewise the seven all left no children, and died. 32 Afterward the woman also died. 33 In the resurrection therefore, whose wife of them will she be? For the seven had her as a wife.”
34 Jesus said to them, “The children of this age marry, and are given in marriage. 35 But those who are considered worthy to attain to that age and the resurrection from the dead, neither marry, nor are given in marriage. 36 For they can’t die any more, for they are like the angels, and are children of God, being children of the resurrection. 37 But that the dead are raised, even Moses showed at the bush, when he called the Lord ‘The God of Abraham, the God of Isaac, and the God of Jacob.’ 38 Now he is not the God of the dead, but of the living, for all are alive to him.”
39 Some of the scribes answered, “Teacher, you speak well.” 40 They didn’t dare to ask him any more questions.
41 He said to them, “Why do they say that the Christ is David’s son? 42 David himself says in the book of Psalms, ‘The Lord said to my Lord, “Sit at my right hand, 43 until I make your enemies the footstool of your feet.”’ 44 “David therefore calls him Lord, so how is he his son?”
45 In the hearing of all the people, he said to his disciples, 46 “Beware of the scribes, who like to walk in long robes, and love greetings in the marketplaces, the best seats in the synagogues, and the best places at feasts; 47 who devour widows’ houses, and for a pretense make long prayers: these will receive greater condemnation.”

3. Fulfillment Notes

  • Give the Vineyard to Others (v. 16): A clear prophecy of the AD 70 jurisdictional transfer. The administration of the Covenant was taken from the biological leaders and given to the Royal Priesthood of the New Creation.
  • The Rejected Stone (v. 17): Christ is the Cornerstone of the New Temple. His rejection by the "builders" (the religious elite) legally activated the collapse of the old structure.
  • Children of That Age (v. 35): Refers to the New Covenant age. Those inhabiting the New Creation are "children of the resurrection," living in a state of life that the Sadducees' old-world logic could not grasp.
  • Until I Make Your Enemies a Footstool (v. 43): Quoting Psalm 110:1. This defines the AD 30–70 transition as the period where the Son's enemies (the old religious order) were being legally and historically subdued.

4. Annotations

  • Authority (v. 2) — exousia. The central question of the chapter. The old order is desperate to find a legal flaw in the Son's jurisdiction.
  • Denarius (v. 24) — The currency of the Roman occupation. The Son uses it to distinguish between the temporary obligations to the world and the permanent allegiance to God.
  • The Bush (v. 37) — Referring to the encounter at Sinai. The Son uses Moses to prove that the God of the Covenant is the God of an indestructible, living people.
  • Long Robes (v. 46) — Symbols of the "Exhausting Performance" and pride of the old priesthood. Their outward display is contrasted with their inward "devouring" of the vulnerable.

5. Fulfillment Commentary

1) The Manifestation of Sovereign Authority

The challenge to the Son's authority (Luke 20:1-8) unveils His glory as the Supreme Jurist. By countering with the question of John’s baptism, He trapped the religious elite in their own political fear. They could not answer without exposing their lack of genuine covenantal standing. The Son's refusal to "tell them" His authority was a legal judgment: they had already forfeited the right to an explanation by their rejection of the previous testimony. This event signaled that the New Creation operates by a higher jurisdiction that the old world cannot interrogate. The glory of the Son is seen in His effortless mastery over the "Pretense" of the Scribes, establishing His Word as the only valid foundation for the Temple's true purpose.

2) The Foreclosure on the Wicked Tenants

The Parable of the Wicked Tenants (Luke 20:9-16) is the formal legal indictment of the Old Covenant administration. The "Farmers" (the religious leaders) had treated the Father's servants (the prophets) shamefully and were now moving to "kill the Heir" to seize the inheritance for themselves. This "Thievery" of the divine vineyard led to the definitive verdict: the destruction of the tenants and the transfer of the vineyard to "others." This was the AD 70 horizon in plain view. The Son was notifying them that their lease on the Kingdom was being terminated. The glory of the New World is found in the "others"—the Royal Priesthood who produce the fruit of the Spirit, replacing the barren performance of the old elite.

3) The Resurrection Logic of "That Age"

The debate with the Sadducees (Luke 20:27-38) unveils the superiority of the New Creation life. The Sadducees tried to use the Law of Moses to make the Resurrection appear absurd, but the Son corrected their jurisdictional error. He distinguished between "this age" (the dying Mosaic world) and "that age" (the New Covenant reality). In the New Creation, we are "children of the resurrection," possessing a life that is no longer subject to the biological constraints or rituals of the old week. By quoting Moses at the bush, the Son proved that Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob are *already* alive to God. This commentary affirms that the transition finalized the victory over death, bringing us into a world where we are "like angels"—living in direct, unhindered union with the God of the living.

4) The Lordship of the Christ over David

In Luke 20:41-44, the Son asserts His absolute Sovereignty by clarifying His identity. If the Christ is merely "David's Son" (a biological descendant), why does David call Him "Lord"? This question silenced the Scribes because it forced them to acknowledge that the Messiah’s authority is not derived from earthly lineage but from His divine status at the "Right Hand." The AD 30–70 transition was the process of making His enemies a "footstool." The glory of the Son is His eternal jurisdiction that precedes and succeeds the Davidic throne. The chapter concludes with a blistering warning against those who "devour widows' houses." This final article of indictment seals the "greater condemnation" of the old system, which used the Law as a mask for extortion. The New Creation is the permanent rest for the vulnerable, protected by the King who has already silenced the accusers.

8. New Creation Reality

In Luke 20, the glory of the Son is revealed as the power that evicts the "Thieves" of religious authority and establishes your permanent standing. As a citizen of the New Creation, you are the "Child of the Resurrection" inhabiting "That Age." You do not live under the "Exhausting Performance" of the Scribes or the cold logic of the Sadducees. Your standing is not based on "Long Robes" or social status, but on your union with the One at the "Right Hand." The "Thief" who tried to steal the inheritance has been broken against the Cornerstone. You live in a world where the "Vineyard" has been given to you as a faithful steward of the Father's joy. The transition is finished; you possess a life that "can't die any more," resting in the favor of the God of the living.

9. Reflection

Question: If the Son declared that the old religious "builders" rejected the true Cornerstone (Luke 20:17) and subsequently took the vineyard from them, why do we still look to titled "builders" in modern institutions to define our standing or our access to God?
