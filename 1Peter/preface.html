Preface — 1 Peter

Hope in the Fiery Trial

1 Peter is a letter written to people under pressure. Not philosophical pressure — the real pressure of social exclusion, economic marginalisation, and the approaching collapse of the world they had grown up inside.

Peter does not write to offer a general theology of suffering. He writes to a specific generation, in a specific decade, facing a specific crisis — and he writes with urgency because the crisis is close.

The Audience

Peter addresses his letter to the elect exiles of the Dispersion across five Roman provinces of Asia Minor. His readers are Jewish and Gentile believers in the late AD 60s — a generation living in the overlap between the Old Covenant age, which is ending, and the New Covenant order, which has arrived but not yet been fully manifested.

They are exiles in two senses:

  • many are literally displaced — scattered from Judea under persecution
  • all are living as strangers in a world whose structures still reflect the old age they have left behind

The exile language is covenantal, not metaphysical. Peter is not saying the physical world is foreign to the believer. He is locating his readers in a specific moment of covenant transition.

The Fiery Trial

Peter warns his readers to expect a fiery trial (4:12) and describes it as something already beginning, not merely anticipated. The Greek word translated "trial" is pyrosis — a burning, a smelting. The image is of metal refined in fire, not destroyed by it.

The trial Peter has in mind is the covenantal upheaval of the late 60s:

  • intensifying Roman hostility toward Jewish Christians
  • the Jewish War beginning in AD 66
  • the growing pressure to choose between the old community and the new
  • the imminent destruction of Jerusalem and the Temple

This is not a timeless description of Christian suffering in every era. It is the specific furnace through which the first-century church passed as the Old Covenant world ended.

The Imminence Statements

1 Peter contains some of the clearest time-statements in the New Testament, and they govern how the whole letter must be read.

  • "salvation ready to be revealed in the last time" (1:5)
  • "the end of all things is at hand" (4:7)
  • "the time has come for judgment to begin at the household of God" (4:17)
  • "after you have suffered a little while" (5:10)

A little while. At hand. The time has come. Peter is not speaking into an indefinite future. He is speaking to his own generation about what is immediately approaching.

Stretching these statements across two thousand years does violence to the text. The fulfilled reading honours them by taking them at face value.

Living Hope

The theological centre of 1 Peter is the resurrection. Not the resurrection as a future event to be anticipated, but the resurrection of Jesus Christ as the already-accomplished ground of a living hope.

Blessed be the God and Father of our Lord Jesus Christ, who according to his great mercy caused us to be born again to a living hope through the resurrection of Jesus Christ from the dead. (1:3)

The hope is living because the one who grounds it is living. It is not wishful thinking. It is not a projection into a distant future. It is the present reality of a people born into a new order through the resurrection that has already happened.

Holiness in the Transition

Much of 1 Peter is concerned with how believers conduct themselves during the transition — specifically, how they behave toward outsiders, toward governing authorities, and toward one another, while the old order collapses around them.

Peter's instructions are shaped by two concerns:

  • that believers not give their accusers legitimate grounds for hostility
  • that the community's conduct commend the gospel even under pressure

The ethic is not one of withdrawal or survival. It is the ethic of a people who know the outcome and conduct themselves accordingly — as those who belong to the incoming order, not the outgoing one.

The Suffering of Christ as the Pattern

Peter returns repeatedly to the suffering of Christ as the framework for understanding what his readers are experiencing. Christ suffered unjustly. He did not retaliate. He entrusted himself to the one who judges justly. He went through death and was vindicated.

This is not offered as a vague inspiration. It is the specific covenantal logic that makes sense of their situation. The pattern of the Head is the pattern of the body. The path through the fiery trial is the same path he walked.

The suffering is real. The vindication is certain. The gap between them is small — a little while.

Judgment Beginning at the House of God

1 Peter 4:17 is one of the most important texts for understanding the letter's eschatological context: "For it is time for judgment to begin at the household of God."

The judgment Peter describes is the covenant judgment that Ezekiel and Jeremiah and Jesus himself had announced — beginning with the people who bore the covenant name, before spreading to those outside. This is the Old Testament pattern of divine discipline, and Peter applies it precisely to the AD 70 crisis.

The destruction of Jerusalem is not God's abandonment of his people. It is the completion of the covenant audit that the prophets had long announced.

How to Read 1 Peter

  • Read it as a first-century letter, addressed to a first-century crisis
  • Take the time statements at face value — the end was at hand for Peter's readers
  • Read the suffering passages in their covenantal context, not as timeless theology
  • Locate the "living hope" in the already-accomplished resurrection, not a future event
  • Read the holiness instructions as transition ethics — how to live faithfully while two ages overlap

Why 1 Peter Matters Now

We do not read 1 Peter as people facing the same trial Peter's readers faced. The trial ended. The Old Covenant age collapsed. Jerusalem fell. The overlap generation passed through the furnace and out the other side.

We read 1 Peter as the record of how the first church navigated the transition that established the world we now inhabit. Their courage and faithfulness under pressure, their refusal to abandon the living hope when the old world was burning around them — this is the testimony that runs before us.

What Peter's readers were moving toward, we have received. The hope they held as imminent, we hold as arrived.
