Matthew 27 — The King Condemned and the Covenant Blood Applied

Immediate Context

Matthew 27 records the public transfer of Israel’s leadership verdict onto Jesus, the exposure of corrupt stewardship, and the decisive “handing over” of the King to death—setting the covenant lawsuit on its rails toward the first-century resolution where the old Temple order would be judged and retired.

Scripture (WEB)

1Now when morning had come, all the chief priests and the elders of the people took counsel against Jesus to put him to death. 2They bound him, led him away, and delivered him up to Pontius Pilate, the governor.

3Then Judas, who betrayed him, when he saw that Jesus was condemned, felt remorse, and brought back the thirty pieces of silver to the chief priests and elders, 4saying, “I have sinned in that I betrayed innocent blood.” But they said, “What is that to us? You see to it.” 5He threw down the pieces of silver in the sanctuary and departed. Then he went away and hanged himself. 6The chief priests took the pieces of silver and said, “It’s not lawful to put them into the treasury, since it is the price of blood.” 7They took counsel, and bought the potter’s field with them to bury strangers in. 8Therefore that field has been called “The Field of Blood” to this day. 9Then that which was spoken through Jeremiah‡ some manuscripts omit “Jeremiah” the prophet was fulfilled, saying, 10“They took the thirty pieces of silver, the price of him upon whom a price had been set, whom some of the children of Israel priced, and they gave them for the potter’s field, as the Lord commanded me.”* Zechariah 11:12-13; Jeremiah 19:1-13; 32:6-9

11Now Jesus stood before the governor; and the governor asked him, saying, “Are you the King of the Jews?” 12Jesus said to him, “So you say.” 13When he was accused by the chief priests and elders, he answered nothing. 14Then Pilate said to him, “Don’t you hear how many things they testify against you?” 15He gave him no answer, not even one word, so that the governor marveled greatly.

16Now at the feast the governor was accustomed to release to the multitude one prisoner whom they desired. 17They had then a notable prisoner called Barabbas. 18When therefore they were gathered together, Pilate said to them, “Whom do you want me to release to you? Barabbas, or Jesus who is called Christ?” 19For he knew that because of envy they had delivered him up. 20While he was sitting on the judgment seat, his wife sent to him, saying, “Have nothing to do with that righteous man, for I have suffered many things today in a dream because of him.” 21Now the chief priests and the elders persuaded the multitudes to ask for Barabbas and destroy Jesus. 22But the governor answered them, “Which of the two do you want me to release to you?” They said, “Barabbas!” 23Pilate said to them, “What then shall I do to Jesus who is called Christ?” They all said to him, “Let him be crucified!” 24But the governor said, “Why? What evil has he done?” But they cried out exceedingly, saying, “Let him be crucified!” 25So when Pilate saw that nothing was being gained, but rather that a disturbance was starting, he took water and washed his hands before the multitude, saying, “I am innocent of the blood of this righteous person. You see to it.” 26All the people answered, “May his blood be on us and on our children!” 27Then he released Barabbas to them, but Jesus he flogged and delivered to be crucified.

28Then the governor’s soldiers took Jesus into the Praetorium, and gathered the whole garrison together against him. 29They stripped him and put a scarlet robe on him. 30They braided a crown of thorns and put it on his head, and a reed in his right hand; and they kneeled down before him and mocked him, saying, “Hail, King of the Jews!” 31They spat on him, and took the reed and struck him on the head. 32When they had mocked him, they took the robe off him, and put his clothes on him, and led him away to crucify him. 33As they came out, they found a man of Cyrene, Simon by name, and they compelled him to go with them, that he might carry his cross. 34When they came to a place called “Golgotha”, that is to say, “The place of a skull,” 35they gave him sour wine‡ or, vinegar to drink mixed with gall.§ Gall is a bitter-tasting, dark green oil from a wormwood plant that is alcoholic in its effect. When he had tasted it, he would not drink.

36When they had crucified him, they divided his clothing among them, casting lots,‡ TR adds “that it might be fulfilled which was spoken by the prophet: ‘They divided my garments among them, and for my clothing they cast lots;’ ” [see Psalms 22:18 and John 19:24] 37and they sat and watched him there. 38They set up over his head the accusation against him written, “THIS IS JESUS, THE KING OF THE JEWS.” 39Then there were two robbers crucified with him, one on his right hand and one on the left. 40Those who passed by blasphemed him, wagging their heads 41and saying, “You who destroy the temple and build it in three days, save yourself! If you are the Son of God, come down from the cross!” 42Likewise the chief priests also mocking with the scribes, the Pharisees,‡ TR omits “the Pharisees” and the elders, said, 43“He saved others, but he can’t save himself. If he is the King of Israel, let him come down from the cross now, and we will believe in him. 44He trusts in God. Let God deliver him now, if he wants him; for he said, ‘I am the Son of God.’ ” 45The robbers also who were crucified with him cast on him the same reproach.

46Now from the sixth hour§ noon there was darkness over all the land until the ninth hour.‡ 3:00 p.m. 47About the ninth hour Jesus cried with a loud voice, saying, “Eli, Eli, lima‡ TR reads “lama” instead of “lima” sabachthani?” That is, “My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?”* Psalms 22:1 48Some of them who stood there, when they heard it, said, “This man is calling Elijah.” 49Immediately one of them ran and took a sponge, filled it with vinegar, put it on a reed, and gave him a drink. 50The rest said, “Let him be. Let’s see whether Elijah comes to save him.” 51Jesus cried again with a loud voice, and yielded up his spirit.

52Behold, the veil of the temple was torn in two from the top to the bottom. The earth quaked and the rocks were split. 53The tombs were opened, and many bodies of the saints who had fallen asleep were raised; 54and coming out of the tombs after his resurrection, they entered into the holy city and appeared to many. 55Now the centurion and those who were with him watching Jesus, when they saw the earthquake and the things that were done, were terrified, saying, “Truly this was the Son of God!” 56Many women were there watching from afar, who had followed Jesus from Galilee, serving him. 57Among them were Mary Magdalene, Mary the mother of James and Joses, and the mother of the sons of Zebedee.

58When evening had come, a rich man from Arimathaea named Joseph, who himself was also Jesus’ disciple, came. 59This man went to Pilate and asked for Jesus’ body. Then Pilate commanded the body to be given up. 60Joseph took the body and wrapped it in a clean linen cloth 61and laid it in his own new tomb, which he had cut out in the rock. Then he rolled a large stone against the door of the tomb, and departed. 62Mary Magdalene was there, and the other Mary, sitting opposite the tomb.

63Now on the next day, which was the day after the Preparation Day, the chief priests and the Pharisees were gathered together to Pilate, 64saying, “Sir, we remember what that deceiver said while he was still alive: ‘After three days I will rise again.’ 65Command therefore that the tomb be made secure until the third day, lest perhaps his disciples come at night and steal him away, and tell the people, ‘He is risen from the dead;’ and the last deception will be worse than the first.” 66Pilate said to them, “You have a guard. Go, make it as secure as you can.” So they went with the guard and made the tomb secure, sealing the stone.

Fulfillment Commentary (DEPTH REQUIRED)

1) The Trial Is Covenant Litigation, Not Random Tragedy

Matthew 27 is not merely “the sad part before Easter.” It is covenant courtroom. The chief priests and elders “took counsel” (v. 1) because the dispute is not only personal; it is governmental. In Matthew’s storyline, Israel’s leaders have been weighed and found false stewards (Matthew 21–23). The Temple establishment claims authority to define righteousness, purity, access, and “the people of God.” Jesus has publicly contradicted that authority, exposed hypocrisy, and announced the Temple’s coming desolation. So the leadership moves to finalize the verdict: eliminate the claimant King and preserve the system. That is why this chapter matters for the Full Preterist lens: it sets the moral and legal ground for the first-century judgment on Jerusalem and the passing of the Mosaic age. AD 70 does not fall out of the sky as a random catastrophe; it arrives as the historical outworking of a covenant lawsuit reaching its climax.

2) Judas, the Silver, and the Public Exposure of Bloodguilt

Judas’ remorse (vv. 3–5) is not repentance; it’s the collapse of a conscience when the machinery of death becomes real. The priests’ reply—“What is that to us?”—is the sound of a system that can parse rules while being morally dead. They refuse the “price of blood” in the treasury (v. 6) while arranging bloodshed at the highest level. The irony is intentional: the leaders who claim to guard holiness become the clearest exhibit that the old administration cannot heal the human heart. The purchased “Field of Blood” (vv. 7–8) becomes a standing public witness that Jerusalem’s leadership has crossed a line.

In covenant terms, this is the unveiling of the accuser-system. The Temple order (priesthood + courts + purity codes) promised “clean” status while producing a culture that can condemn the Innocent and sleep at night. That’s why Matthew flags fulfillment language here (vv. 9–10): not to play proof-text games, but to show that Israel’s story is reaching its appointed end in the Messiah. The leadership is acting out the very pattern the prophets warned about: corrupt shepherds, mispriced righteousness, and the land bearing witness against them.

3) Barabbas: The Choice of the Old World Over the True King

The Barabbas exchange (vv. 16–26) is more than crowd psychology. It is covenant symbolism. The people, led by priests and elders, choose a known rebel over the Prince of Peace. In Matthew’s larger frame, this is Israel clinging to the old identity—power preserved by coercion, nationalism, and violence—rather than receiving the Kingdom that arrives as mercy, truth, and open access. Pilate’s handwashing does not cleanse anything; it only highlights the public nature of the transfer of responsibility. The infamous cry, “May his blood be on us and on our children!” (v. 25), is corporate covenant speech. It is not a license for antisemitism, not a perpetual curse on later ethnic descendants, and not a blanket permission slip for church history’s atrocities. It is first-century covenant language spoken by that generation in that crisis.

And here is the sharp, time-bound point: Matthew has already trained you how to read this. Jesus has repeatedly warned “this generation” of impending judgment (e.g., Matthew 23:36). This line (v. 25) belongs inside that same horizon. In AD 70, Jerusalem’s leadership and many within the city’s social body experienced the historical consequence of rejecting the Messiah and clinging to the doomed Temple order. The covenant “blood” language lands historically in that same world, not as a mystical hex floating through centuries.

4) Mockery, Cross, and the Collapse of Temple Claims

The soldiers’ mockery (vv. 27–31) is empire parodying kingship, but Matthew wants you to see the deeper reversal: the true King is enthroned by humiliation. The accusation sign—“THIS IS JESUS, THE KING OF THE JEWS” (v. 37)—is meant as irony, yet becomes proclamation. The passers-by and leaders taunt Him with Temple language: “You who destroy the temple and build it in three days” (v. 40). That accusation is the hinge between cross and AD 70. Jesus has already redefined the Temple around Himself—His body, His priesthood, His access. So their taunt becomes prophecy: the old Temple will indeed be rendered obsolete, and a new covenant “house” will stand—one not built with hands.

5) The Torn Veil Is an Enacted Verdict on the Old Access System

When Jesus yields up His spirit, “the veil of the temple was torn in two from the top to the bottom” (v. 51). That is not decorative symbolism; it is a legal sign-act. The veil represented restricted access, mediated presence, and the central claim of the Temple order: God is “behind the curtain,” and you approach by priestly management and sacrifice. The tearing “from the top” signals God’s own action—He is ending that arrangement. In a fulfilled-covenant frame, this is the announcement that the old administration has lost its authority to define access. The cross is the decisive covenant act, and AD 70 is the historical eviction of the building that embodied the old administration.

That’s why Full Preterism refuses futurist anxiety here. The decisive “opening” has happened. The judgment that removes the rival structure happened in history. What remains now is not a pending access upgrade; it is settled New Creation reality: presence without priests, acceptance without Temple economy, life without fear-based countdowns.

6) Watch the Witnesses: A Gentile Confession and a Secured Tomb

The centurion’s confession—“Truly this was the Son of God!” (v. 54)—matters. In Matthew, Gentile faith repeatedly anticipates the nations recognizing Israel’s King while Jerusalem’s leadership hardens. This is covenant transition in miniature: the Kingdom is no longer an ethnic badge administered by a Temple bureaucracy; it is the reign of Christ extending to the nations.

Finally, the sealed tomb (vv. 62–66) shows the old order doing what it always does: attempting to control outcomes, suppress testimony, and manage reality by force. Matthew is setting up the next chapter’s reversal: the system that can seal stones cannot seal God’s verdict. The resurrection will not be a “private spiritual metaphor”; it will be the Father’s public vindication of the Son—after which the first-century story moves inexorably toward the removal of the old Temple world and the public establishment of New Covenant life without that structure.

New Creation Reality

You are not trying to get behind a curtain that has already been torn; you live from open access in Christ. The old system that measured you by accusation and distance has been judged and retired, so your life now is presence, belonging, and Spirit-led faithfulness—without fear narratives and without “delay” thinking.

Reflection

Where are you still living as if access must be earned or managed, instead of living from the torn-veil reality of settled nearness?
