5) "I Will Come Again" Does Not Mean Second Coming from Heaven

Jesus says, "I will come again and will take you to myself" (v. 3). Futurist interpretation assumes this means a bodily return from the sky thousands of years later. But covenant reading recognizes this as Jesus' "coming" in judgment and vindication at the destruction of Jerusalem in AD 70.

Throughout Scripture, God "comes" in historical acts of deliverance or judgment. He "came down" to Egypt (Exod 3:8), "came" to Sinai (Exod 19:20), and "came" in judgment against nations (Isa 19:1). Jesus uses this same language: He would "come on the clouds" (Matt 24:30, 26:64) ‚Äî not literally floating down from the sky, but arriving in divine judgment using the prophetic imagery of cloud-theophany.

Notice the time frame. In Matthew 24:34, Jesus says, "This generation will not pass away until all these things take place." In Matthew 16:28, He promises some disciples "will not taste death until they see the Son of Man coming in his kingdom." And in John 21:22, Jesus speaks of returning within the potential lifetime of John himself.

What Jesus promised in John 14:3 was fulfilled in AD 70 when the Father vindicated the Son by destroying the city and temple that rejected Him. The disciples who lived to see it witnessed Jesus' coming in power ‚Äî not a physical descent, but a divine visitation that ended the old age and confirmed the New Creation order.

Jesus "came again" through resurrection, through the Spirit (v. 18: "I will come to you"), and through the covenant judgment of AD 70. He did not leave His disciples waiting indefinitely. The transition He spoke of was completed in the first century.
