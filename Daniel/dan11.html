Daniel 11

The Kings of the North and South • Antiochus Epiphanes • The Rise of Rome

1. Immediate Context

Continuing seamlessly from the angelic encounter in Chapter 10, the heavenly messenger provides Daniel with a literal, detailed roadmap of the centuries to come. The prophecy outlines the fall of Persia, the rapid rise and fracturing of Alexander the Great's Greek Empire, and the centuries of brutal warfare between the resulting Ptolemaic dynasty in Egypt (King of the South) and the Seleucid dynasty in Syria (King of the North).

Judea, sitting geographically right between them, becomes the blood-soaked battleground. The vision details the rise of the vile Antiochus Epiphanes, who desecrates the temple, and then (starting around verse 36) shifts to the rising power of the new iron beast—the Roman Empire—which will dominate the earth leading up to the arrival of Christ.

3. Fulfillment Notes

  • Staggering Historical Accuracy: Verses 1-35 are so historically accurate regarding the wars between the Ptolemies and Seleucids that critical scholars claim Daniel must have been written after the events happened. For the believer, it is the ultimate proof of God's sovereign foreknowledge and control over human politics.
  • The First Abomination (v. 31): This explicitly details the actions of Antiochus IV Epiphanes in 167 BC. He set up an altar to Zeus in the Jerusalem temple and offered a pig. Jesus used this exact historical event as the prototype to warn His disciples about the coming Roman desecration of the temple in AD 70 (Matthew 24:15).
  • No Futuristic Gap (v. 36): Futurists insert a 2,000-year gap between verse 35 and 36, claiming "the king who does as he pleases" is a future European Antichrist. However, reading the text chronologically, this king represents the shift from Greek dominance to Roman dominance (the Caesars/Herod), perfectly setting up the Roman-era tribulation of Daniel 12.

4. Annotations

  • a mighty king (v. 3) — Alexander the Great, whose empire was divided into four parts ("toward the four winds") upon his sudden death.
  • king of the South — The Ptolemaic dynasty ruling from Egypt.
  • king of the North — The Seleucid dynasty ruling from Syria.
  • the glorious land — Israel, geographically positioned as the land bridge and constant battlefield between Egypt and Syria.
  • the people who know their God (v. 32) — Historically fulfilled by the Maccabees, the Jewish priestly family who led a fierce guerrilla revolt against Antiochus Epiphanes to cleanse and rededicate the temple (the origin of Hanukkah).

5. Fulfillment Commentary

1) The Geopolitical Chessboard (vv. 2–20)

God does not deal in vague spiritual generalities. The angel gives Daniel a play-by-play of the next several centuries. He predicts the Persian invasion of Greece, the retaliatory conquest by Alexander the Great, and the resulting centuries of political assassinations, forced marriages, and broken treaties between the Ptolemies and the Seleucids.

For the Jewish people living through this, these prophecies were a lifeline. When their homeland was being trampled by opposing armies, they could read Daniel 11 and know with absolute certainty: God wrote this script centuries ago. He is not surprised, and He is completely in control.

2) The Vile Person: Antiochus Epiphanes (vv. 21–35)

The focus zeroes in on one specific Seleucid king: Antiochus IV. He was a madman who demanded to be worshipped as a god. Humiliated by the rising power of the Roman Republic (the "ships of Kittim" in v. 30), he took his rage out on the holy covenant in Jerusalem.

He slaughtered tens of thousands of Jews, outlawed the reading of the Torah, and set up the "abomination that makes desolate." But God promised that a faithful remnant would "be strong, and take action" (v. 32). This was famously fulfilled by the Maccabean revolt, proving that even in the darkest hours of persecution, God preserves a faithful remnant who refuse to bow to the Beast system.

3) The Transition to the Fourth Empire (vv. 36–45)

Here is where futurism breaks the timeline. Because the details of verses 36-45 do not perfectly align with the end of Antiochus's life, futurists claim there is a 2,000-year gap, pausing the clock to describe a 21st-century Antichrist.

But there is no gap. The prophecy is simply moving down the timeline of the empires established in Daniel 2 and Daniel 7. As the Greek empire fades, the Iron Beast of Rome rises. The "king who does according to his will" accurately describes the Roman imperial power (specifically the dynasty of the Caesars, and their proxy King Herod in Judea) who magnified themselves as gods and demanded worship.

By understanding this as the transition to Roman authority, the text flows seamlessly into Chapter 12, which describes the final tribulation and the destruction of the Jewish nation in the first century—exactly when Rome was in power.

8. New Creation Reality

Daniel 11 is a chaotic record of political betrayals, assassinations, and wars. But behind every earthly king was the sovereign pen of God. You are reading covenant history for assurance, not headlines for doom. The political turbulence of our modern world is not spiraling out of control; it is fully known by the Most High. You belong to an unshakable Kingdom that outlasts every "King of the North" and "King of the South" that history will ever produce.
