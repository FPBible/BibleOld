Matthew 1

The Messiah Placed Within Israel’s Covenant Story

Matthew opens by anchoring Jesus firmly inside Israel’s covenant history. This chapter is not background filler — it establishes legitimacy, transition, and arrival.

Original Text

Matthew 1 — World English Bible (WEB)

FP Paraphrase

1This is the record of Jesus the Messiah, the son of David, the son of Abraham.

2–6From Abraham the line moved forward through Isaac, Jacob, Judah, and the fathers, until it reached David the king.

6–11From David the royal line continued through Israel’s kings, even through failure and collapse, until the Babylonian exile.

12–16After the exile the line continued, carrying hope forward, until it came to Joseph, the husband of Mary, from whom Jesus—called Messiah—was born.

17So all the generations from Abraham to David were fourteen, from David to the exile fourteen, and from the exile to the Messiah fourteen.

18The birth of Jesus the Messiah took place this way: Mary was pledged to Joseph, and before they came together she was found to be with child by the Holy Spirit.

19–20Joseph, being righteous and unwilling to shame her, planned to release her quietly. But an angel appeared to him in a dream, saying, “Joseph, son of David, do not fear. What is conceived in her is from the Holy Spirit.”

21“She will bear a son. You shall name Him Jesus, because He will save His people from their sins.”

22–23All this happened to bring to reality what the Lord had spoken: a virgin will conceive and bear a son, and they will call His name Immanuel — God with us.

24–25Joseph obeyed. He took Mary as his wife, and when the child was born, he named Him Jesus.

Commentary

1. Why Matthew begins with a genealogy

Matthew does not open with sentiment or theology, but with covenant legitimacy. Jesus is deliberately located within Israel’s story, not outside of it.

By naming Abraham and David immediately, Matthew signals two covenant anchors: promise and kingship. Jesus is not a religious innovator — He is the lawful heir of Israel’s covenant world.

2. The exile as covenant collapse

The Babylonian exile is not a historical footnote. It represents the breaking of Israel’s world — loss of land, throne, temple, and identity.

Matthew frames history as moving toward resolution: promise → kingdom → collapse → Messiah. The genealogy is already telling you that Jesus arrives at the moment of covenant repair.

3. “He will save His people from their sins”

This statement is covenantal, not abstract. “Sin” here is not generic moral failure; it is Israel’s condition under condemnation, exile logic, and law-bound separation.

Matthew presents Jesus as the one who ends that condition — restoring access, forgiveness, and covenant standing.

4. Fulfillment as arrival, not postponement

Matthew’s use of “fulfilled” does not point forward to distant outcomes. It marks realisation — God’s word coming to embodied reality in history.

“God with us” is not a future promise. It is the defining claim of the Gospel from its opening chapter.

Living This Now

Matthew begins by grounding faith in what God has already done, not what believers must still wait for.

New Creation life starts from identity, not anticipation. Jesus is not gradually becoming Lord — He arrives as Lord.

Living faithfully now means resting in legitimacy already secured, presence already given, and salvation already accomplished.
