Is the "Second Coming" a Biblical Phrase?

Examining the psychological weight of an unbiblical label, and recovering the true terminology of Christ's covenantal arrival.

Terminology Parousia AD 70 Fulfillment

1. The Psychological Weight of an Invented Phrase

The phrase "Second Coming" does not appear anywhere in Scripture. Not in the Gospels. Not in the Epistles. Not in Revelation. It is a later theological label imposed onto the text.

Why does this matter? Because the phrase "Second Coming" carries enormous psychological and doctrinal baggage. For many, it automatically triggers Hollywood images of planetary destruction, global graveyards splitting open, history collapsing, and time itself ending.

None of those assumptions come from the phrase itself—because the phrase doesn't exist. They come from later doctrinal systems layered over biblical language. The Bible speaks repeatedly of Christ’s coming, appearing, revelation, and day—but never once calls it the "Second Coming."

2. The Only "Second" Text: Hebrews 9

There is only one place in the entire New Testament where the word "second" is attached to Christ's appearance:

"So Christ also, having been once offered to bear the sins of many, will appear a second time, apart from sin, to those who are eagerly waiting for him for salvation." — Hebrews 9:28

This verse is not describing a global end-of-time scenario. As established in the Day of Atonement study, the author of Hebrews is using strict High Priestly imagery.

The High Priest disappeared behind the veil to offer blood (the "first" appearing). The congregation waited anxiously outside. When the Priest stepped back out from the veil (the "second" appearing), it meant the sacrifice was accepted and their covenant salvation was secured. This was written to living, first-century believers who were actively waiting for that covenant closure. The promise was imminent to them, not thousands of years removed.

3. The Biblical Language of "Coming"

When we remove the unbiblical phrase "Second Coming" and return to the actual Greek words used by the Apostles, the discussion immediately becomes more precise and historically grounded.

Parousia (Presence)

Often translated "coming," this word literally means presence or arrival. It does not inherently describe physical descent from outer space. In the ancient world, it was the standard term used for a royal visitation or the authoritative arrival of a king to deal with his subjects.

Erchomai (To Come)

This common verb is frequently used in judgment contexts. The Old Testament repeatedly describes God "coming" (erchomai in the LXX) in judgment against nations like Egypt or Babylon without a literal bodily manifestation.

Epiphaneia (Appearing)

This word refers to a manifestation or visible unveiling—the public display of authority, glory, or judgment.

Apokalypsis (Revelation)

This means unveiling. It is the disclosure of what was previously hidden or operating behind the scenes. It does not mean spatial relocation.

None of these terms demand a future cosmic extinction event. They must be interpreted within their covenantal context.

4. Prophetic Idiom: Coming in the Clouds

"Coming in the clouds" is established Old Testament prophetic imagery. When the Lord judged Egypt (Isaiah 19:1), He was described as riding on a swift cloud. These descriptions were never meant to be literal atmospheric disturbances; they were covenantal judgment language.

Likewise, the language of the sun, moon, and stars darkening referred to the fall of ruling authorities and national powers, not the collapse of the solar system. When Jesus told the High Priest he would see the Son of Man "coming on the clouds of heaven" (Matt 26:64), He was invoking Daniel 7. He was declaring that the long-promised covenant lawsuit was about to reach its climax in their generation.

5. The Time Indicators

The New Testament repeatedly anchors the coming (parousia) of Christ in the time frame of the first-century audience. Jesus declared plainly:

"Most certainly I tell you, this generation will not pass away until all these things are accomplished." — Matthew 24:34

The coming of the Son of Man was tied directly to the destruction of the Temple and the end of the Old Covenant age (aion). The disciples were not asking about the end of world history; they were asking about the end of the covenant order centered in Jerusalem.

  • "Some standing here will not taste death..." (Matt 16:28)
  • "The Judge is standing at the door." (James 5:9)
  • "The end of all things is near." (1 Peter 4:7)
  • "In a very little while, he who comes will come, and will not wait." (Hebrews 10:37)

These statements cannot be stretched across millennia without emptying them of meaning. The Apostles were not mistaken. They were announcing an imminent, observable covenant transition.

6. What We Affirm and Reject

The story of Scripture does not end in destruction; it culminates in clarity. The destruction of Jerusalem in AD 70 was the visible, covenantal confirmation that the Old Covenant had passed away and the New Creation was fully established.

  • We affirm that Christ came in judgment exactly when and how He promised—within the lifetime of His hearers.
  • We affirm that AD 70 was the final vindication of His authority, ending the Old Covenant age and fully establishing the New Creation reality.
  • We reject the imposed idea of a yet-future "Second Coming" event that delays fulfillment and disconnects prophecy from its original first-century audience.

Christ reigns. The covenant has transitioned. The New Creation is present reality.
