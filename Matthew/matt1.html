Matthew 1

Messiah Legitimated · Promise Traced · Birth Announced

FPBible rule: if a passage sounds “still pending” without explanation, it fails and is rewritten. Matthew opens by proving Jesus’ covenant legitimacy, then moves straight into the arrival of “God with us.”

Original Text WEB

Verify against the World English Bible (public domain): Matthew 1 (WEB)

Note: Matthew uses “fulfillment” language as covenant reality arriving in history, not as vague prediction talk.

FP Paraphrase

1:1–17

The line of the King

1This is the record of Jesus the Messiah—rooted in David’s kingship and Abraham’s promise.

2–6From Abraham through the fathers, the line moves forward until it reaches David the king.

6–11From David the line continues through the royal house, even through national failure, until the exile—when Israel’s world cracked and its crown dimmed.

12–16After the exile the line continues, carrying hope forward, until it reaches Joseph—Mary’s husband—through whom Jesus is publicly placed within David’s house.

17The story is deliberately framed in structured generations: promise → kingdom → exile → Messiah. Matthew is telling you: history has been moving toward this arrival.

1:18–25

The birth of Jesus

18This is how the Messiah’s birth unfolded: Mary was pledged to Joseph, and before they lived as husband and wife she was found to be with child—by the Holy Spirit.

19Joseph was just and compassionate. He refused to shame her, and planned a quiet separation.

20But as he weighed it up, the Lord’s messenger came to him in a dream: “Joseph, son of David, don’t fear. Take Mary as your wife. This child’s life begins by God’s Spirit.”

21“She will bear a son. Name Him Jesus—because He will save His people from their sins.”

22–23This happened in line with what God had spoken: a virgin will conceive and bear a son, and His name will signal the reality—God with us.

24–25Joseph obeyed. He took Mary as his wife, and when the child was born he named Him Jesus—publicly receiving the Messiah into David’s house.

Notes

Covenant Horizon

Why Matthew starts with a genealogy

  • Legitimacy: Matthew opens by anchoring Jesus in Israel’s covenant story—Abraham (promise) and David (kingship).
  • Exile logic: The exile is not trivia. It’s the collapse of Israel’s old world—pointing to the need for restoration that the Messiah brings.
  • Fulfillment tone: “Fulfilled” in Matthew is not “someday.” It is covenant reality arriving in history and taking form in Christ.
Identity

“He will save His people from their sins”

  • Matthew’s opening is already covenant-shaped: salvation is not vague self-improvement—it's God ending sin’s rule and restoring access.
  • From the start, the Messiah is presented as the One who deals with the root problem, not merely the symptoms.
No Futurism

“God with us” is not postponed

  • Matthew does not introduce a God who will eventually arrive. He introduces the arrival of God’s presence in the Messiah.
  • This sets the tone for the whole Gospel: kingdom, judgment, vindication, and New Creation life belong to the covenant transition story Matthew will unfold.
