Luke 16

The Unjust Manager • The Law Fulfilled • The Rich Man's Eviction

1. Immediate Context

Luke 16 presents a decisive legal contrast between the "Old World" of religious status and the "New Creation" of sovereign grace. During the AD 30–70 transition, the Son unveiled the coming foreclosure of the Mosaic economy. He uses the parable of the Shrewd Manager to instruct the remnant on how to handle the "wealth" of the old system—by using it to secure a place in the eternal dwellings of the New World before the old house is completely dismantled.

The chapter reaches its exultant climax in the narrative of the Rich Man and Lazarus. This is not a general lesson on the afterlife, but a specific covenantal verdict against the religious elite who wore "purple and fine linen" (priestly garments) while neglecting the "Lazarus" (the remnant/nations) at their gate. The "Great Gulf" fixed between them signifies the permanent jurisdictional separation established during the first-century transition. As the AD 70 horizon approached, the Son was declaring that the old world's "comfort" was ending, and the "torment" of its legal desolation was becoming an irreversible reality.

2. Scripture (WEB)

Source (verbatim): https://ebible.org/web/LUK16.htm

1 He also said to his disciples, “There was a certain rich man who had a manager. An accusation was made to him that this man was wasting his possessions. 2 He called him and said to him, ‘What is this that I hear about you? Give an accounting of your management, for you can no longer be manager.’
3 “The manager said within himself, ‘What will I do, seeing that my lord is taking away the management from me? I don’t have strength to dig. I am ashamed to beg. 4 I know what I will do, so that when I am removed from management, they may receive me into their houses.’ 5 Calling each one of his lord’s debtors to him, he said to the first, ‘How much do you owe my lord?’ 6 He said, ‘A hundred batos of oil.’ He said to him, ‘Take your bill, and sit down quickly and write fifty.’ 7 Then he said to another, ‘How much do you owe?’ He said, ‘A hundred koros of wheat.’ He said to him, ‘Take your bill, and write eighty.’
8 “His lord commended the dishonest manager because he had done wisely, for the children of this world are, in their own generation, wiser than the children of the light. 9 I tell you, make for yourselves friends by means of unrighteous mammon, so that when you fail, they may receive you into the eternal dwellings. 10 He who is faithful in a very little is faithful also in much. He who is dishonest in a very little is dishonest also in much. 11 If therefore you have not been faithful in the unrighteous mammon, who will commit to your trust the true riches? 12 If you have not been faithful in that which is another’s, who will give you that which is your own? 13 No servant can serve two masters, for either he will hate the one, and love the other; or else he will hold to one, and despise the other. You aren’t able to serve God and mammon.”
14 The Pharisees, who were lovers of money, also heard all these things, and they scoffed at him. 15 He said to them, “You are those who justify yourselves in the sight of men, but God knows your hearts. For that which is exalted among men is an abomination in the sight of God. 16 The law and the prophets were until John. From that time the Good News of God’s Kingdom is preached, and everyone is forcing his way into it. 17 But it is easier for heaven and earth to pass away, than for one tiny stroke of a pen in the law to fall. 18 Everyone who divorces his wife and marries another commits adultery. He who marries one who is divorced from a husband commits adultery.
19 “Now there was a certain rich man, and he was clothed in purple and fine linen, living in luxury every day. 20 A certain beggar, named Lazarus, was laid at his gate, full of sores, 21 and desiring to be fed with the crumbs that fell from the rich man’s table. Yes, even the dogs came and licked his sores. 22 It happened that the beggar died, and that he was carried away by the angels to Abraham’s bosom. The rich man also died, and was buried. 23 In Hades, he lifted up his eyes, being in torment, and saw Abraham far off, and Lazarus at his bosom. 24 He cried and said, ‘Father Abraham, have mercy on me! Send Lazarus, that he may dip the tip of his finger in water, and cool my tongue! For I am in anguish in this flame.’
25 “But Abraham said, ‘Son, remember that you, in your lifetime, received your good things, and Lazarus, in the same way, bad things. But now here he is comforted, and you are in anguish. 26 Besides all this, between us and you there is a great gulf fixed, that those who want to pass from here to you are not able, and that no one may cross over from there to us.’
27 “He said, ‘I beg you therefore, father, that you would send him to my father’s house; 28 for I have five brothers, that he may testify to them, lest they also come into this place of torment.’
29 “But Abraham said to him, ‘They have Moses and the prophets. Let them listen to them.’ 30 “He said, ‘No, father Abraham, but if one goes to them from the dead, they will repent.’ 31 “He said to him, ‘If they don’t listen to Moses and the prophets, neither will they be persuaded if one rises from the dead.’”

3. Fulfillment Notes

  • Until John (v. 16): A definitive marker of the transition. The Law and Prophets reached their climactic focus in John the Baptist; from then on, the New Creation Kingdom is the present jurisdictional reality.
  • Heaven and Earth to Pass (v. 17): A reference to the Old Covenant world. Jesus declares that the Law’s fulfillment is more certain than the existence of the physical universe, signaling that every stroke of the Law reached its terminus in Him.
  • Purple and Fine Linen (v. 19): The specific garments of the Levitical Priesthood (Exodus 28:5). This identifies the "Rich Man" as the religious establishment of the transition generation.
  • Great Gulf Fixed (v. 26): The permanent jurisdictional boundary between the dying Old World and the Established New Creation. Once AD 70 occurred, the separation became historically and spiritually absolute.

4. Annotations

  • Unrighteous Mammon (v. 9) — The currency and resources of the old, passing world. The Son instructs the remnant to use these to advance the interests of the Kingdom.
  • Abraham’s Bosom (v. 22) — A Hebrew idiom for the place of honor and inheritance. Lazarus (the remnant/gentile) finds rest where the biological elite (the Rich Man) assumed they belonged.
  • Anguish in this Flame (v. 24) — Represents the covenantal judgment of AD 70. The "Rich Man" system is tormented by the realization that their legal standing and physical city are being consumed.
  • Five Brothers (v. 28) — Likely representing the remaining "houses" of the religious elite who had yet to face the finality of the transition's end.

5. Fulfillment Commentary

1) The Shrewd Strategy of the Transition

The parable of the Shrewd Manager (Luke 16:1-9) is the Son’s instructional guide for the "Eighth Day" residents living within the final hours of the old age. The manager, realizing his tenure is over, uses the "lord's" resources to cancel debts and secure future relationships. In the same way, the transition generation was instructed to use the "Mammon" of the dying world to secure their standing in the New Creation. This was a legal move: by canceling debts (extending mercy), they were acting in the spirit of the Jubilee and aligning themselves with the coming Sovereign. The Son's glory is unveiled in His wisdom, teaching His people how to navigate the "foreclosure" of the old world with a focus on the "eternal dwellings" of the New World. This commentary affirms that true faithfulness is found in recognizing the temporary nature of the old and investing everything in the permanent reality of the King's Presence.

2) The Legal Finality of the Law's Terminus

In Luke 16:16-17, the Son delivers a definitive jurisdictional verdict. He announces that the "Law and the Prophets" remained the primary administration only "until John." From that point forward, the Kingdom of God is the active jurisdiction. However, He emphasizes that this is not a "breaking" of the Law, but a perfect "fulfilling" of it. Not a "tiny stroke of a pen" will fall until all is accomplished. This was the legal foundation for the New Creation: it is not a lawless world, but a world where the Law's requirements have been totally exhausted by the Sovereign. The "forcing of the way" into the Kingdom represents the urgency of the remnant to exit the old order before it "passes away." The Son’s glory is His status as the Fulfiller who closes the old book and opens the new, ensuring that the New Creation stands on a completed legal work.

3) The Eviction of the Religious Elite

The narrative of the Rich Man and Lazarus (Luke 16:19-31) is a specific covenantal judgment. The Rich Man, clothed in the "purple and fine linen" of the priesthood, represents the institutional leaders who feasted on the covenant blessings while leaving the "Lazarus" (the poor and the nations) to rot at the gate. Their "death" and subsequent "torment" represent the AD 70 destruction, where their religious and social status was physically and legally incinerated. Lazarus, however, is carried to "Abraham’s Bosom"—the place of true covenantal inheritance. This is the great reversal of the transition: the "last" (Lazarus) become the first, while the "first" (the religious elite) are evicted from the house of mercy. The Son’s glory is His role as the Judge who fixed the "Great Gulf," ensuring that the old order could never again cross over to contaminate the purity of the New World.

4) The Sufficiency of the Prophetic Word

The closing dialogue between the Rich Man and Abraham (Luke 16:29-31) exposes the hard heart of the old system. The Rich Man's plea for a supernatural sign for his "five brothers" is met with a legal refusal: "They have Moses and the prophets." The Son is teaching that the legal testimony already present in the scriptures was sufficient to identify Him. If they refused to "listen" to the shadows, they would not be "persuaded" by the Substance—even when the Son rose from the dead. This commentary highlights the tragedy of the first-century religious mind: they possessed the "Key of Knowledge" but used it to lock themselves into a burning house. The glory of the New Creation is found in those who "listen" and recognize the Voice of the Son in the pages of Moses, exiting the old order to find eternal life in the One who has truly risen.

8. New Creation Reality

In Luke 16, the glory of the Son is revealed as the power that flips the tables of the world and establishes your permanent comfort. As a citizen of the New Creation, you are "Lazarus" in Abraham's bosom—restored to the place of honor and inheritance. You do not live in the "Anguish" of trying to maintain a dying religious status, nor are you begging for "crumbs" from a professional priesthood. You inhabit the "True Riches" that have been delivered to your trust by the Sovereign. The "Thief" who hoarded the fine linen has been evicted, and a "Great Gulf" has been fixed to protect your rest. You live in a world where the Law has been perfectly finished, and your only requirement is to "listen" to the Voice that has already raised you from the dead. The transition is complete; you possess that which is "your own" in the presence of the King.

9. Reflection

Question: If the Son declared that the "Law and the Prophets were until John" (Luke 16:16), why do we still allow modern "Rich Men" (religious leaders) to drape themselves in the "purple" of institutional authority and use the Law to keep the "Lazarus" of the world outside the gate of the Father's mercy?
