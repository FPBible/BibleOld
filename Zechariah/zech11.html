Zechariah 11

The Rejection of the Good Shepherd and the Judgment of the Flock

1. Immediate Context

Zechariah 11 presents a dramatic and judicial shift from the restoration promises of Chapter 10. The prophet is commanded to perform a "sign-act": shepherd a flock destined for slaughter. This chapter documents the legal termination of the Old Covenant "brotherhood" between Judah and Israel. The central event is the rejection of the Good Shepherd, valued at the insulting price of thirty pieces of silver, which leads to the breaking of the two symbolic staffs—Favor (Beauty) and Union (Bands). This vision provides the judicial rationale for the AD 70 destruction, where the "fire" consumes the cedars of Lebanon (the elite) and a "worthless shepherd" is raised up to devour the apostate flock.

2. Scripture (WEB)

1 Open your doors, Lebanon, that the fire may devour your cedars! 2 Wail, fir tree, for the cedar has fallen, because the stately ones are destroyed. Wail, you oaks of Bashan, for the strong forest has come down. 3 A voice of the wailing of the shepherds! For their glory is destroyed: a voice of the roaring of young lions! For the pride of the Jordan is ruined.
4 Thus says Yahweh my God: "Shepherd the flock of slaughter. 5 Their buyers slaughter them, and go unpunished. Those who sell them say, 'Blessed be Yahweh, for I am rich;' and their own shepherds don’t pity them. 6 For I will no more pity the inhabitants of the land," says Yahweh; "but, behold, I will deliver the men everyone into his neighbor’s hand, and into the hand of his king. They will strike the land, and I will not deliver them out of their hand."
7 So I shepherded the flock of slaughter, especially the oppressed of the flock. I took for myself two staffs. The one I called "Favor," and the other I called "Union," and I fed the flock. 8 I cut off the three shepherds in one month; for my soul was weary of them, and their soul also loathed me. 9 Then I said, "I will not feed you. That which dies, let it die; and that which is to be cut off, let it be cut off; and let those who are left eat each other’s flesh." 10 I took my staff "Favor," and cut it apart, that I might break my covenant which I had made with all the peoples. 11 It was broken in that day; and thus the oppressed of the flock who saw me knew that it was the word of Yahweh.
12 I said to them, "If you think it best, give me my wages; and if not, keep them." So they weighed for my wages thirty pieces of silver. 13 Yahweh said to me, "Throw it to the potter, the handsome price that I was valued at by them!" I took the thirty pieces of silver, and threw them to the potter, in the house of Yahweh. 14 Then I cut apart my other staff, "Union," that I might break the brotherhood between Judah and Israel.
15 Yahweh said to me, "Take for yourself yet again the instruments of a foolish shepherd. 16 For, behold, I will raise up a shepherd in the land, who will not visit those who are cut off, neither will seek those who are scattered, nor heal that which is broken, nor feed that which is sound; but he will eat the flesh of the fat sheep, and will tear their hoofs in pieces. 17 Woe to the worthless shepherd who leaves the flock! The sword will be on his arm, and on his right eye. His arm will be completely withered, and his right eye will be totally blinded!"

3. Fulfillment Notes

  • Fire in Lebanon (v. 1): Historically, Lebanon was the source of the Temple’s wood. The "opening of the doors" signifies the Roman entry into Jerusalem and the burning of the Temple in AD 70.
  • Thirty Pieces of Silver (v. 12): The exact price paid to Judas Iscariot for the betrayal of Jesus (Matthew 26:15). In the Law, this was the price of a slave (Exodus 21:32).
  • Throwing to the Potter (v. 13): Fulfilled when Judas threw the silver into the Temple, and the priests used it to buy the "Potter's Field" (Matthew 27:3-10).
  • Breaking the Staffs (vv. 10, 14): Signifies the legal termination of the Old Covenant "Favor" and the "Union" (the corporate brotherhood) of ethnic Israel. After AD 70, the genealogical and tribal distinction of Israel was permanently dissolved.
  • The Worthless Shepherd (v. 16): Represents the apostate leadership (the Zealots or the Herodian dynasty) that led the nation into the suicidal war with Rome, devouring the people they were supposed to protect.

4. Annotations

  • Lebanon/Cedars: Often used as a metaphor for the pride and the high-ranking elite of Israel.
  • Favor (No’am): Can be translated as Beauty or Graciousness; represents God’s special covenantal kindness toward the nation.
  • Union (Chobelim): Means Binders or Unity; represents the organic and legal connection between the tribes under the Law.
  • Three Shepherds: Likely representing the three offices of Prophet, Priest, and King, which were "cut off" as functional Old Covenant offices when Christ arrived.
  • Worthless Shepherd: (Hebrew: ro’eh ha-elil) Literally "shepherd of nothingness" or "idol shepherd."

5. Fulfillment Commentary

The Judicial Price of a Slave

Zechariah 11 provides the legal "receipt" for the rejection of the Messiah. When the prophet asks for his wages (v. 12), the people weigh out thirty pieces of silver—the compensation for a gored slave. This "handsome price" was an act of supreme covenantal contempt. Yahweh’s command to "throw it to the potter" (v. 13) was a judicial decree. In the first century, this was literally executed. The silver that bought the Good Shepherd was cast into the Temple—the very "House" that was about to be destroyed—and used to purchase a field of death (the Potter's Field). This act legally closed the account of the Old Covenant age; the Shepherd was rejected, and the payment for His life became the price of the nation's burial ground.

The Breaking of the Staffs and the AD 70 Desolation

The breaking of the two staffs, "Favor" and "Union," documents the end of the Old Covenant administration. When "Favor" was broken (v. 10), it signified that the protective covenantal grace that had spared Israel from the nations was retracted. When "Union" was broken (v. 14), the "brotherhood" of the twelve tribes was legally dissolved. This was physically realized in the horrific civil wars within the walls of Jerusalem during the Roman siege (AD 66-70). The "worthless shepherd" (v. 16) was the judgment itself—the rise of tyrant leaders who "tore the hoofs" of the people. The "fire" in Lebanon (v. 1) was the literal burning of the Temple and its stately structures, leaving the "flock of slaughter" to eat "each other's flesh" (v. 9)—a detail recorded with chilling accuracy by the historian Josephus during the final siege.

8. New Creation Reality (Spiritual Instruction)

In the New Creation era, you are no longer part of the "flock of slaughter" subjected to "worthless shepherds." Spiritual Instruction: Your security is found in the Shepherd who was rejected, but who now sits on the throne.

The "Union" of ethnic Israel has been broken to make way for the "Union" of the Spirit, where there is no Jew or Gentile. Practical Application: Do not look for spiritual "Favor" in the structures or genealogical pride of the old world; those staffs were broken long ago. Your "Favor" is found in the indestructible life of Christ. Be wary of "worthless shepherds"—leaders who seek to "eat the fat" of the flock rather than feed the souls of the people. You belong to the Good Shepherd who gave His life for the sheep, and in His New Creation, there is no "fire" of judgment left for those who are His. You walk in the "Beauty" of a Covenant that can never be broken.
