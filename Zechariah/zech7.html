Zechariah 7

The Question of Fasting and the Warning of Wrath

1. Immediate Context

Two years after the initial eight night visions, a delegation from Bethel arrives in Jerusalem with a religious question: Should they continue to fast in the fifth month to mourn the destruction of the first temple? God's response through Zechariah abruptly shifts the focus from external ritual to internal, covenantal obedience. Instead of giving a simple "yes" or "no," God exposes the self-centeredness of their fleshly religion. This chapter serves as a profound prophetic indictment of apostasy. By reminding them of the historical judgment that fell upon their fathers for refusing the Spirit's word, Zechariah perfectly foreshadows the ultimate, first-century climax—the scattering and desolation of the generation that would harden their hearts against the final Prophet, Jesus Christ.

2. Scripture (WEB)

Source (verbatim): https://ebible.org/web/ZEC07.htm

1 It happened in the fourth year of king Darius that the word of Yahweh came to Zechariah in the fourth day of the ninth month, the month of Chislev.
2 The people of Bethel sent Sharezer and Regem Melech and their men to entreat Yahweh's favor,
3 and to speak to the priests of the house of Yahweh of Armies and to the prophets, saying, "Should I weep in the fifth month, separating myself, as I have done these so many years?"
4 Then the word of Yahweh of Armies came to me, saying,
5 "Speak to all the people of the land and to the priests, saying, 'When you fasted and mourned in the fifth and seventh month for these seventy years, did you at all fast to me, really to me?
6 When you eat and when you drink, don't you eat for yourselves and drink for yourselves?
7 Aren't these the words which Yahweh proclaimed by the former prophets when Jerusalem was inhabited and in prosperity, and its cities around her, and the South and the lowland were inhabited?'"
8 Yahweh's word came to Zechariah, saying,
9 "Thus has Yahweh of Armies spoken, saying, 'Execute true judgment, and show kindness and compassion every man to his brother.
10 Don't oppress the widow, the fatherless, the foreigner, nor the poor; and let none of you devise evil against his brother in your heart.'
11 "But they refused to listen, and turned their backs, and stopped their ears, that they might not hear.
12 Yes, they made their hearts as hard as flint, lest they might hear the law and the words which Yahweh of Armies had sent by his Spirit by the former prophets. Therefore great wrath came from Yahweh of Armies.
13 It has come to pass that, as he called and they refused to listen, so they will call and I will not listen," said Yahweh of Armies;
14 "but I will scatter them with a whirlwind among all the nations which they have not known. Thus the land was desolate after them, so that no man passed through nor returned; for they made the pleasant land desolate."

3. Fulfillment Notes

  • Empty Religion vs. True Justice: Anticipates the direct confrontation Jesus had with the first-century Pharisees, who meticulously tithed herbs but neglected justice and mercy (Matthew 23:23).
  • Rejection of the Spirit's Prophets: Stephen echoed this exact historical pattern in Acts 7, pointing out that the first-century generation was doing exactly as their fathers did: resisting the Holy Spirit and killing the prophets.
  • Great Wrath and Scattering: While historically referencing the Babylonian exile, this serves as the typological guarantee of the final "days of vengeance" (Luke 21:22-24) when the apostate nation was permanently scattered in AD 70.

4. Annotations

  • The Fifth Month Fast: An annual mourning observed by the Jews marking the burning of Solomon's temple by Nebuchadnezzar (2 Kings 25:8-9).
  • Bethel: Literally "House of God." Historically, this city was a center for illicit, mixed worship instituted by Jeroboam, highlighting the compromised religious nature of the delegation.
  • Seventy Years: The duration of the Babylonian captivity, a period characterized by divine discipline rather than true spiritual renewal.
  • Hearts Hard as Flint: A metaphor for total covenantal obstinacy and insensitivity to the conviction of the Holy Spirit.

5. Fulfillment Commentary

The Empty Rituals of the Old Order (Verses 1-3)

The delegation from Bethel brings a question steeped in fleshly, localized religion. For seventy years, they had been ritually fasting in the fifth month to commemorate the destruction of the first temple. Now that a new physical temple is being built, they want to know if they can stop the mourning. Their religion was bound entirely to physical stones and calendar dates.

This mindset perfectly captures the limitation of the Old Covenant system. It was a ministry of external ordinances. By the time of the first century, this religious externalism had completely metastasized. The Pharisees were obsessed with washing cups, fasting twice a week, and keeping Sabbath regulations, while utterly missing the presence of God standing in front of them. The New Creation reality, however, is not about geography or calendar fasts; it is about worshipping in Spirit and in truth (John 4:23-24).

The Self-Centeredness of Fleshly Religion (Verses 4-7)

God’s response is a piercing indictment: "Did you at all fast to me, really to me? When you eat and drink, don't you eat for yourselves?" The people were performing religious duties not out of love for God, but as a mechanism of self-pity and self-preservation. They mourned the loss of their physical city, but they did not mourn the loss of God’s glory or their own covenantal rebellion.

In fulfilled eschatology, this highlights why the Old Covenant system had to be dismantled. God desires mercy, not sacrifice. The first-century religious elite used the temple system to enrich and justify themselves. Jesus condemned them for devouring widows' houses while making long prayers for pretense (Mark 12:40). God reminds Zechariah's audience that the former prophets preached the exact same message when Jerusalem was prosperous, but the people ignored it then, just as they were prone to ignore it now.

The True Requirements of the Covenant (Verses 8-10)

Zechariah outlines what God actually required: "Execute true judgment, and show kindness and compassion... don't oppress the widow, the fatherless, the foreigner, nor the poor." The heart of the covenant was never meant to be a cold, legalistic transaction of animal blood and starving oneself on Tuesdays. It was meant to produce a society that reflected the righteousness and compassion of Yahweh.

This is the very ethic Jesus established in the Sermon on the Mount. The transition to the New Covenant was not a lowering of moral standards; it was the impartation of the Spirit so that the righteous requirement of the law might be fully met in us (Romans 8:4). The Old Covenant produced hypocrites who could fast perfectly but hated their brother; the New Covenant produces a transformed heart capable of executing true love and justice.

The Hardened Heart and Rejection of the Prophets (Verses 11-12)

Despite the clear instruction, the historical record of the fathers was devastating: "They refused to listen, and turned their backs, and stopped their ears... they made their hearts as hard as flint." This was not passive ignorance; it was active, aggressive resistance against the words sent by the Holy Spirit through the prophets.

This is perhaps the most crucial typological link to the end of the Old Covenant age. When Stephen stood before the Sanhedrin in Acts 7:51, he drew a direct line from Zechariah's generation to his own: "You stiff-necked people... you always resist the Holy Spirit! As your fathers did, so do you." The first-century Jewish leadership made their hearts like flint against Christ. They stopped their ears to the Gospel, guaranteeing that the covenant wrath promised in the Law would finally fall upon them.

The Inevitability of Covenant Wrath (Verses 13-14)

God declares a terrifying principle of divine reciprocity: "As he called and they refused to listen, so they will call and I will not listen." Because they rejected the terms of the covenant, God scattered them with a whirlwind, leaving the pleasant land desolate.

While this specifically references the Babylonian destruction of 586 BC, it stands as the unbreakable prophetic template for AD 70. Jesus wept over Jerusalem, declaring, "Behold, your house is left to you desolate" (Matthew 23:38), because they did not recognize the time of their visitation. When the Roman whirlwind came, the apostate nation called out for deliverance, but the heavens were silent. The physical land was made desolate permanently so that the New Creation—the spiritual, eternal Jerusalem—could stand as the sole, unshakeable habitation of God's people.

8. New Creation Reality

The Old Covenant exhausted people with empty religious rituals that could never change a heart of flint. The New Creation reality is that God has removed your heart of stone and given you His Spirit, empowering you to live out true justice and compassion rather than relying on dead, self-centered religion.

9. Reflection

In your own walk with God, are there any "fasts" or religious routines you perform mostly out of habit or for self-assurance, rather than out of a genuine desire to reflect His compassion to those around you?
