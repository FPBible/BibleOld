This appendix is a map. It is not a full commentary. It highlights the major theological themes that shape how the Book of Isaiah is utilized by the New Testament writers, and how Isaiah‚Äôs "end-of-world" imagery functions covenantally rather than cosmologically.

1) Covenant Lawsuit: Judgment With Moral Clarity

Isaiah repeatedly frames judgment as a formal covenant lawsuit (rib). God is not acting out of random, unpredictable anger. He acts as the righteous Judge exposing hypocrisy, economic oppression, false worship, and corrupt leadership within the nation of Israel.

Israel was bound by the specific terms of the Mosaic Covenant (Leviticus 26, Deuteronomy 28). Therefore, the judgments prophesied in Isaiah are the legal, contractual consequences of breaking those terms. Understanding this prevents the reader from applying these specific national judgments to the end of global human history.

2) The Holy One of Israel: God‚Äôs Character as the Centre

Isaiah‚Äôs God cannot be negotiated with or manipulated by empty religious rituals. He is completely holy, relentlessly faithful, and absolutely sovereign over the Gentile empires (Assyria, Babylon) that He uses as His instruments of judgment.

This holiness does not eliminate mercy‚Äîit defines it. The restoration promised in the latter half of Isaiah is not sentimental; it is righteous. God provides a legal mechanism (the Servant) to satisfy the demands of justice so that His mercy can flow freely to the nations.

3) The Servant: Suffering, Healing, Vindication

The "Servant" theme carries the entirety of Isaiah‚Äôs hope forward. The nation of Israel was called to be God's servant, but they failed due to spiritual blindness and deafness (Isaiah 42:19). Therefore, God condenses the calling of the nation into a single, faithful individual.

The New Testament writers unanimously read Jesus through Isaiah‚Äôs servant lens. He is the one who bears the rejection of the people, suffers a substitutionary death to absorb the covenant curse, and is ultimately vindicated by the resurrection to justify the many (Isaiah 53).

4) Zion: Covenant Centre and Renewed Identity

Zion language in Isaiah transitions from a physical, geographic hill in Jerusalem to an exalted, spiritual covenant centre. Promises of Zion‚Äôs renewal describe restored covenant life‚Äînot merely the reclamation of Middle Eastern territory.

The author of Hebrews confirms this transition, declaring that the first-century church had already "come to Mount Zion, and to the city of the living God, the heavenly Jerusalem" (Hebrews 12:22). Zion is the New Covenant community.

5) Nations: The Expansion of Belonging

Isaiah profoundly anticipates the Gentile nations turning to the God of Israel. He envisions a day when historic enemies will experience the "wolf and lamb" peace (Isaiah 11) and will flow into the mountain of the Lord to be taught His ways (Isaiah 2).

The New Testament presents this ingathering not as a secondary "Plan B" or a parenthesis, but as the climactic fulfillment of God's original promise to Abraham. The dividing wall of hostility is torn down, forming one renewed, multi-ethnic family in Christ.

6) New Heavens and New Earth: Covenant Renewal

Isaiah‚Äôs "New Creation" language (Isaiah 65‚Äì66) is not a statement about physical astrophysics or global geology. "Heaven and Earth" is the established biblical idiom for a covenantal system.

The promise of a New Heavens and New Earth describes a renewed covenant world‚Äînew worship, new identity, and the indwelling presence of the Spirit‚Äîstanding in stark contrast to the old, obsolete Mosaic system that was marked by condemnation, animal blood, and exile.
