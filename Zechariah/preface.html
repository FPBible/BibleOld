Preface to Zechariah

The Architectural Blueprints of the New Creation

The Theological Framework

To read the Book of Zechariah without a fulfilled covenantal framework is to walk through a grand cathedral blindfolded. Written approximately 520 BC, Zechariah ministered to a discouraged Jewish remnant who had returned from Babylonian exile. Their immediate task was to rebuild the physical temple in Jerusalem. Yet, the Holy Spirit used this historical moment as a prophetic canvas to paint the ultimate, spiritual restoration of God's people.

Zechariah is heavily apocalyptic, utilizing night visions, angelic mediators, colored horses, and measuring lines. These are not arbitrary symbols, nor are they waiting for a 21st-century fulfillment in the modern Middle East. They are the theological blueprints that the Apostle John would later draw upon to write the Book of Revelation. Zechariah prophesies the transition from the types and shadows of the Old Covenant to the permanent, abiding reality of the New Covenant.

The Two Temples

Throughout Zechariah, there is a profound tension between the physical and the spiritual. The prophet encourages the hands of Zerubbabel to finish the physical structure of wood and stone (Zechariah 4:9). However, God immediately elevates the discourse to reveal a future Temple built by "The Branch" (Jesus Christ), who would serve as both King and Priest upon His throne (Zechariah 6:12-13).

Under fulfilled eschatology, we recognize that the physical temple rebuilt in Zechariah's day—which was later expanded by Herod—was destined for final destruction. It was merely a scaffolding. Once the true spiritual Temple (the Body of Christ, the New Jerusalem) was fully established by the end of the first-century generation, the scaffolding was completely removed in AD 70.

The AD 70 Climax in Zechariah

As the book progresses into its final chapters (specifically chapters 12-14), the prophetic language shifts to intense covenantal judgment and vindication. Zechariah foresees a time when Jerusalem would become a "cup of reeling" and be besieged by the nations. He foresees the mourning for the pierced Messiah (fulfilled at the cross and its first-century aftermath) and the ultimate clearing away of the old sacrificial system.

Zechariah 14 presents the definitive end of the Old Covenant age. The catastrophic language describing the Mount of Olives splitting and the living waters flowing out from Jerusalem points directly to the events surrounding the fall of fleshly Jerusalem in AD 70 and the simultaneous, victorious emergence of the New Covenant Church. The living waters are a present, active reality for the believer today, not a postponed geographical anomaly.

How to Read This Commentary

As you move through the chapters of Zechariah in the Fulfilled Prophecy Bible, you will notice a strict adherence to audience relevance. We honor the historical context of the post-exilic Jews, but we trace every prophetic trajectory to its intended climax: the finished work of Christ and the complete establishment of His Kingdom in the first century.

Look closely for the markers of transition. Watch as the physical priesthood of Joshua gives way to the Melchizedekian priesthood of Christ. Watch as the physical walls of Jerusalem are replaced by God Himself becoming a "wall of fire" around His people. Zechariah is not a book of fearful, delayed doom; it is a triumphant declaration that the New Creation is here, it is fully measured, and it is secure forever.
