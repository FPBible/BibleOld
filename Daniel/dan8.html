Daniel 8

The Ram and the Goat • Antiochus Epiphanes • The Sealed Vision

1. Immediate Context

In Daniel 7, the prophet was given a sweeping vision of the four great empires culminating in Rome and the arrival of the Son of Man. In Chapter 8, the camera zooms in specifically on the transition between the second empire (Medo-Persia) and the third empire (Greece).

This chapter is not a mystery; the angel Gabriel explicitly names the nations involved. It details the meteoric rise of Alexander the Great (the Goat), the fracturing of his empire, and the subsequent rise of a specific "little horn" (Antiochus IV Epiphanes) who would horrifically desecrate the Jewish Temple in the second century BC. This historical desecration serves as the typological forerunner to the ultimate "Abomination of Desolation" that Jesus warns about in Matthew 24.

3 Then I lifted up my eyes, and saw, and behold, there stood before the river a ram which had two horns. The two horns were high; but one was higher than the other, and the higher came up last.
4 I saw the ram pushing westward, and northward, and southward; and no animals could stand before him, neither was there any who could deliver out of his hand; but he did according to his will, and magnified himself.
5 As I was considering, behold, a male goat came from the west over the surface of the whole earth, and didn’t touch the ground. The goat had a notable horn between his eyes.
6 He came to the ram that had the two horns, which I saw standing before the river, and ran on him in the fury of his power.
7 I saw him come close to the ram, and he was moved with anger against him, and struck the ram, and broke his two horns. There was no power in the ram to stand before him; but he cast him down to the ground, and trampled on him. There was no one who could deliver the ram out of his hand.
8 The male goat magnified himself exceedingly. When he was strong, the great horn was broken; and instead of it there came up four notable horns toward the four winds of the sky.
9 Out of one of them came out a little horn, which grew exceedingly great, toward the south, and toward the east, and toward the glorious land.
10 It grew great, even to the army of the sky; and some of the army and of the stars it cast down to the ground, and trampled on them.
11 Yes, it magnified itself, even to the prince of the army; and it took away from him the continual burnt offering, and the place of his sanctuary was cast down.
12 The army was given over to it together with the continual burnt offering through disobedience; and it cast down truth to the ground, and it did its pleasure and prospered.

20 The ram which you saw, that had the two horns, they are the kings of Media and Persia.
21 The rough male goat is the king of Greece. The great horn that is between his eyes is the first king.
22 As for that which was broken, in the place where four stood up, four kingdoms will stand up out of the nation, but not with his power.

3. Fulfillment Notes

  • Explicit Identification (vv. 20-21): There is no guesswork required here. Gabriel explicitly tells Daniel that the Ram is Medo-Persia and the Goat is Greece. The "notable horn" is the first king of Greece: Alexander the Great, who conquered the known world with unprecedented speed (the goat "didn't touch the ground").
  • The Two "Little Horns": Futurism often conflates all "little horns" into one future Antichrist. This is a massive error. The little horn in Daniel 7 emerges from the Fourth Beast (Rome) and represents the persecuting power of the Caesars (Nero). The little horn in Daniel 8 emerges from the Third Beast (Greece) and represents the historical Seleucid king, Antiochus IV Epiphanes, who sacked Jerusalem in 167 BC.
  • The Deathblow to Futurism (v. 26): Daniel is told to "seal up the vision, for it belongs to many days to come." The events of Antiochus Epiphanes were roughly 400 years away from Daniel's time. God considered 400 years to be "many days," requiring the book to be sealed. In Revelation 22:10, John is told not to seal the book because the time is "near." If 400 years is a long time that requires sealing, the unsealed events of Revelation cannot possibly be 2,000+ years in the future.

4. Annotations

  • four notable horns (v. 8) — When Alexander the Great died suddenly at the height of his power, his empire did not go to his children. It was divided among his four generals (Cassander, Ptolemy, Antigonus, and Seleucus).
  • the glorious land — The land of Judea/Israel, which sat squarely between the warring factions of the divided Greek empire (the Ptolemies in Egypt and the Seleucids in Syria).
  • cast down some of the stars — In apocalyptic language, "stars" often refer to the rulers, priests, or righteous leaders of God's covenant people (just as Abraham's descendants were to be like the "stars of heaven"). Antiochus slaughtered the Jewish leadership and corrupted the priesthood.
  • the continual burnt offering — Antiochus Epiphanes literally stopped the daily sacrifices in the Jerusalem Temple in 167 BC, setting up an altar to Zeus and sacrificing a pig on it. This sparked the Maccabean Revolt.

5. Fulfillment Commentary

1) The Clash of Empires (vv. 3–8)

The vision accurately maps the geopolitical shifts of the ancient world. The Medo-Persian empire (the Ram) pushed westward and became a massive, arrogant superpower. But a Goat from the west (Greece) charged it with terrifying fury.

Alexander the Great's military campaign was so astonishingly fast that Daniel describes the goat as flying across the earth without its feet touching the ground. He utterly shattered the Persian empire. However, verse 8 accurately predicts that at the height of his power, the great horn would be broken. Alexander died at age 32, and his vast empire was split into four lesser kingdoms.

2) The Greek Little Horn (vv. 9–14)

Out of the Seleucid division of the Greek empire arose a "little horn"—Antiochus IV, who gave himself the title "Epiphanes" (God Manifest). He turned his fury toward the "glorious land" (Judea).

What Antiochus did to the Jewish people is thoroughly documented in the historical books of 1 and 2 Maccabees. He banned circumcision, burned the Torah, slaughtered the priests (the "stars"), and completely stopped the daily sacrifices at the Temple, substituting them with pagan worship. This historical trauma was seared into the Jewish consciousness. When Jesus later warned His disciples about a coming "Abomination of Desolation" (Matthew 24:15), they immediately understood He was predicting a new desecration of the temple by Rome, mirroring what Antiochus had done centuries earlier.

3) The "Time of the End" Context (vv. 17–19)

Gabriel tells Daniel that this vision concerns "the appointed time of the end" and the "latter time of the indignation." Futurists often rip this phrase out of context and apply it to the end of the physical universe.

However, the context is strictly covenantal and local. The "indignation" refers to God's disciplinary wrath upon Israel for their covenant unfaithfulness. The "end" being spoken of here is the climax of that specific Old Covenant crisis under Antiochus, which would serve as the foreshadowing for the ultimate "end of the age" in AD 70 when the temple system was permanently destroyed.

4) The Sealed Book (v. 26)

This is one of the most important verses in the entire Bible for interpreting prophecy. Gabriel commands Daniel: "Seal up the vision, for it belongs to many days to come."

God’s standard for "many days" is clearly established here. The events of Antiochus Epiphanes were approximately 400 years in the future. Because 400 years is a long time, the prophecy was to be sealed.

Now compare this to Revelation 22:10. The angel tells John: "Do not seal up the words of the prophecy of this book, for the time is near." If Daniel had to seal his book for a 400-year delay, it is hermeneutically impossible to claim that John's unsealed book applies to events that are 2,000+ years away. Revelation was fulfilled in the first-century generation, exactly as Jesus promised.

8. New Creation Reality

The prophetic scriptures are not a loose collection of terrifying mysteries waiting to ambush us in the 21st century. God keeps time perfectly. When He said the time was "far off" for Daniel, it was. When He said the time was "near" for the early church, it was. You are reading covenant history for assurance, not headlines for doom. Because the book is unsealed and the prophecies are fulfilled, you can live today in the absolute certainty that the Kingdom of God has triumphed.
