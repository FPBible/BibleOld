Matthew 1: The Genealogy and Birth of the King

Immediate Context

Matthew opens his Gospel with a carefully structured genealogy tracing Jesus through the royal line of David. This was not mere historical record but a covenantal declaration: the promised Messiah-King has arrived to fulfill God's ancient promises to Israel. The virgin birth narrative establishes Jesus as both fully human (through Mary) and divinely appointed (conceived by the Holy Spirit). Matthew's original Jewish audience would immediately recognize the significance—this is the one who will save His people from their sins and establish the new covenant.

Scripture: Matthew 1 (LSV)

1A scroll of the birth of Jesus Christ, Son of David, Son of Abraham. 2Abraham begot Isaac, and Isaac begot Jacob, and Jacob begot Judah and his brothers, 3and Judah begot Perez and Zerah of Tamar, and Perez begot Hezron, and Hezron begot Ram, 4and Ram begot Amminadab, and Amminadab begot Nahshon, and Nahshon begot Salmon, 5and Salmon begot Boaz of Rahab, and Boaz begot Obed of Ruth, and Obed begot Jesse, 6and Jesse begot David the king. And David the king begot Solomon, of her [who had been] Uriah's, 7and Solomon begot Rehoboam, and Rehoboam begot Abijah, and Abijah begot Asa, 8and Asa begot Jehoshaphat, and Jehoshaphat begot Joram, and Joram begot Uzziah, 9and Uzziah begot Jotham, and Jotham begot Ahaz, and Ahaz begot Hezekiah, 10and Hezekiah begot Manasseh, and Manasseh begot Amon, and Amon begot Josiah, 11and Josiah begot Jeconiah and his brothers, at the Babylonian removal. 12And after the Babylonian removal, Jeconiah begot Shealtiel, and Shealtiel begot Zerubbabel, 13and Zerubbabel begot Abiud, and Abiud begot Eliakim, and Eliakim begot Azor, 14and Azor begot Sadok, and Sadok begot Achim, and Achim begot Eliud, 15and Eliud begot Eleazar, and Eleazar begot Matthan, and Matthan begot Jacob, 16and Jacob begot Joseph, the husband of Mary, of whom was begotten Jesus, who is named Christ. 17All the generations, therefore, from Abraham to David [are] fourteen generations, and from David to the Babylonian removal fourteen generations, and from the Babylonian removal to the Christ, fourteen generations.

18And of Jesus Christ, the birth was thus: for His mother Mary having been betrothed to Joseph, before their coming together she was found to have conceived from the Holy Spirit, 19and Joseph her husband being righteous, and not willing to make her an example, resolved to send her away privately. 20And on his thinking of these things, behold, a messenger of the LORD appeared to him in a dream, saying, "Joseph, son of David, you may not fear to receive your wife Mary, for that which was begotten in her is from [the] Holy Spirit, 21and she will bring forth a Son, and you will call His Name Jesus, for He will save His people from their sins." 22And all this has come to pass, that it may be fulfilled that was spoken by the LORD through the prophet, saying, 23"Behold, the virgin will conceive, and she will bring forth a Son, and they will call His Name Immanuel," which is, being interpreted, "God with us." 24And Joseph, having risen from sleep, did as the messenger of the LORD directed him, and received his wife, 25and did not know her until she brought forth her Son—the firstborn, and he called His Name Jesus.

Key Terms & Annotations

Son of David, Son of Abraham: Establishes Jesus' legal right to the Davidic throne and His role as the fulfillment of the Abrahamic covenant.
Fourteen generations: Matthew structures the genealogy in three sets of fourteen, likely corresponding to key covenant periods—promise, kingdom, and exile leading to restoration.
Save His people from their sins: Not a future, ongoing process, but a definitive covenantal deliverance accomplished through His death, resurrection, and the judgment on Old Covenant Israel.
Immanuel—God with us: Fulfilled in Jesus' earthly ministry and perfected in the New Covenant age when God dwells with His people through the Spirit.

Fulfillment Commentary: The King Arrives

1. The Genealogical Proof of Messianic Credentials

Matthew's genealogy is not arbitrary. It establishes beyond doubt that Jesus is the legal heir to David's throne. From a Full Preterist perspective, this matters immensely because the promises to David were fulfilled in the first century, not in some distant future millennial reign. Jesus is presented as the culmination of Israel's covenantal history. The three sets of fourteen generations mark the progression from promise (Abraham to David), to kingdom (David to exile), to restoration (exile to Christ). This structure demonstrates that Jesus appears at the climax of redemptive history, not to start a multi-thousand-year waiting period, but to immediately inaugurate the Kingdom.

2. The Virgin Birth and Covenant Transition

The virgin birth is essential to Full Preterism. Jesus is uniquely qualified to be the Last Adam—born of woman but not of Adam's fallen seed. This miraculous conception signifies a new creation breaking into the old. He is "God with us" not in some distant millennial temple, but in His incarnation, ministry, and ongoing presence through the Spirit in the New Covenant community established by AD 70.

3. "He Will Save His People from Their Sins"

This is past-tense salvation from the Full Preterist lens. The Greek construction indicates a completed action with ongoing effects. Jesus did not come to begin a salvation process that continues indefinitely. He came to save His people—the remnant of Israel and the ingrafted Gentiles—from their sins through His atoning death. The removal of sins is tied directly to the removal of the Old Covenant system. Hebrews 9:26 says He appeared "once at the end of the ages" to put away sin. That end came in AD 70 when the temple-based sacrificial system was obliterated, demonstrating that Christ's once-for-all sacrifice had accomplished full atonement.

New Creation Reality

Because Jesus has come and fulfilled all covenant promises, we live in the reality of Immanuel—God truly with us. The genealogy reminds us that God works through history, not in disconnected dispensational epochs. Every promise made to the fathers has found its "Yes" in Christ. We are not awaiting a future kingdom; we are presently seated with Christ in heavenly places, participating in the New Creation that began with His resurrection and was consummated in AD 70.

Matthew 2: The Flight to Egypt and Return

Immediate Context

The narrative of Jesus' infancy is steeped in Old Testament typology. Herod's murderous rage, the flight to Egypt, and the return to Nazareth all echo Israel's own history. Matthew is showing that Jesus is the true Israel, the faithful Son who will succeed where national Israel failed. The chapter also introduces a critical theme: the rejection of the Messiah by the Jewish leadership, a rejection that will culminate in AD 70.

Scripture: Matthew 2 (LSV)

1And Jesus having been born in Beth-Lehem of Judea, in the days of Herod the king, behold, magi from the east came to Jerusalem, 2saying, "Where is He who was born King of the Jews? For we saw His star in the east, and we came to worship Him." 3And Herod the king having heard, was stirred, and all Jerusalem with him, 4and having gathered all the chief priests and scribes of the people, he was inquiring from them where the Christ is born. 5And they said to him, "In Beth-Lehem of Judea, for thus it has been written through the prophet: 6'And you, Beth-Lehem, the land of Judah, you are by no means least among the leaders of Judah, for out of you will come one leading, who will feed My people Israel.'" 7Then Herod, having called the magi privately, inquired exactly from them the time of the appearing star, 8and having sent them to Beth-Lehem, he said, "Having gone, inquire exactly for the Child, and whenever you may have found, bring me back word, that I also having come, may worship Him." 9And they, having heard the king, departed, and behold, the star, that they saw in the east, went before them, until, having come, it stood over where the Child was. 10And having seen the star, they rejoiced with exceedingly great joy, 11and having come into the house, they found the Child with Mary His mother, and having fallen down, they worshiped Him, and having opened their treasures, they brought to Him gifts, gold, and frankincense, and myrrh, 12and having been divinely warned in a dream not to return to Herod, through another way they withdrew to their own region.

13And on their having withdrawn, behold, a messenger of the LORD appears in a dream to Joseph, saying, "Having risen, take the Child and His mother, and flee to Egypt, and be there until I may speak to you, for Herod is about to seek the Child to destroy Him." 14And he, having risen, took the Child and His mother by night, and withdrew to Egypt, 15and he was there until the death of Herod, that it might be fulfilled that was spoken by the LORD through the prophet, saying, "I called My Son out of Egypt." 16Then Herod, having seen that he was deceived by the magi, was very angry, and having sent forth, he slew all the male children in Beth-Lehem, and in all its borders, from two years and under, according to the time that he inquired exactly from the magi. 17Then was fulfilled that which was spoken through Jeremiah the prophet, saying, 18"A voice in Ramah was heard—weeping and much mourning—Rachel weeping [for] her children, and she did not will to be comforted because they are not." 19And Herod having died, behold, a messenger of the LORD appears in a dream to Joseph in Egypt, 20saying, "Having risen, take the Child and His mother, and be going to the land of Israel, for they have died—those seeking the life of the Child." 21And he, having risen, took the Child and His mother, and came into the land of Israel, 22and having heard that Archelaus reigns over Judea instead of his father Herod, he was afraid to go there, and having been divinely warned in a dream, he withdrew to the parts of Galilee, 23and coming, he dwelt in a city named Nazareth, that it might be fulfilled that was spoken through the prophets, that "He will be called a Nazarene."

Key Terms & Annotations

Magi from the east: Gentile seekers recognize the King while Jewish leaders remain indifferent—foreshadowing the Gentile inclusion and Jewish rejection themes.
Out of Egypt I called My son: Originally Hosea 11:1 referring to Israel's exodus; Matthew applies it typologically to Jesus as the true Israel.
Rachel weeping: Jeremiah 31:15, tied to the exile, now applied to Herod's infanticide—showing continuity of Israel's suffering leading to ultimate redemption in Christ.
He will be called a Nazarene: Not a direct quote but a thematic fulfillment, possibly playing on "branch" (netzer) imagery from Isaiah 11:1.

Fulfillment Commentary: Jesus as the True Israel

1. The Rejection Motif Begins

Herod's attempt to kill the infant King sets the tone for the entire Gospel. The political and religious establishment of Jerusalem will consistently reject Jesus, culminating in His crucifixion. From a Full Preterist standpoint, this rejection is not incidental but covenantal. Israel's leadership, representing the Old Covenant system, refuses to recognize their Messiah. This refusal seals their doom. The judgment on Jerusalem in AD 70 is divine vindication—God's wrath poured out on the generation that rejected and killed the Son.

2. Typological Fulfillment: Jesus Recapitulates Israel's History

Matthew explicitly connects Jesus to Israel's history through the Hosea 11:1 quotation. Just as Israel was called out of Egypt, so is Jesus. But unlike Israel, who rebelled in the wilderness, Jesus remains faithful. He is the faithful Son, the true Israel who will accomplish what the nation could not. This is not allegory—it is typological fulfillment. The Old Testament narratives were prophetic patterns pointing forward to Christ. When Jesus emerges from Egypt, He is not repeating history; He is fulfilling it. The exodus was a shadow; Jesus is the substance.

3. Rachel's Weeping and the End of the Old Covenant

Jeremiah 31:15 originally referred to the Babylonian exile, but Matthew sees Herod's massacre as a fulfillment of this lament. Why? Because both events—exile and infanticide—represent the suffering of Israel under the curse of the broken covenant. Yet Jeremiah 31 goes on to promise the New Covenant (Jeremiah 31:31-34). The weeping of Rachel is not the end; it is the birth pang of the New Creation. Herod's slaughter is a dark prelude to the coming judgment, but also to the resurrection and New Covenant inauguration. In AD 70, the old order of weeping and death is finally swept away.

New Creation Reality

We are not waiting for Jesus to "come out of Egypt" again or to repeat the exodus. Those events are fulfilled. We live in the age where the true Israel—the church—has been called out of bondage (sin and death under the Old Covenant) and brought into the Promised Land (the New Covenant rest). Our task is not to look backward or forward to another fulfillment but to live in the reality of what Christ has already accomplished.

Matthew 24: The Olivet Discourse—The End of the Age

Immediate Context

After pronouncing woes upon the scribes and Pharisees in chapter 23, Jesus leaves the temple and declares its coming desolation. His disciples ask when these things will happen and what will be the sign of His coming and the end of the age. This chapter is the most critical passage for Full Preterism. Jesus is not describing a distant, future "end of the world" but the imminent end of the Mosaic Age, culminating in the destruction of Jerusalem and the temple in AD 70. Every detail of this discourse finds its fulfillment within the first-century generation.
