The “Second Coming” — A Biblical Phrase?

Introduction

The phrase “Second Coming” does not appear anywhere in Scripture. Not in the Gospels. Not in the Epistles. Not in Revelation. It is a later theological label. The Bible speaks repeatedly of Christ’s coming, appearing, revelation, and day — but never once calls it the “Second Coming.”

Fulfillment Notes

  • The only “second” reference is Hebrews 9:28, which speaks of appearing a second time to those waiting.
  • All major “coming” passages were addressed to the first-century generation.
  • “Coming in the clouds” is Old Testament judgment language, not atmospheric descent.
  • The end described by Jesus was the end of the Old Covenant age, not the end of history.
  • AD 70 marked the visible covenantal transition promised by Christ.

The Only “Second” Text

“So Christ also, having been once offered to bear the sins of many, will appear a second time, apart from sin, to those who are eagerly waiting for him.” — Hebrews 9:28

This verse contrasts Christ’s first appearing to deal with sin with a second appearing connected to salvation. It does not describe a global end-of-time scenario. It was written to living believers who were actively waiting. The promise was imminent to them, not thousands of years removed.

The Biblical Language of “Coming”

Parousia (Presence)

Often translated “coming,” this word means presence or arrival. It does not inherently describe physical descent from outer space. It was used for royal visitation and authoritative arrival.

Erchomai (To Come)

This common verb is frequently used in judgment contexts. The Old Testament repeatedly describes God “coming” in judgment against nations without literal bodily manifestation.

Epiphaneia (Appearing)

This word refers to manifestation or visible unveiling — the public display of authority or glory.

Apokalypsis (Revelation)

This means unveiling. It is disclosure of what was hidden, not necessarily spatial relocation.

None of these terms require a future cosmic extinction event. They must be interpreted within covenantal and audience context.

Audience Relevance

Jesus declared plainly:

“This generation will not pass away until all these things happen.” — Matthew 24:34

The coming of the Son of Man was tied directly to that generation. It was connected to Jerusalem, the Temple, and the end of the age they were living in. The disciples were not asking about the end of world history. They were asking about the end of the covenant order represented by the Temple.

The destruction of Jerusalem in AD 70 fulfilled the covenant warnings given throughout the Law and the Prophets. It marked the definitive end of the Old Covenant system and the full manifestation of Christ’s authority.

Old Testament Background

“Coming in the clouds” is established prophetic imagery. When the Lord judged Egypt, Babylon, or Edom, He was described as coming with clouds, darkness, and shaking heavens. These descriptions were not literal atmospheric disturbances but covenantal judgment language.

The language of sun, moon, and stars darkening referred to the fall of ruling authorities. “Heaven and earth” often described covenantal order rather than the physical cosmos. When Jesus used these images, He was standing firmly in prophetic tradition.

Expanded Fulfillment Commentary

1. The Psychological Weight of the Phrase

The phrase “Second Coming” carries enormous emotional and theological weight. For many, it automatically triggers images of planetary destruction, graves opening across the globe, history collapsing, and time itself ending. None of those assumptions come from the phrase itself — because the phrase does not exist in Scripture. They come from later doctrinal systems layered over biblical language.

When we remove the phrase and return to the actual words used by the apostles, the discussion immediately becomes more precise. The New Testament writers spoke to real congregations facing real covenant transition pressure. They were not writing abstract end-of-cosmos speculation. They were announcing the climax of Israel’s covenant story.

2. Covenant Lawsuit and Prophetic Pattern

Throughout the Law and the Prophets, when Israel broke covenant, God warned of a coming “day.” That day involved judgment on Jerusalem, destruction of the Temple, scattering, and the end of a corrupt leadership system. The language used to describe those events was cosmic in imagery but covenantal in meaning: darkened sun, falling stars, shaking heavens, clouds of judgment.

Jesus stands directly in that prophetic stream. When He speaks of the Son of Man coming in the clouds, He is invoking Danielic enthronement language and Isaiah-style judgment imagery. He is not inventing a new category. He is declaring that the long-promised covenant reckoning was about to reach its climax.

3. Audience Time Indicators

The New Testament repeatedly anchors the coming of Christ in the time frame of the first-century audience:

  • “Some standing here will not taste death…”
  • “The Judge is standing at the door.”
  • “The end of all things is at hand.”
  • “In a very little while, He who is coming will come and will not delay.”

These statements cannot be stretched across millennia without emptying them of meaning. The apostles were not mistaken. They were not exaggerating. They were announcing an imminent covenant transition.

4. The End of the Age, Not the End of History

When the disciples asked about “the end of the age,” the word used was aion, meaning age or epoch. The age in view was the Mosaic covenant order centered in Jerusalem. That age had a temple, priesthood, sacrifices, genealogical boundaries, and national identity markers. It was already declared obsolete in Hebrews before AD 70. Its removal was historical and observable.

The destruction of the Temple in AD 70 was not a random geopolitical tragedy. It was the visible, covenantal confirmation that the Old Covenant had passed away. The sacrificial system ceased permanently. The genealogical priesthood ended irreversibly. The covenant administration tied to Sinai was removed from history.

5. Vindication and Kingship

The “coming” of the Son of Man is inseparably tied to vindication. Jesus was condemned by the Sanhedrin as a blasphemer. He declared they would see the Son of Man seated at the right hand of Power and coming with the clouds. That was a courtroom declaration. The destruction of Jerusalem vindicated His claim. The very system that condemned Him was dismantled.

This was not Christ descending to begin reigning. It was the public manifestation that He was already reigning.

6. The Resurrection Context

The resurrection language surrounding the coming passages is covenantal as well. Israel’s restoration had long been described in resurrection terms (Ezekiel 37). The transition from Old Covenant death to New Covenant life is framed as resurrection. The New Testament declares believers had already been raised with Christ. The corporate body was moving from one covenantal state to another.

The fulfillment of these promises does not eliminate hope. It establishes it. The resurrection life of the New Creation is present covenant reality, not postponed expectation.

7. What Remains

After the shaking came the unshakable kingdom. After the removal of the old came the establishment of what cannot be removed. The New Jerusalem is not a descending future city but the covenant people of God in union with Christ.

The story does not end in destruction. It culminates in clarity: Christ reigns. The covenant has transitioned. The kingdom stands. The New Creation is not waiting to begin — it is the present order of reality in Christ.

What We Affirm

We affirm that Christ came in judgment exactly as He promised. We affirm that He vindicated His authority within the lifetime of His hearers. We affirm that He ended the Old Covenant age and fully established the New Creation reality.

We reject the imposed idea of a yet-future “Second Coming” event that delays fulfillment and disconnects prophecy from its original audience.

Christ reigns. The kingdom stands. The New Creation is present reality.
