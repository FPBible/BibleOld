Matthew 1

Genealogy and Birth of Jesus the Messiah

Source text (LSV on eBible): Matthew 1 — Literal Standard Version

Immediate Context

Matthew opens by legally locating Jesus inside Israel’s covenant story: Abraham → David → exile → Messiah. This is not trivia—it’s covenant proof that the promised seed and King has arrived, and that the old age is reaching its appointed climax as the New Covenant reality breaks into history.

Scripture (LSV)

1The scroll of the genealogy of Jesus Christ, son of David, son of Abraham.

2Abraham begot Isaac, and Isaac begot Jacob, and Jacob begot Judah and his brothers,

3and Judah begot Pharez and Zarah of Tamar, and Pharez begot Hezron, and Hezron begot Ram,

4and Ram begot Amminadab, and Amminadab begot Nahshon, and Nahshon begot Salmon,

5and Salmon begot Boaz of Rahab, and Boaz begot Obed of Ruth, and Obed begot Jesse,

6and Jesse begot David the king. And David the king begot Solomon of her [who had been] Uriah’s,

7and Solomon begot Rehoboam, and Rehoboam begot Abijah, and Abijah begot Asa,

8and Asa begot Jehoshaphat, and Jehoshaphat begot Joram, and Joram begot Uzziah,

9and Uzziah begot Jotham, and Jotham begot Ahaz, and Ahaz begot Hezekiah,

10and Hezekiah begot Manasseh, and Manasseh begot Amon, and Amon begot Josiah,

11and Josiah begot Jeconiah and his brothers, at the Babylonian removal.

12And after the Babylonian removal, Jeconiah begot Shealtiel, and Shealtiel begot Zerubbabel,

13and Zerubbabel begot Abiud, and Abiud begot Eliakim, and Eliakim begot Azor,

14and Azor begot Zadok, and Zadok begot Achim, and Achim begot Eliud,

15and Eliud begot Eleazar, and Eleazar begot Matthan, and Matthan begot Jacob,

16and Jacob begot Joseph, the husband of Mary, of whom was begotten Jesus, who is named Christ.

17All the generations, then, from Abraham to David [are] fourteen generations, and from David to the Babylonian removal fourteen generations, and from the Babylonian removal to the Christ, fourteen generations.

18And of Jesus Christ, the birth was thus: for His mother Mary having been betrothed to Joseph, before their coming together she was found to have [conceived] in womb from the Holy Spirit,

19and Joseph her husband, being righteous, and not willing to make her an example, did wish privately to send her away.

20And on his thinking of these things, behold, a messenger of the LORD appeared to him in a dream, saying, “Joseph, son of David, you may not fear to receive Mary your wife, for that which was begotten in her is from the Holy Spirit,

21and she will bring forth a Son, and you will call His Name Jesus, for He will save His people from their sins.”

22And all this has come to pass, that it might be fulfilled that which was spoken by the LORD through the prophet, saying,

23“Behold, the virgin will have [conceived] in womb, and she will bring forth a Son, and they will call His Name Immanuel,” which is, being interpreted, “With us [is] God.”

24And Joseph, having risen from the sleep, did as the messenger of the LORD directed him, and received his wife,

25and did not know her until she brought forth her son—the first-born, and he called His Name Jesus.

Fulfillment Notes

  • Matthew begins with covenant credentials: Jesus is presented as the promised seed of Abraham and the legal heir of David—Israel’s story reaching its intended climax.
  • The genealogy’s structure signals intentional completion: history is being “summed up” into the Messiah as the old age approaches its end.
  • “He will save His people from their sins” is covenant language: deliverance from the Law’s condemnation and the exile-condition, completed in Christ and finalized as the old system passed away in the first-century transition.
  • “Immanuel” announces the shift from localized, temple-bound presence to God-with-us in the Messiah and His people—New Covenant presence, not future waiting.

Annotations

The scroll of the genealogy — “Book/record of origins”; a covenant/legal introduction that anchors Jesus in Israel’s promised storyline.
Jesus — “Yahweh saves”; the Name identifies mission: deliverance, not merely advice.
fourteen generations — A deliberately arranged pattern emphasizing Davidic identity and messianic legitimacy.
He will save His people from their sins — Not moral pep-talk; covenant removal of condemnation, the curse, and separation produced by the Mosaic administration.
Immanuel — “God with us”; temple-shadow gives way to direct presence in the Messiah and His covenant people.

Fulfillment Commentary

1) Why Matthew Starts With a Genealogy (and Why It’s Not “Filler”)

Matthew doesn’t open with philosophy. He opens with covenant proof. In Israel, identity was legal: who you were, what promises applied to you, and what authority you could claim all ran through covenant lineage and covenant history. By starting with “the scroll of the genealogy,” Matthew is not giving trivia—he is presenting credentials. Jesus is named “son of David” and “son of Abraham” because those titles are the two great covenant hinges: Abraham carries promise (seed and blessing), and David carries kingship (throne and rule). Matthew is saying: the promised Seed and the promised King is not a concept; He is a Person, and He has arrived inside the actual story of Israel.

2) The Patterned Generations: History Being “Summed Up” Into the Messiah

Matthew highlights the structure (three blocks of fourteen). That isn’t accidental. It’s a theological signal that Israel’s long narrative is reaching its intended goal. The story moves from promise (Abraham), to monarchy (David), to exile (Babylonian removal), and then to Messiah. This is the covenant arc: election → kingdom → judgment/exile → restoration in the Christ. In a fulfilled, covenantal lens, the point is not “numerology for its own sake,” but the claim that God has been steering this storyline toward a resolution. The Messiah is presented as the climax of the old world’s covenant development—the One who gathers the entire history into Himself and completes what the old administration could only anticipate.

3) “He Will Save His People From Their Sins” as Covenant Deliverance

The angelic announcement defines the mission: “He will save His people from their sins.” In modern reading, that line often collapses into private guilt management. In Matthew’s covenant context, “sins” is not merely “bad behavior”; it’s the covenant breach condition that kept Israel under condemnation—sin defined and empowered by the Law’s jurisdiction. The Mosaic administration could diagnose and expose sin, but it could not finally remove it. It produced repeated reminders, repeated sacrifices, and a conscience still haunted by unresolved separation.

So the naming of Jesus (“Yahweh saves”) is not sentimental; it’s covenantal. The Messiah would do what the Law could not: fulfill its demands, take its curse, and end its power to accuse. That salvation was inaugurated at the cross and publicly finalized as the old temple-world reached its historical end in the first-century covenant transition. The outcome for the New Creation people is not “still pending.” It is settled: no condemnation, no exile-status, no Law-based accusation.

4) Immanuel: The End of Localized Presence

Matthew quotes Isaiah: “they will call His Name Immanuel… God with us.” That is the headline of the entire gospel: presence. Under the old covenant, presence was mediated and restricted—priesthood, sanctuary boundaries, sacrifice schedules, and a physical center (Jerusalem). Immanuel signals the shift. God’s nearness is no longer managed by an external system; it is embodied in the Messiah and then shared with His covenant people. This is exactly why Matthew’s gospel will later intensify temple conflict: when true presence arrives, the shadow-system becomes obsolete.

In fulfilled terms, Immanuel is not merely “God showed up for a while.” It’s the announcement that the entire covenant arrangement is being transformed—from distance to nearness, from shadow to substance, from external regulation to internal life. When the old temple-world passed away, it didn’t erase God’s presence—it confirmed that presence had been relocated: the Messiah and His body as the living temple of the New Covenant.

5) The Women in the Line: Mercy, Inclusion, and the Unstoppable Promise

Matthew’s genealogy includes Tamar, Rahab, Ruth, and “her who had been Uriah’s.” That’s not random detail; it’s a signal that the covenant promise is not a fragile purity project. It moves through scandal, outsiders, and brokenness—without losing its line. This sets the tone for the gospel itself: the Messiah is not here to preserve the old boundary pride; He is here to bring the promise to its intended breadth and depth. This is the seed of Abraham heading toward blessing for the nations, and it begins by revealing that the story was never about human bragging rights.

6) The Opening Claim: The New Covenant Reality Starts Here

Matthew 1 is not merely prologue. It is the legal announcement that the covenant transition has begun. The rightful King is present in history. The promised salvation is defined. The presence of God is embodied. From here on, Matthew will press the question: if the Messiah has arrived, what happens to the old structures that opposed, obscured, or mediated what He now brings directly? The answer unfolds through the gospel and reaches its historical closure as the old age ends and the New Covenant world stands fully established.

New Creation Reality

You are not trying to “get God with you.” Immanuel has already arrived in Christ, and you live from that nearness. Your identity isn’t anchored in natural lineage, religious performance, or old-covenant measurement—it’s anchored in the finished covenant deliverance Jesus came to accomplish.

Reflection

Where do you still relate to God as “distant” or “conditional,” instead of living from the settled reality of Immanuel—God with us, now?
