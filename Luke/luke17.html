Luke 17

The Internal Kingdom • The Day of the Son of Man • The Removal of the Old

1. Immediate Context

Luke 17 presents a definitive unveiling of the nature of the New Creation jurisdiction. During the first-century transition (AD 30–70), there was intense pressure to locate the Kingdom in physical observation or nationalistic revolt. The Son corrects this by declaring that the Kingdom does not come with "observation" but is already "within" or "among" them. This is the hallmark of the New World: it is an internal, spiritual reality that bypasses the localized, Temple-centric mediation of the old age.

The chapter moves toward the AD 70 horizon with a series of sharp eschatological warnings. The Son describes the "Day of the Son of Man" as a sudden, cataclysmic separation, paralleling the days of Noah and Lot. As the legal foreclosure on Jerusalem approached, the people were warned not to "look back" at the old world's property or status. This chapter serves as the final jurisdictional briefing for the remnant, teaching them that their safety is found in their internal union with the Sovereign, while the physical structures of the old covenant are destined for a sudden and total removal.

2. Scripture (WEB)

Source (verbatim): https://ebible.org/web/LUK17.htm

1 He said to the disciples, “It is impossible that occasions of stumbling should not come, but woe to him through whom they come! 2 It would be better for him if a millstone were hung around his neck, and he were thrown into the sea, rather than that he should cause one of these little ones to stumble. 3 Be careful. If your brother sins against you, rebuke him. If he repents, forgive him. 4 If he sins against you seven times in the day, and seven times returns, saying, ‘I repent,’ you shall forgive him.”
5 The apostles said to the Lord, “Increase our faith.”
6 The Lord said, “If you had faith like a grain of mustard seed, you would tell this sycamore tree, ‘Be uprooted, and be planted in the sea,’ and it would obey you. 7 But who is there among you, having a servant plowing or keeping sheep, that will say when he comes in from the field, ‘Come immediately and sit down at the table,’ 8 and will not rather tell him, ‘Prepare my supper, clothe yourself properly, and serve me, while I eat and drink. Afterward you shall eat and drink’? 9 Does he thank that servant because he did the things that were commanded? I think not. 10 Even so you also, when you have done all the things that are commanded you, say, ‘We are unworthy servants. We have done our duty.’”
11 It happened as he was on his way to Jerusalem, that he was passing along the borders of Samaria and Galilee. 12 As he entered into a certain village, ten men who were lepers met him, who stood at a distance. 13 They lifted up their voices, saying, “Jesus, Master, have mercy on us!”
14 When he saw them, he said to them, “Go and show yourselves to the priests.” It happened that as they went, they were cleansed. 15 One of them, when he saw that he was healed, turned back, glorifying God with a loud voice. 16 He fell on his face at his feet, giving him thanks; and he was a Samaritan. 17 Jesus answered, “Weren’t the ten cleansed? But where are the nine? 18 Were there none found who returned to give glory to God, except this stranger?” 19 He said to him, “Get up, and go your way. Your faith has made you well.”
20 Being asked by the Pharisees when God’s Kingdom would come, he answered them, “God’s Kingdom doesn’t come with observation; 21 neither will they say, ‘Look, here!’ or, ‘Look, there!’ for behold, God’s Kingdom is within you.”
22 He said to the disciples, “The days will come when you will desire to see one of the days of the Son of Man, and you will not see it. 23 They will tell you, ‘Look, here!’ or ‘Look, there!’ Don’t go away, nor follow after them, 24 for as the lightning, when it flashes out of the one part under the sky, shines to the other part under the sky; so will the Son of Man be in his day. 25 But first he must suffer many things and be rejected by this generation.
26 “As it happened in the days of Noah, even so will it be also in the days of the Son of Man. 27 They ate, they drank, they married, they were given in marriage, until the day that Noah entered into the ship, and the flood came and destroyed them all. 28 Likewise, even as it happened in the days of Lot: they ate, they drank, they bought, they sold, they planted, they built; 29 but in the day that Lot went out from Sodom, it rained fire and sulfur from the sky and destroyed them all. 30 It will be the same way in the day that the Son of Man is revealed. 31 In that day, he who will be on the housetop, and his goods in the house, let him not go down to take them away. He who is in the field, let him likewise not turn back. 32 Remember Lot’s wife! 33 Whoever seeks to save his life will lose it, but whoever loses his life will preserve it. 34 I tell you, in that night there will be two people in one bed: one will be taken, and the other will be left. 35 There will be two women grinding grain together: one will be taken, and the other will be left.” 37 They answering, said to him, “Where, Lord?” He said to them, “Where the body is, there will the vultures also be gathered together.”

3. Fulfillment Notes

  • God’s Kingdom is Within You (v. 21): entos hymōn. This is the pivotal jurisdictional claim of the transition. The Kingdom is not a physical geography to be "observed," but a present spiritual reality inhabited by the remnant.
  • This Generation (v. 25): The Son explicitly anchors the rejection, the suffering, and the subsequent "Day" to the contemporary first-century generation.
  • As in the Days of Noah/Lot (vv. 26-29): Historical parallels for the AD 70 destruction. These were not global events but specific local judgments that removed an old order to preserve a remnant.
  • One Taken, One Left (vv. 34-35): Describes the radical separation during the Roman siege of Jerusalem. Those "taken" were taken in judgment; those "left" were the preserved remnant of the New Creation.

4. Annotations

  • Observation (v. 20) — paratērēseōs. Refers to religious or astrological spying. The Son declares that the New Creation does not depend on the "Performance" of signs.
  • Lightning (v. 24) — Symbolizes the inescapable and universal nature of the judgment against the old world order. There would be no secret "here" or "there."
  • Remember Lot’s Wife (v. 32) — A warning against "looking back" to the old world’s property, status, or ritual security as the AD 70 horizon approached.
  • Where the Body Is (v. 37) — A Hebrew idiom for the location of judgment. Where the "dead body" of the Old Covenant system lay, the "vultures" (Roman eagles) would gather for the final removal.

5. Fulfillment Commentary

1) The Manifestation of Internal Jurisdiction

In the response to the Pharisees (Luke 17:20-21), the Son unveils the fundamental nature of New Creation glory. The old religious mind was obsessed with "observation"—seeking physical signs, political revolutions, and ritualistic proofs. The Son declares that the Kingdom "doesn't come with observation." Instead, it is "within you" or "among you." This was a massive legal pivot. It meant that the Kingdom’s authority was no longer tied to the physical Temple or the biological borders of Israel. It was a present, internal reality based on union with the Sovereign. This provided the transition generation with a status that was independent of their earthly circumstances. They did not have to wait for a physical kingdom to be "observed"; they were already inhabiting a jurisdiction of the Spirit that the old world's "spying" eyes could not perceive.

2) The Cleansing of the Ten: Faith Over Ritual

The healing of the ten lepers (Luke 17:12-19) serves as a visual demonstration of the Kingdom’s "within" nature. All ten were cleansed as they obeyed the command to go to the priests, but only one—a Samaritan "stranger"—returned to recognize the Source. The Son’s glory is manifest in His authority to cleanse by a word, but the true restoration occurred for the one whose faith brought him back to Jesus' feet. The "nine" sought the ritual validation of the old world's priesthood, while the "one" found his standing in the Sovereign's presence. This commentary affirms that the New Creation is for those who look past the shadows of the old ritual to the substance of the King. The Samaritan's "wellness" (*sesōken*) was not just physical; it was a jurisdictional wholeness based on his recognition of the manifest majesty of God standing on the road.

3) The Suddenness of Covenantal Removal

The Son’s discourse on the "Day of the Son of Man" (Luke 17:26-30) provides the historical blueprint for the AD 70 transition. He uses the days of Noah and Lot to show that while the old world continues in its "eating and drinking" (Exhausting Performance), a sudden legal foreclosure is occurring. The New Creation remnant is the "Noah" and "Lot" being called out of a system scheduled for destruction. This was the lived reality of the first-century believers. They were instructed not to be tethered to their "goods" in the old house, but to be ready for the "revelation" of the Son’s judgment. This commentary highlights that the AD 70 horizon was the physical manifestation of this spiritual "Day," where the old order was removed "as lightning" to make room for the established reality of the Kingdom that cannot be shaken.

4) The Separation of the Remnant

The closing warnings about "one taken and one left" (Luke 17:34-37) describe the radical covenantal separation of the transition generation. Those "taken" were taken in the "flood" of judgment—captured or destroyed during the Roman war. Those "left" were the preserved citizens of the New World, the remnant who had heeded the Son's warnings. This separation was not a future event but the historical finality of AD 70. The "vultures" (the Roman legions) gathered where the "body" of the old system lay. The Son’s glory is seen in His role as the Sovereign who warns His people to lose their "life" in the old world to preserve it in the New. This commentary affirms that we live on the other side of this separation, inhabiting a world where the "Strong Man" has been removed and the internal Kingdom is our permanent and unshakable residence.

8. New Creation Reality

In Luke 17, the glory of the Son is revealed as the power that establishes your Kingdom identity "within you." As a citizen of the New Creation, you do not need to "look here" or "look there" for a physical sign of God's favor. You inhabit a jurisdictional rest that is independent of the "chaos" of the world or the "performance" of religion. You are the "one" who has returned to give glory to the Sovereign, and your "faith has made you well." The "Thief" of religious observation has been silenced. You live on the other side of the AD 70 "flood," in a world where the old has been taken and you have been left to reign. Your life is not found in "saving" your status in a dying system, but in resting in the internal presence of the King.

9. Reflection

Question: If the Son declared that the Kingdom is "within you" and doesn't come with "observation" (Luke 17:21), why do we still allow modern "leaders" to point us to external "signs," political movements, or religious "observations" as the proof of God's presence?
