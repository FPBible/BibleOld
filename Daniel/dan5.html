Daniel 5

Belshazzar's Feast • The Writing on the Wall • The Fall of Babylon

1. Immediate Context

Decades have passed since the events of Chapter 4. King Nebuchadnezzar is dead, and his descendant Belshazzar is acting as co-regent of Babylon. The mighty Medo-Persian army is outside the city walls, but Belshazzar feels completely secure inside Babylon's impregnable defenses.

In a display of supreme blasphemy, he orders the holy gold and silver vessels—stolen from the Temple in Jerusalem—to be used for a drunken feast praising pagan gods. God immediately interrupts the party with a disembodied hand writing a terrifying judgment on the wall. By the time the sun rises, the first of Daniel's prophesied empires (the Head of Gold) is dead, and the second empire (the Chest of Silver) has taken its place.

3. Fulfillment Notes

  • Sudden Destruction (v. 30): The Apostle Paul warned the Thessalonians that when the wicked say "Peace and safety" (like Belshazzar feasting securely behind his walls), then "sudden destruction will come upon them" (1 Thess 5:3). Babylon's overnight collapse is the exact prophetic type for the sudden, inescapable judgment that fell upon apostate Jerusalem in AD 70.
  • Desecrating the Holy Things (v. 2): Belshazzar's ultimate sin was taking the sacred things of God's covenant and using them for pagan, self-glorifying worship. Centuries later, the Jewish religious leaders would take the sacred things of the Law and the Temple and use them to reject, mock, and crucify the Lord of Glory. Both acts resulted in the total destruction of their respective cities.
  • The Timeline Advances (v. 31): Nebuchadnezzar's dream in Daniel 2 is officially rolling forward. The Head of Gold (Babylon) is dead. The Chest of Silver (Medo-Persia) has taken the throne. There is no stopping the prophetic clock; it is ticking steadily toward the arrival of the Roman Empire and the Kingdom of God.

4. Annotations

  • vessels which Nebuchadnezzar... had taken — These were the consecrated gold and silver items from Solomon's Temple, dedicated exclusively for the worship of Yahweh.
  • fingers of a man's hand — This terrifying display of divine power echoes Exodus 31:18, where the Law was written by the "finger of God." It represents the undeniable, inescapable verdict of the heavenly courtroom.
  • Mene, Mene, Tekel, Upharsin — These are Aramaic terms related to weights and currency (a mina, a shekel, and half-pieces). Daniel brilliantly decodes the double-meaning: God has "numbered" (Mene) the days of the empire; the king has been "weighed" (Tekel) and found too light/worthless; the kingdom will be "divided" (Peres/Upharsin) and given to the Persians.

5. Fulfillment Commentary

1) The Arrogance of the Beast (vv. 1–4)

The Medo-Persian army, led by Cyrus the Great, was literally encamped outside the walls of Babylon. Yet, Belshazzar was so confident in the massive, impenetrable walls of his city (which were wide enough to race chariots on) that he decided to throw a party for a thousand nobles.

This is the defining characteristic of "Babylon" throughout scripture: an arrogant, impenetrable sense of self-sufficiency. In Revelation 18, Mystery Babylon (apostate Jerusalem) boasts in her heart, "I sit as a queen, and am no widow, and will in no way see mourning." Belshazzar believed he was untouchable. To prove it, he brought out the holy vessels of Yahweh to toast to gods of wood and stone. He crossed the ultimate covenantal line.

2) The Writing on the Wall (vv. 5–24)

The divine lawsuit was served instantly. The writing on the wall was not a warning of things that might happen if Belshazzar didn't repent; it was the final, unchangeable verdict of the heavenly court. His time for repentance had expired.

Daniel’s rebuke of the king is blistering. He reminds Belshazzar of what happened to his grandfather, Nebuchadnezzar, who was driven into the fields like a beast until he acknowledged the Most High. Daniel says, "You... have not humbled your heart, though you knew all this." Belshazzar sinned against the light. He knew the history of God's sovereignty, but he chose to mock it anyway.

3) Weighed in the Balances (vv. 25–28)

TEKEL: You are weighed in the balances, and are found wanting.

This is the terrifying reality of God's perfect justice. Human empires measure success by wealth, military might, and the height of their walls. But God places the empire on the scales of His covenant justice. Belshazzar had zero moral or spiritual weight. His empire was an empty shell of pride, completely devoid of righteousness.

4) Sudden Destruction (vv. 29–31)

"In that night Belshazzar the Chaldean King was slain."

There was no drawn-out, multi-year siege. History records that the Medes and Persians achieved a brilliant tactical victory. They diverted the flow of the Euphrates River (which ran directly under the walls of Babylon) and simply marched their army into the city through the dry riverbed, completely undetected while the Babylonians were drunk.

This is the prophetic archetype for the end of an age. The transition of power was instantaneous. The Book of Revelation uses this exact same imagery to describe the fall of the persecuting Jewish system: "Therefore in one day her plagues will come... for in an hour your judgment has come" (Rev 18:8, 10). The judgment of God does not always announce itself with a long, slow decline; sometimes, the river runs dry, the enemy is inside the gates, and the age ends in a single night.

You are reading covenant history for assurance, not headlines for doom. You belong to the Kingdom that cannot be shaken or conquered in the night. Because Christ has satisfied the scales of God's justice on your behalf, you never have to fear the writing on the wall.
