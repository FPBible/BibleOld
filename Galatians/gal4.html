Galatians 4

From Slaves to Sons · The End of the Guardian

1. Immediate Context

Paul shifts from establishing the theology of justification to addressing the urgent relational and identity crisis facing the Galatians. Having demonstrated that the law was a temporary guardian, he exposes the tragic irony of Gentile believers willingly submitting to Old Covenant slavery after Christ has already granted them the full rights of sonship. Caught in the intense, first-century overlap of the ages, the Galatians are being tempted to trade the present reality of the Spirit for the external boundary markers of a fading, earth-bound system.

2. Scripture (WEB)

Source (verbatim): https://ebible.org/web/GAL04.htm

1 But I say that so long as the heir is a child, he is no different from a bondservant, though he is lord of all;
2 but is under guardians and stewards until the day appointed by the father.
3 So we also, when we were children, were held in bondage under the elemental principles of the world.
4 But when the fullness of the time came, God sent out his Son, born to a woman, born under the law,
5 that he might redeem those who were under the law, that we might receive the adoption of children.
6 And because you are children, God sent out the Spirit of his Son into your hearts, crying, “Abba, Father!”
7 So you are no longer a bondservant, but a son; and if a son, then an heir of God through Christ.
8 However at that time, not knowing God, you were in bondage to those who by nature are not gods.
9 But now that you have come to know God, or rather to be known by God, why do you turn back again to the weak and miserable elemental principles, to which you desire to be in bondage all over again?
10 You observe days, months, seasons, and years.
11 I am afraid for you, that I might have wasted my labor for you.
12 I beg you, brothers, become as I am, for I also have become as you are. You did me no wrong,
13 but you know that because of weakness of the flesh I preached the Good News to you the first time.
14 That which was a temptation to you in my flesh, you didn’t despise nor reject; but you received me as an angel of God, even as Christ Jesus.
15 What was the blessing you enjoyed? For I testify to you that, if possible, you would have plucked out your eyes and given them to me.
16 So then, have I become your enemy by telling you the truth?
17 They zealously seek you in no good way. No, they desire to alienate you, that you may seek them.
18 But it is always good to be zealous in a good cause, and not only when I am present with you.
19 My little children, of whom I am again in travail until Christ is formed in you—
20 but I could wish to be present with you now, and to change my tone, for I am perplexed about you.
21 Tell me, you that desire to be under the law, don’t you listen to the law?
22 For it is written that Abraham had two sons, one by the handmaid, and one by the free woman.
23 However, the son by the handmaid was born according to the flesh, but the son by the free woman was born through promise.
24 These things contain an allegory, for these are two covenants. One is from Mount Sinai, bearing children to bondage, which is Hagar.
25 For this Hagar is Mount Sinai in Arabia, and answers to the Jerusalem that exists now, for she is in bondage with her children.
26 But the Jerusalem that is above is free, which is the mother of us all.
27 For it is written, “Rejoice, you barren who don’t bear. Break out and shout, you who don’t travail. For more are the children of the desolate than of her who has a husband.”
28 Now we, brothers, as Isaac was, are children of promise.
29 But as then, he who was born according to the flesh persecuted him who was born according to the Spirit, so also it is now.
30 However what does the Scripture say? “Throw out the handmaid and her son, for the son of the handmaid will not inherit with the son of the free woman.”
31 So then, brothers, we are not children of a handmaid, but of the free woman.

3. Fulfillment Notes

  • Time-Frame Statements: "When the fullness of the time came" marks the decisive, first-century climax of redemptive history. The transition from slavery to sonship was a present reality for the original audience.
  • Audience Indicators: Paul confronts Gentiles who are beginning to "observe days, months, seasons, and years"—clear indicators they were being pressured into adopting the Jewish liturgical calendar.
  • First-Century Contextual Anchors: "The Jerusalem that exists now" identifies the literal, earthly city of Jerusalem before its destruction in AD 70. Paul explicitly ties it to bondage and the Old Covenant.
  • Covenant Transition Markers: The command to "Throw out the handmaid and her son" is a prophetic declaration that the Old Covenant system would not share the inheritance with the New Covenant and was about to be completely removed.

4. Annotations

  • Elemental principles (stoicheia): Refers to basic religious systems that govern identity through external regulations. Paul equates submitting to the Torah with submitting to pagan, world-bound systems of works.
  • Abba, Father: Deeply intimate familial language that signifies the Holy Spirit has already enacted a completed legal status of adoption.
  • Jerusalem that exists now: The earthly center of the Old Covenant, which rejected the Messiah and remained enslaved to the law.
  • Jerusalem that is above: The spiritual reality of the New Covenant—the true, eternal home and mother of all believers, free from the law of sin and death.

5. Fulfillment Commentary

The Fullness of Time and Completed Adoption (v. 1-7)

Paul uses the analogy of a wealthy heir who, while still a child, is functionally no different from a slave because he is under the control of guardians. This represents the condition of humanity prior to the incarnation. The Old Covenant served as this temporary guardian. "The fullness of the time" is not a vague spiritual concept; it marks the precise, historical climax of God's redemptive plan in the first century. Christ arrived precisely when the old era had exhausted its purpose.

Through Christ's redemptive work, adoption is not presented as a progressive journey, but as a finished legal reality. The Galatians do not need to earn their sonship through Torah obedience; the Spirit crying "Abba, Father" within them is the ultimate proof that the transition has already occurred. To return to the law is to voluntarily step back into childhood supervision after the Father has already handed over the keys to the estate.

The Tragedy of Returning to Elemental Principles (v. 8-11)

Paul delivers a stunning theological blow to the Judaizers by categorizing the Old Covenant law alongside pagan idolatry. Before knowing Christ, the Gentiles were enslaved to false gods. Now, they are turning to the "weak and miserable elemental principles" by observing Jewish liturgical calendars ("days, months, seasons, and years").

In the framework of fulfilled eschatology, relying on external, calendar-based religion is a covenantal downgrade. It seeks to map out spiritual reality using earth-bound, fading shadows instead of resting in the permanent light of the New Creation. Paul fears his labor was wasted because returning to boundary-marker religion nullifies the grace of God entirely.

The Relational Cost of Covenantal Drift (v. 12-20)

The commentary shifts to an intensely personal appeal. Paul reminds the Galatians of the deep bond they once shared, when they received him despite his physical weakness. The gospel of grace creates environments of radical love and shared belonging. However, the introduction of law-based religion immediately fractures relationships.

Paul exposes the tactic of the false teachers: "they desire to alienate you, that you may seek them." Law-based influence thrives on separation, hierarchy, and dependence. The Judaizers want to build a wall between Paul and the Galatians so they can act as the exclusive gatekeepers of God's favor. Grace liberates, while the law isolates to control.

The Allegory of Two Covenants (v. 21-27)

Paul employs an aggressive typological argument, explicitly stating that Hagar and Sarah represent two distinct covenants. Hagar, the slave woman, corresponds to Mount Sinai and "the Jerusalem that exists now." This was a revolutionary statement in the first century. The Judaizers believed earthly Jerusalem was the pinnacle of God's blessing. Paul declares it is actually the center of slavery.

In contrast, the "Jerusalem that is above" represents the New Covenant. This is not a physical city hovering in the sky waiting to descend in our future; it is the present, operating reality of the Spirit-born church. As children of promise (like Isaac), believers derive their identity from this free, heavenly reality, not from the doomed, earthly system centered in the Middle East.

Casting Out the Bondwoman (v. 28-31)

The historical tension in the first-century church is perfectly captured in verse 29: the child born of the flesh (the Old Covenant establishment) was actively persecuting the child born of the Spirit (the early Christian church). The earthly Jerusalem was fiercely resisting its obsolescence.

Paul points to the ultimate scriptural verdict: "Throw out the handmaid and her son." This is a prophetic mandate declaring that the two systems cannot permanently coexist. The Old Covenant and its law-based righteousness would not share the inheritance with the New Creation. This theological eviction notice would be visually and historically finalized a short time later with the destruction of the earthly temple in AD 70, leaving only the free children of promise standing.

8. New Creation Reality

Galatians 4 calls you to live entirely from a place of belonging, not toward it. If you are in Christ, you are already a son, already an heir, and already completely free. Any religious system or teaching that places you back under the supervision of performance, fear, or external boundary markers is actively denying the Spirit of adoption who already cries “Abba” within you. You are a child of the free woman; walk in the freedom of the completed New Covenant.

9. Reflection

Where might I still be living with the mindset of a slave, striving to earn approval in a house where I have already been declared a full son and heir?
