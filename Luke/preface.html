Preface — Luke

The Kingdom Come and Jerusalem Fallen

Luke writes as a historian with theological precision. His Gospel is not a timeless spiritual manual. It is the documented account of the Kingdom arriving, the old covenant ending, and Jerusalem falling—exactly as Jesus said it would, within that generation.

Luke knows what happened in AD 70. He writes after the Temple burned, after the city was razed, after the covenant world his readers had known collapsed. His Gospel traces the path from Jesus weeping over the city to its destruction, from the announcement of the Kingdom to its vindication through judgment.

Luke the Historian

Luke opens with a historical claim. He has investigated everything carefully from the beginning, consulting eyewitnesses, compiling an orderly account so that his reader, Theophilus, might have certainty concerning the things he has been taught. This is not mythology. It is documented testimony.

Luke-Acts functions as a two-volume work. The Gospel shows the Kingdom arriving and the old order being condemned. Acts shows the transition—the overlap period when the new covenant community emerged while the Temple still stood, and the gospel went to the nations before Jerusalem fell. Together, they tell the story of covenantal shift.

This Generation

The most critical phrase for reading Luke is "this generation." Jesus uses it repeatedly, and it governs the entire narrative.

  • "The blood of all the prophets, shed from the foundation of the world, may be charged against this generation" (11:50)
  • "This is an evil generation. It seeks for a sign" (11:29)
  • "This generation will not pass away until all has taken place" (21:32)

This generation is not a metaphor for humanity in general. It is the specific generation Jesus addressed—the generation alive when he spoke, the generation that would witness the Temple's destruction. Jesus places a timeline on the judgment he announces: within their lifetimes, within forty years, everything he predicts would be fulfilled.

Stretching "this generation" across two thousand years or more violates the plain meaning of the text. Luke expects his readers to recognize that the generation Jesus addressed lived to see the judgment fall.

Jesus Weeps Over Jerusalem

In Luke 19:41-44, as Jesus approaches Jerusalem for the final time, he weeps over the city. This is not generic sadness. It is prophetic lament over what is coming within a single generation.

"If you, even you, had only known on this day what would bring you peace—but now it is hidden from your eyes. The days will come upon you when your enemies will build an embankment against you and encircle you and hem you in on every side. They will dash you to the ground, you and the children within your walls. They will not leave one stone on another, because you did not recognize the time of God's coming to you."

This is not vague distant prophecy. Luke is describing the siege of AD 70 with historical precision—the Roman embankment, the encirclement, the destruction so complete that not one stone would be left on another. Jesus speaks this knowing it will happen within forty years. Luke writes it knowing it already has.

The Kingdom in Your Midst

Luke's Kingdom language is persistently present-tense. The Kingdom is not merely future. It is arriving, breaking in, present in the ministry of Jesus.

  • "The Kingdom of God is in your midst" (17:21)
  • "If I cast out demons by the finger of God, then the Kingdom of God has come upon you" (11:20)
  • "The Law and the Prophets were until John; since then the good news of the Kingdom of God is preached" (16:16)

The Kingdom is not postponed to a distant age. It arrives with Jesus, advances through his ministry, and is established fully through the judgment that vindicates his claims and removes the old covenant order. AD 70 does not inaugurate the Kingdom—it confirms it by dismantling what opposed it.

The Olivet Discourse: Luke 21

Luke 21 is the extended prophetic discourse on the destruction of Jerusalem and the Temple. It parallels Matthew 24 and Mark 13, but Luke's version contains details that make the first-century fulfillment unmistakable.

  • "When you see Jerusalem surrounded by armies, then know that its desolation has come near" (21:20)
  • "They will fall by the edge of the sword and be led captive among all nations, and Jerusalem will be trampled underfoot by the Gentiles" (21:24)
  • "This generation will not pass away until all has taken place" (21:32)

Luke does not spiritualize these events. Jerusalem surrounded by armies is the Roman siege. The falling by the sword is the slaughter inside the city. The trampling by Gentiles is the occupation after the destruction. And all of it happens within the lifespan of the generation Jesus addressed.

Luke 21 is not about the end of the physical world. It is about the end of the covenant world centered in Jerusalem and the Temple. The language is apocalyptic, but the referent is historical.

Daughters of Jerusalem, Weep for Yourselves

On the way to the cross, Jesus turns to the women of Jerusalem and speaks one final warning.

"Daughters of Jerusalem, do not weep for me, but weep for yourselves and for your children. For behold, the days are coming when they will say, 'Blessed are the barren and the wombs that never bore and the breasts that never nursed!' Then they will begin to say to the mountains, 'Fall on us,' and to the hills, 'Cover us.' For if they do these things when the wood is green, what will happen when it is dry?" (23:28-31)

This is not generic suffering. Jesus is speaking of the horrors that will come upon Jerusalem within that generation—starvation so severe that barrenness would be counted a mercy, desperation so complete that death would be preferable to what they would endure. Josephus documents the fulfillment: the famine inside the besieged city, mothers eating their own children, people begging to be killed to escape the suffering.

If this is what happens to the green wood—Jesus, the innocent one—what will happen to the dry wood, the covenant-breaking city that rejected him? The answer came in AD 70.

The Law and the Prophets Until John

Luke 16:16 marks a covenantal turning point. "The Law and the Prophets were until John; since then the good news of the Kingdom of God is preached."

John the Baptist stands at the hinge. The old covenant order—the Law and the Prophets—reached its terminus in his ministry. What follows is not more Law but the gospel of the Kingdom. This is transition language. The old is ending. The new is arriving. The overlap period lasts until the Temple falls, at which point the old is removed entirely and the new stands alone.

The Resurrection and the Opened Scriptures

Luke concludes with the resurrection and Jesus opening the disciples' minds to understand the Scriptures. Everything written about him in the Law of Moses, the Prophets, and the Psalms must be fulfilled.

The fulfillment is not distant. It begins with his death and resurrection and continues through the events the Gospel has been tracing toward—the destruction of the Temple, the vindication of the faithful, the full establishment of the new covenant order.

Luke ends with the ascension and the promise of the Spirit. Acts begins with Pentecost—the New Covenant inauguration. The two volumes together tell the complete story: the Kingdom arriving, the old order condemned, the judgment executed, and the new creation standing as the sole operative reality.

How to Read Luke

  • Take "this generation" literally—the generation Jesus addressed lived to see the judgment fall
  • Read the Kingdom language as present-tense, not merely future
  • Locate the Olivet Discourse in the AD 70 context, not the end of the physical world
  • See Jerusalem's destruction as covenantal judgment, not cosmic catastrophe
  • Read Luke-Acts as a two-volume work tracing the transition from old to new covenant
  • Honour the time statements—Jesus spoke with a forty-year horizon, and Luke writes after it was fulfilled

Why Luke Matters Now

We do not read Luke as people waiting for Jerusalem to fall. The city fell. The Temple burned. The generation Jesus addressed witnessed everything he predicted. We read Luke as the documented account of how it happened—the Kingdom arriving, the old order ending, the judgment executed exactly as promised.

What Luke's first readers were moving toward, we have inherited. The Kingdom they saw breaking in, we live within. The vindication they awaited, we stand on the far side of.

Luke is not a roadmap to future events. It is the testimony of completed events, the record of covenant transition, and the proof that Jesus spoke truly when he said, "This generation will not pass away until all has taken place."
