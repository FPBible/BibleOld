Prophecy Commentary

1) Fishing without Christ produces nothing

The empty nets echo fruitlessness apart from the Messiah. The moment Jesus directs the work, abundance follows.

2) What ended

Leadership defined by merit, performance, or flawless loyalty. In the New Covenant community, failure does not become a permanent identity label.

3) What replaced it

6) "Until I Come" Was a First-Century Expectation

When Peter asks about John's future, Jesus replies, "If it is my will that he remain until I come, what is that to you?" (v. 22). The early church took this to mean John wouldn't die before Jesus returned â€” and they were almost right.

John clarifies that Jesus didn't promise John would never die, only that it was possible for him to remain alive "until I come" (v. 23). This statement only makes sense if Jesus' coming was expected within John's natural lifetime. If "coming" meant a return thousands of years later, why would anyone think John might live to see it?

The answer is simple: Jesus consistently taught that His coming would occur within the generation of His hearers. He told the disciples, "There are some standing here who will not taste death until they see the Son of Man coming in his kingdom" (Matt 16:28). He promised the high priest, "From now on you will see the Son of Man seated at the right hand of Power and coming on the clouds of heaven" (Matt 26:64). And He assured them, "This generation will not pass away until all these things take place" (Matt 24:34).

John did live to see Christ's coming â€” not as a bodily descent from the sky, but as the prophetic fulfillment of Daniel 7 when the Son of Man received His kingdom through the judgment of the old covenant order. That judgment climaxed in AD 70 when Jerusalem fell and the temple was destroyed.

According to early church tradition, John was the last living apostle and wrote his Gospel in the 80s or 90s AD â€” after witnessing the very events Jesus had promised. The conversation in John 21 makes perfect sense when we understand that Jesus' "coming" was a first-century event, not a perpetually postponed future.
