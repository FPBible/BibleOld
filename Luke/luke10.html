Luke 10

The Harvest of Nations • The Fall of the Adversary • The Good Samaritan

1. Immediate Context

In Luke 10, the authority of the Son expands from the core twelve to a broader administrative body of seventy. Within the AD 30–70 transition, this number (70) carries profound legal weight, signifying the reclamation of all the nations of the world (paralleling the table of nations in Genesis 10). The Messiah is no longer just cleansing Israel; He is commissioning a vanguard to announce the King's arrival across the entire global jurisdiction. This mission serves as a legal eviction notice to the spiritual powers that had dominated the nations since the scattering at Babel.

The chapter documents a seismic shift in the spiritual atmosphere. Upon the return of the seventy, the Son declares the "Fall of Satan" from heaven—a definitive legal judgment indicating that the adversary's jurisdictional hold on humanity was being terminated. The chapter concludes with two iconic scenes: the parable of the Good Samaritan and the visit to Martha and Mary. These events redefine the Law from a burden of exhausting performance to a reality of love and established rest. As the AD 70 horizon approached, the Son was teaching His people that the true inheritance of the New Creation is found in "sitting at His feet," leaving behind the anxious toil of a dying religious order.

2. Scripture (WEB)

Source (verbatim): https://ebible.org/web/LUK10.htm

1 Now after these things, the Lord appointed seventy others also, and sent them two by two before his face into every city and place, where he was about to come. 2 Then he said to them, “The harvest is indeed plentiful, but the laborers are few. Pray therefore to the Lord of the harvest, that he may send out laborers into his harvest. 3 Go your ways. Behold, I send you out as lambs among wolves. 4 Carry no purse, nor wallet, nor sandals. Greet no one on the way. 5 Into whatever house you enter, first say, ‘Peace be to this house.’ 6 If a son of peace is there, your peace will rest on him; but if not, it will return to you. 7 Remain in that same house, eating and drinking such things as they give, for the laborer is worthy of his wages. Don’t go from house to house. 8 Into whatever city you enter, and they receive you, eat the things that are set before you. 9 Heal the sick who are therein, and tell them, ‘God’s Kingdom has come near to you.’ 10 But into whatever city you enter, and they don’t receive you, go out into its streets and say, 11 ‘Even the dust from your city that clings to us, we wipe off against you. Nevertheless know this, that God’s Kingdom has come near to you.’ 12 I tell you, it will be more tolerable in that day for Sodom than for that city.
13 “Woe to you, Chorazin! Woe to you, Bethsaida! For if the mighty works had been done in Tyre and Sidon which were done in you, they would have repented long ago, sitting in sackcloth and ashes. 14 But it will be more tolerable for Tyre and Sidon in the judgment than for you. 15 You, Capernaum, who are exalted to heaven, will be brought down to Hades. 16 Whoever listens to you listens to me, and whoever rejects you rejects me. Whoever rejects me rejects him who sent me.”
17 The seventy returned with joy, saying, “Lord, even the demons are subject to us in your name!”
18 He said to them, “I saw Satan having fallen as lightning from heaven. 19 Behold, I give you authority to tread on serpents and scorpions, and over all the power of the enemy. Nothing will in any way hurt you. 20 Nevertheless, don’t rejoice in this, that the spirits are subject to you, but rejoice that your names are written in heaven.”
21 In that same hour Jesus rejoiced in the Holy Spirit, and said, “I thank you, O Father, Lord of heaven and earth, that you hid these things from the wise and understanding, and revealed them to infants. Yes, Father, for so it was well-pleasing in your sight.”
22 “All things have been delivered to me by my Father. No one knows who the Son is, except the Father, and who the Father is, except the Son, and he to whom the Son chooses to reveal him.”
23 Turning to the disciples, he said privately, “Blessed are the eyes which see the things that you see, 24 for I tell you that many prophets and kings desired to see the things which you see, and didn’t see them, and to hear the things which you hear, and didn’t hear them.”
25 Behold, a certain lawyer stood up and tested him, saying, “Teacher, what shall I do to inherit eternal life?”
26 He said to him, “What is written in the law? How do you read it?”
27 He answered, “You shall love the Lord your God with all your heart, with all your soul, with all your strength, and with all your mind; and your neighbor as yourself.”
28 He said to him, “You have answered correctly. Do this, and you will live.”
29 But he, desiring to justify himself, asked Jesus, “Who is my neighbor?”
30 Jesus answered, “A certain man was going down from Jerusalem to Jericho, and he fell among robbers, who both stripped him and beat him, and departed, leaving him half dead. 31 By chance a certain priest was going down that way. When he saw him, he passed by on the other side. 32 In the same way a Levite also, when he came to the place, and saw him, passed by on the other side. 33 But a certain Samaritan, as he traveled, came where he was. When he saw him, he was moved with compassion, 34 came to him, and bound up his wounds, pouring on oil and wine. He set him on his own animal, and brought him to an inn, and took care of him. 35 On the next day, when he departed, he took out two denarii, and gave them to the host, and said to him, ‘Take care of him. Whatever you spend beyond that, I will repay you when I return.’ 36 Now which of these three do you think seemed to be a neighbor to him who fell among the robbers?”
37 He said, “He who showed mercy on him.” Then Jesus said to him, “Go and do likewise.”
38 It happened as they went on their way, he entered into a certain village, and a certain woman named Martha received him into her house. 39 She had a sister called Mary, who also sat at Jesus’ feet, and heard his word. 40 But Martha was distracted with much serving, and she came up to him, and said, “Lord, don’t you care that my sister left me to serve alone? Ask her therefore to help me.”
41 Jesus answered her, “Martha, Martha, you are anxious and troubled about many things, 42 but one thing is needed. Mary has chosen the good part, which will not be taken away from her.”

3. Fulfillment Notes

  • Seventy Others (v. 1): The number of the "Nations" according to Genesis 10. This mission is the formal legal claim of the Son over all ethnicities, not just Israel.
  • Satan Fallen (v. 18): A definitive jurisdictional shift. The "Prince of this World" is legally evicted from his seat of power in heaven as the New Creation's administration (the seventy) begins to operate on earth.
  • Names Written in Heaven (v. 20): Citizenship in the New Creation. This is the permanent status of the Royal Priesthood, which remains valid even as the old earthly scrolls of the Temple were destroyed in AD 70.
  • Reveal the Father (v. 22): The end of mediation. The Son asserts that He is the only source of knowledge regarding the Father, rendering the professional class of priests and lawyers legally redundant.

4. Annotations

  • Serpents and Scorpions (v. 19) — Symbols of the deceptive spiritual powers of the old world. The Son grants His administration jurisdictional immunity over these forces.
  • Tyre, Sidon, and Sodom (vv. 12-14) — Ancient Gentile benchmarks. The Son uses them to show that the rejection of the Kingdom by the covenant people (Chorazin, Bethsaida) carries a higher legal penalty.
  • Oil and Wine (v. 34) — The elements used by the Samaritan. In the transition, these signify the mercy and Spirit of the New World, applied to a humanity left "half dead" by the failure of the old law.
  • Mary’s Part (v. 42) — Agathēn merida. The "Good Portion." A Hebrew idiom for one's inheritance. Mary’s inheritance is the presence of the Sovereign, which "will not be taken away."

5. Fulfillment Commentary

1) The Manifestation of Global Jurisdiction

The commissioning of the seventy (Luke 10:1-12) is a momentous legal expansion. While the twelve represented the tribes of Israel, the seventy represent the global scope of the Son's glory. By sending them "before His face," He is preparing the world for His sovereign arrival. This was the "Harvest" of the transition generation—the gathering of the firstfruits of the New Creation from every city and place. The legal instructions to carry "no purse" and "no sandals" emphasized that the Kingdom is self-sustaining and relies on the King's provision, not the world's economy. Those who reject this message are not merely refusing a philosophy; they are rejecting the jurisdiction of the Sovereign, inviting a judgment more severe than that of Sodom. This event notified the old world that its biological and borders were being superseded by a Kingdom with no limits.

2) The Fall of the Adversary and Heavenly Citizenship

The return of the seventy (Luke 10:17-20) marks a seismic shift in spiritual reality. When they reported that "even the demons are subject to us," the Son responded by unveiling a cosmic legal fact: "I saw Satan having fallen as lightning from heaven." This was the definitive signal that the "Thief" of human sovereignty had been legally evicted. The adversary’s power to accuse and dominate was being stripped in preparation for the finality of AD 70. However, the Son directs their joy away from the exercise of power and toward the fact of their "Names written in heaven." This is the core of the New Creation: citizenship is not found in an earthly genealogy or a Temple scroll, but in the indestructible ledger of the King’s glory. We are no longer defined by our struggle against the "enemy," but by our established standing in the Presence.

3) The Good Samaritan: Mercy vs. The Empty Law

The parable of the Good Samaritan (Luke 10:30-37) is a legal indictment of the Old Covenant "Performance." The man left "half dead" represents a humanity broken by the curse. The Priest and the Levite—the professional guardians of the old system—passed by on the other side, proving that their ritualistic life could not restore humanity. It was a "despised" Samaritan who manifested the heart of the New Creation through compassion, oil, and wine. The Samaritan is a picture of the Son Himself, who enters the "ditch" of our condition to bind our wounds and pay our debt. This story proves that the True Neighbor is the one who acts out of the Sovereign's mercy, rendering the "Performance" of the Priest and Levite legally and morally void. Love is the fulfillment of the Law, and in the New World, this love is our primary jurisdiction.

4) Mary’s Choice: The Established Rest of the Kingdom

The visit to Martha and Mary (Luke 10:38-42) provides the final definition of the New Creation life. Martha, "distracted with much serving," represents the anxious performance of the old religious order—always "troubled about many things," always seeking to appease the Master through toil. Mary, however, chose the "good part": sitting at the Son's feet to hear His word. This is the **Established Rest** of the Kingdom. The Son’s rebuke of Martha was a compassionate signal that the era of anxious performance was ending. In the New World, we do not work *for* the King's favor; we live *from* the King's presence. Mary’s choice is the permanent portion of the Royal Priesthood. It is an inheritance that "will not be taken away," even when the earthly houses of service and the Temple of toil were physically removed in AD 70.

8. New Creation Reality

In Luke 10, the glory of the Son is revealed as a power that evicts the adversary and establishes a global kingdom of rest. As a citizen of the New Creation, your "Name is written in heaven." You are not defined by your "labor" or your ability to keep a religious schedule. You have been granted jurisdictional authority over the chaos of the old world, but your greatest joy is found in your permanent standing with the Father. You are the recipient of the Samaritan's mercy, and you have been invited into Mary’s portion: sitting at the feet of the Sovereign. The "Thief" of anxious performance has been defeated. You live in a world where the King has already paid the "innkeeper" for your care, and your only requirement is to abide in His established peace.

9. Reflection

Question: If the Son declared that "only one thing is needed" (Luke 10:42)—sitting at His feet—why do we still allow modern "leaders" to bury us under the "Martha-toil" of religious programs, tithes, and exhausting performance?
