Matthew 4

Testing, Triumph, and the Kingdom at Hand

Immediate Context

Matthew 4 follows the public anointing of Jesus and immediately places Him in the wilderness. Where Israel failed in the wilderness under the Law, Jesus succeeds as the True Son. This chapter marks the decisive confrontation between the old covenant powers and the incoming Kingdom.

Scripture (WEB)

1Then Jesus was led up by the Spirit into the wilderness to be tempted by the devil.

2When he had fasted forty days and forty nights, he was hungry afterward.

3The tempter came and said to him, “If you are the Son of God, command that these stones become bread.”

4But he answered, “It is written, ‘Man shall not live by bread alone, but by every word that proceeds out of the mouth of God.’”

5Then the devil took him into the holy city. He set him on the pinnacle of the temple,

6and said to him, “If you are the Son of God, throw yourself down, for it is written, ‘He will command his angels concerning you,’ and, ‘On their hands they will bear you up, so that you don’t dash your foot against a stone.’”

7Jesus said to him, “Again, it is written, ‘You shall not test the Lord, your God.’”

8Again, the devil took him to an exceedingly high mountain, and showed him all the kingdoms of the world, and their glory.

9He said to him, “I will give you all of these things, if you will fall down and worship me.”

10Then Jesus said to him, “Get behind me, Satan! For it is written, ‘You shall worship the Lord your God, and you shall serve him only.’”

11Then the devil left him, and behold, angels came and served him.

12Now when Jesus heard that John was delivered up, he withdrew into Galilee.

13Leaving Nazareth, he came and lived in Capernaum, which is by the sea, in the region of Zebulun and Naphtali,

14that it might be fulfilled which was spoken through Isaiah the prophet, saying,

15“The land of Zebulun and the land of Naphtali, toward the sea, beyond the Jordan, Galilee of the Gentiles,

16the people who sat in darkness saw a great light; to those who sat in the region and shadow of death, to them light has dawned.”

17From that time, Jesus began to preach, and to say, “Repent! For the Kingdom of Heaven is at hand.”

18Walking by the sea of Galilee, he saw two brothers: Simon, who is called Peter, and Andrew, his brother, casting a net into the sea; for they were fishermen.

19He said to them, “Come after me, and I will make you fishers for men.”

20They immediately left their nets and followed him.

21Going on from there, he saw two other brothers, James the son of Zebedee, and John his brother, in the boat with Zebedee their father, mending their nets. He called them.

22They immediately left the boat and their father, and followed him.

23Jesus went about in all Galilee, teaching in their synagogues, preaching the Good News of the Kingdom, and healing every disease and every sickness among the people.

24The report about him went out into all Syria. They brought to him all who were sick, afflicted with various diseases and torments, possessed with demons, epileptics, and paralytics; and he healed them.

25Great multitudes from Galilee, Decapolis, Jerusalem, Judea, and from beyond the Jordan followed him.

Annotations

Devil / Satan: The adversarial power of the old covenant order opposing the Son.
Forty days: Recapitulates Israel’s wilderness testing; Jesus succeeds where Israel failed.
Holy city / Temple: The center of the Mosaic system soon to be judged.
Kingdom of Heaven at hand: First-century imminence of covenant transition.
Great light: The arrival of New Creation life into Israel’s darkness.

Fulfillment Notes

  • The wilderness testing mirrors Israel’s failure and signals Jesus as the faithful Son.
  • The temptation scenes confront the authority of the Temple-centered order.
  • “Kingdom at hand” refers to the imminent end of the Mosaic age.
  • Isaiah’s light prophecy is fulfilled in Jesus’ Galilean ministry.

Fulfillment Commentary

1. The True Son in the Wilderness

Matthew 4 intentionally presents Jesus as the faithful Israel. The forty-day fast recalls Israel’s forty years, but where the nation failed under the Law, Jesus succeeds under obedience. This is covenantal substitution: Jesus stands in Israel’s place and fulfills what the old covenant people could not.

2. The Adversary as Covenant Opposition

The “devil” is not a red-horned cartoon villain but the adversarial power embedded in the old order—accusation, testing, and conditional sonship. Each temptation questions Jesus’ identity as Son, echoing Israel’s own wilderness testing. Jesus answers not with new revelation, but with Scripture rightly applied, demonstrating that the Law itself pointed beyond its own era.

3. Temple, Kingdoms, and Authority

The pinnacle of the Temple scene is crucial. The final battleground of the old covenant was not Rome, but Jerusalem. By refusing to prove Himself through Temple spectacle, Jesus signals that the authority of the Temple system is ending. The offer of “all the kingdoms” exposes the false power structures competing with the Kingdom of God.

4. Light Dawns Before Judgment

Jesus’ move to Galilee fulfills Isaiah’s promise that light would dawn before destruction. The New Creation does not begin in Jerusalem’s corridors of power but among the marginalized regions of Israel. This light precedes the coming judgment of AD 70 and offers escape for those who follow the Son.

5. The Kingdom Announced, Not Delayed

“Repent, for the Kingdom of Heaven is at hand” is not an open-ended invitation stretched over millennia. It is a time-bound announcement. Within that generation, the old covenant world would pass, and the Kingdom would stand complete. The calling of the disciples signals the formation of a new people who will carry the Kingdom beyond Israel into the nations.

New Creation Reality

The testing is over. The Son has prevailed. We live beyond the wilderness in the settled Kingdom. There is no accusation left to answer, no covenant to pass, and no Kingdom still waiting to arrive.
