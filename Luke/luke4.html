Luke 4

The Last Adam • The Year of Release • The Synagogue Departure

1. Immediate Context

Luke 4 presents the legal confrontation between the New Creation and the "powers" of the old age. Following his baptism, Jesus is led into the wilderness to face the same trials where Israel—the "son" of the Old Covenant—failed. This is the legal testing of the Last Adam. By quoting Deuteronomy to the accuser, Jesus demonstrates that the Word of God is the true sustenance, effectively undoing the Adamic curse through perfect legal compliance.

The scene then shifts to Nazareth, where Jesus enters the synagogue—the local center of religious mediation. He reads from Isaiah 61, declaring the "Year of the Lord’s Favor" (the Jubilee). This is a formal declaration of legal amnesty and release from the debt of the Law. The violent rejection he faces from the hometown religious crowd serves as a microcosm of the AD 70 horizon: the old system cannot coexist with the reality of a finished work. He bypasses their attempt to kill him, signifying that his life and his Kingdom are not subject to the institutional "thief."

2. Scripture (WEB)

Source: https://ebible.org/web/LUK04.htm

1 Jesus, full of the Holy Spirit, returned from the Jordan, and was led by the Spirit into the wilderness 2 for forty days, being tempted by the devil. He ate nothing in those days. Afterward, when they were completed, he was hungry. 3 The devil said to him, “If you are the Son of God, command this stone to become bread.” 4 Jesus answered him, saying, “It is written, ‘Every man shall not live by bread alone, but by every word of God.’”
5 The devil, leading him up on a high mountain, showed him all the kingdoms of the world in a moment of time. 6 The devil said to him, “I will give you all this authority, and their glory, for it has been delivered to me; and I give it to whomever I want. 7 If you therefore will worship before me, it will all be yours.” 8 Jesus answered him, “Get behind me, Satan! For it is written, ‘You shall worship the Lord your God, and him only shall you serve.’”
9 He led him to Jerusalem, and set him on the pinnacle of the temple, and said to him, “If you are the Son of God, cast yourself down from here, 10 for it is written, ‘He will give his angels charge concerning you, to guard you;’ 11 and, ‘On their hands they will bear you up, lest perhaps you dash your foot against a stone.’” 12 Jesus answering, said to him, “It has been said, ‘You shall not tempt the Lord your God.’” 13 When the devil had completed every temptation, he departed from him until another time.
14 Jesus returned in the power of the Spirit into Galilee, and news about him spread through all the surrounding area. 15 He taught in their synagogues, being glorified by all. 16 He came to Nazareth, where he had been brought up. He entered, as was his custom, into the synagogue on the Sabbath day, and stood up to read. 17 The book of the prophet Isaiah was handed to him. He opened the book, and found the place where it was written, 18 “The Spirit of the Lord is on me, because he has anointed me to preach good news to the poor. He has sent me to heal the brokenhearted, to proclaim release to the captives, recovering of sight to the blind, to deliver those who are crushed, 19 and to proclaim the acceptable year of the Lord.” 20 He closed the book, gave it back to the attendant, and sat down. The eyes of all in the synagogue were fastened on him. 21 He began to tell them, “Today, this Scripture has been fulfilled in your hearing.”
22 All testified about him, and wondered at the gracious words which proceeded out of his mouth. They said, “Isn’t this Joseph’s son?” 23 He said to them, “Doubtless you will tell me this proverb, ‘Physician, heal yourself! Whatever we have heard done at Capernaum, do also here in your hometown.’” 24 He said, “Most certainly I tell you, no prophet is acceptable in his hometown. 25 But I tell you in truth, there were many widows in Israel in the days of Elijah, when the sky was shut up three years and six months, when a great famine came over all the land. 26 Elijah was sent to none of them, except to Zarephath, in the land of Sidon, to a woman who was a widow. 27 There were many lepers in Israel in the time of Elisha the prophet, yet not one of them was cleansed, except Naaman the Syrian.”
28 They were all filled with wrath in the synagogue, as they heard these things. 29 They rose up, threw him out of the city, and led him to the brow of the hill that their city was built on, that they might throw him off the cliff. 30 But he, passing through the middle of them, went his way. 31 He came down to Capernaum, a city of Galilee. He was teaching them on the Sabbath day... 33 In the synagogue there was a man who had a spirit of an unclean demon; and he cried out with a loud voice... 43 But he said to them, “I must preach the good news of the Kingdom of God to the other cities also. For this reason I have been sent.” 44 He was preaching in the synagogues of Galilee.

3. Fulfillment Notes

  • Forty Days (v. 2): Matches the 40 years Israel wandered in the wilderness and the 40-year transition (AD 30–70). Jesus fulfills the testing of the "corporate son" in the timeframe allotted.
  • Kingdoms of the World (v. 5): Oikoumene. The Roman-centric order. The adversary claims legal custody of the old age's political systems, which Jesus will legally reclaim through the Cross.
  • Release (v. 18): Greek aphesis. Used legally for the cancellation of a debt or the pardon of a prisoner. Jesus declares the legal end of the Law's "debt."
  • Acceptable Year of the Lord (v. 19): The Jubilee. According to Leviticus 25, this was when slaves were freed and debts were cancelled. Jesus identifies his ministry as the legal Jubilee for the New Creation.

4. Annotations

  • Pinnacle of the Temple (v. 9) — The adversary attempts to use the "thief's" headquarters as a place of temptation. Jesus refuses to validate the institutional Temple through a spectacle.
  • Today (v. 21) — Sēmeron. The definitive audience-relevance marker. The fulfillment was not "someday" or "not yet"; it was that specific day.
  • Widows and Lepers (vv. 25-27) — Elijah and Elisha bypassing Israel to heal Gentiles. This is a direct legal threat to the religious monopoly of the synagogue, predicting the inclusion of the nations in the New Creation.
  • Middle of them (v. 30) — A sign of sovereign authority. The religious mob has no legal power over the King until the "hour" of the transition's climax.

5. Fulfillment Commentary

1) The Legal Triumph of the Last Adam

In the wilderness, Jesus is tested as the representative of the new humanity. Where the first Adam failed over food, and Israel failed over testing God, Jesus uses the "Word" as the legal shield (v. 4). He proves he is the one qualified to legally "shut down" the old Adamic era. This wasn't just a moral victory; it was a legal prerequisite for the New Creation.

2) The Jubilee Proclamation

By reading Isaiah 61, Jesus issues a formal legal decree. The "Good News" (v. 18) is the news that the debt of the Law—the "handwriting of ordinances" that was against us—is being legally cancelled. He does not announce a new set of rules; he announces a "Release." This is the core of "Established Rest."

3) The Synagogue Rejection and the AD 70 Horizon

The wrath of the Nazareth crowd (v. 28) is the instinctive reaction of the "Thief." When the religious establishment hears that God's grace is not restricted to their institution, their only response is violence. This foreshadows the final rejection of the Messiah by the nation, which led to the "fire" of AD 70. Jesus’s departure from their presence is the legal "leaving desolate" of the old system.

4) Authority over the "Powers"

The healing of the demon-possessed man (v. 33) is a demonstration of the New Kingdom's legal jurisdiction. The "unclean spirits" of the old world recognize the King and know their time is short. This is the "binding of the strong man" in preparation for the complete plunder of the old age's house.

8. New Creation Reality

In Luke 4, the "Today" of fulfillment has dawned (v. 21). You are not living in the "Temptation Wilderness"—you are living in the "Proclaimed Release." The debt of the Law has been legally cancelled; the "Year of Favor" is your permanent environment. You do not need to perform to be "acceptable," because the King has already proclaimed you so. You do not belong to the Synagogue of performance; you belong to the Kingdom of Rest. The "Thief" has no legal claim over you, and his authority has been stripped in the presence of the Spirit.
