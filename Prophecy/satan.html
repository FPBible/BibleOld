Satan

The Adversary as Accuser and Covenant Opponent

“Satan” is a title, not a personal name. It means “adversary” or “accuser.” Scripture uses it functionally, not mythologically.

1. Satan in the Hebrew Scriptures

In the Old Testament, “the satan” appears as an accuser within God’s court (Job 1–2; Zech 3). It is not a rebel deity. It operates within the covenant framework as a prosecuting role.

2. Satan in the Gospels

In the Gospels, Satan represents the **accusatory power** working through religious leadership, law-keeping identity, and resistance to Jesus.

Jesus confronts Satan not by exorcising a cosmic being, but by exposing and dismantling the system of accusation, condemnation, and death.

3. “The Ruler of This World”

Jesus says the ruler of this world is being judged and cast out (John 12). This refers to the authority structure rooted in the old covenant order.

Satan’s power depends on:

  • law
  • accusation
  • condemnation
  • fear of death

When the law’s authority ends, the accuser loses his standing.

4. The Defeat of Satan

The New Testament consistently places Satan’s defeat within the ministry of Jesus and the early church:

  • the strong man is bound
  • authority is taken away
  • accusation is silenced

This is not postponed to the end of time. It is tied to the cross, the resurrection, and the removal of the old covenant system.

Summary

“Satan” names the adversarial, accusatory power embedded in a covenant system that condemned humanity. That power was exposed, judged, and disarmed in Christ.
