Daniel 10

The Invisible War • The Prince of Persia • The Vision of the Man in Linen

1. Immediate Context

Chapters 10, 11, and 12 are actually a single, continuous vision. Chapter 10 serves as the prologue. The year is the third year of Cyrus, King of Persia. The Jewish exiles have begun returning to Jerusalem, but they are facing intense local opposition, and the rebuilding of the temple has stalled. Daniel, heavily burdened by this, goes into a three-week fast.

By the Tigris river, Daniel receives a terrifying vision of a divine being. The angelic messenger reveals to Daniel that his prayer was heard on the very first day, but the answer was delayed by a 21-day cosmic battle against the demonic "Prince of Persia." This chapter demonstrates that earthly geopolitical struggles are directly tied to invisible spiritual conflicts.

2. Scripture (WEB)

Source (verbatim): https://ebible.org/web/DAN10.htm

Note: The following text highlights the core narrative (vv. 1-6, 12-14, 20-21) for study.

1 In the third year of Cyrus king of Persia a thing was revealed to Daniel, whose name was called Belteshazzar; and the thing was true, even a great warfare. He understood the thing, and had understanding of the vision.
2 In those days I, Daniel, was mourning three whole weeks.
3 I ate no pleasant bread, neither came flesh nor wine into my mouth, neither did I anoint myself at all, until three whole weeks were fulfilled.
4 In the twenty-fourth day of the first month, as I was by the side of the great river, which is Hiddekel,
5 I lifted up my eyes, and looked, and behold, a man clothed in linen, whose waist was adorned with pure gold of Uphaz.
6 His body also was like beryl, and his face as the appearance of lightning, and his eyes as flaming torches, and his arms and his feet like burnished brass, and the voice of his words like the voice of a multitude.
...
12 Then he said to me, “Don’t be afraid, Daniel; for from the first day that you set your heart to understand, and to humble yourself before your God, your words were heard. I have come for your words’ sake.
13 But the prince of the kingdom of Persia withstood me twenty-one days; but, behold, Michael, one of the chief princes, came to help me because I remained there with the kings of Persia.
14 Now I have come to make you understand what will happen to your people in the latter days; for the vision is yet for many days.”
...
20 Then he said, “Do you know why I have come to you? Now I will return to fight with the prince of Persia. When I go out, behold, the prince of Greece will come.
21 But I will tell you that which is inscribed in the writing of truth. There is no one who holds with me against these, but Michael your prince.”

3. Fulfillment Notes

  • The "Latter Days" Context (v. 14): The angel explicitly states that this vision concerns what will happen to Daniel's people (Israel) in the "latter days." In biblical covenant language, the "latter days" or "last days" consistently refers to the closing period of the Old Covenant age (from the arrival of Christ to the destruction of Jerusalem in AD 70), not the end of planet Earth.
  • The Typology of the Vision (vv. 5-6): The description of the "man clothed in linen" with eyes like flaming torches and a voice like a multitude is practically identical to John's vision of the ascended Jesus in Revelation 1. Whether this is a Christophany (a pre-incarnate appearance of Christ) or a high-ranking angel representing Him, it directly connects Daniel's apocalypse to John's apocalypse.
  • Principalities and Powers: This chapter confirms that the human empires (the Beast systems) of Babylon, Persia, Greece, and Rome are animated and driven by invisible demonic intelligence. The "Prince of Persia" is not a human king, but a spiritual entity assigned to manipulate that empire against God's covenant people.

4. Annotations

  • a great warfare — The text literally translates to "a great conflict" or "a great tribulation." This sets the tone for chapters 11 and 12, which detail the horrific persecution the Jews would face under Antiochus Epiphanes and later Rome.
  • mourning three whole weeks — A period of 21 days. Daniel's earthly fast directly mirrored the 21-day battle happening in the heavenly realm.
  • Michael, one of the chief princes — Michael is consistently identified as the archangel assigned to protect the nation of Israel. He appears again in Daniel 12:1 to stand up during the great tribulation, and in Revelation 12 to cast Satan out of heaven.
  • the prince of Greece — As the Medo-Persian empire falls, the angelic battle will shift to confront the demonic power behind the rising Greek empire (the "Goat" of chapter 8).

5. Fulfillment Commentary

1) The Glory of the Heavenly Messenger (vv. 4–9)

The visual majesty of this figure is overwhelming. Daniel, who had faced lions and stared down Babylonian dictators without flinching, completely collapses at the sight of this being. All his strength leaves him.

Compare this to Revelation 1:12-17. When the Apostle John saw the glorified Christ, with eyes like flames of fire and feet like burnished bronze, he too fell at His feet as though dead. This establishes a profound biblical truth: a true encounter with the unveiled glory of heaven always produces deep humility and the shattering of human self-reliance.

2) The Reality of Unseen Warfare (vv. 12–14)

This is one of the most revealing glimpses into the spiritual realm in all of Scripture. God dispatched the answer to Daniel's prayer on the very first day. But the messenger was intercepted in the heavenly places by the "Prince of Persia."

We must understand that the human emperors—Cyrus, Alexander the Great, Nero—are not operating in a vacuum. Behind the throne of the empire (the "Beast") is the "Dragon" (Satan and his principalities) giving it power and authority (Rev 13:2). The delay in Daniel's answer wasn't because God didn't care; it was because a literal cosmic war was being waged over the destiny of God's covenant people.

3) Preparation for the "Latter Days" (v. 14)

The angel clarifies his mission: "Now I have come to make you understand what will happen to your people in the latter days."

Futurists read "latter days" and think "the 21st century." But the biblical writers did not view history that way. The "latter days" was the prophesied time when the Old Covenant system would face its final, agonizing transition into the New Covenant Kingdom.

The writer of Hebrews confirms this exact timeline: "God... has in these last days spoken to us by his Son" (Heb 1:1-2). Peter stood up on the Day of Pentecost and declared, "This is what was spoken by the prophet Joel: 'And it shall come to pass in the last days...'" (Acts 2:16-17). Daniel 10 is preparing us for the climax of the Old Covenant age, which culminated in the first century.

4) The Shifting of Empires (vv. 20–21)

The angel tells Daniel that the fight with the Prince of Persia is not over, and once that is finished, the Prince of Greece will arrive. The heavenly warfare perfectly tracks with the earthly timeline established in Daniel 2 and Daniel 8. As the geopolitical empires clash on earth (Persia falling to Greece), their demonic counterparts are clashing in the heavens.

But Israel is not unprotected. Michael, the great prince, stands with the heavenly messenger. This guarantees that despite the horrific turbulence of the coming centuries (detailed in Chapter 11), the faithful remnant of Israel will survive to see the arrival of the Messiah.

8. New Creation Reality

The intense, cosmic warfare surrounding the transition of the Old Covenant is finished. Christ has disarmed the principalities and powers, making a public spectacle of them at the cross (Colossians 2:15). You are reading covenant history for assurance, not headlines for doom. You do not need to fear the "princes" of modern nations or the chaos of global politics, because the true King has already seated you with Him in the heavenly places, far above all rule and authority (Ephesians 2:6).
