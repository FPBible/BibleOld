Preface to Revelation

The non-negotiable rules for reading the Apocalypse.

Audience Relevance Apocalyptic Genre Covenant Judgment Fulfilled Transition

This preface establishes the non-negotiable rules for reading the Book of Revelation. Everything that follows in this commentary module is strictly governed by these principles.

1. What Revelation Is ‚Äî And Is Not

Revelation is apocalyptic prophecy written to real, first-century churches located in Asia Minor. These historical congregations were facing imminent persecution, local compromise, and a massive approaching covenantal crisis.

It is not a timeline of modern geopolitics. It is not a prediction of future 21st-century technology (microchips, helicopters, or nuclear weapons). It is not a coded forecast of events happening in our modern news cycle.

When Revelation is divorced from its original historical audience, it becomes infinitely flexible. It loses all interpretive control and devolves into a tool for fearmongering and speculative fiction. We must respect the original audience.

2. Time-Frame Lock

The Book of Revelation explicitly states‚Äîin both its opening sentence and its closing chapter‚Äîthat its contents concerned events that were going to happen soon and that the time was near.

  • Revelation 1:1, 3: "things which must happen soon... for the time is near."
  • Revelation 22:10: "Don't seal up the words of the prophecy of this book, for the time is near."

If a modern prophet told you an event was going to happen "soon," but secretly meant it wouldn't happen for 2,000+ years, you would rightfully call him a false prophet. At FPBible, these divine time-statements are taken at face value. The fulfillment of Revelation is firmly anchored in the first-century covenant transition, culminating in the violent fall of the Old Covenant system in AD 70.

3. Genre: Apocalyptic Language

Revelation communicates through signs, visions, numbers, and dense imagery drawn entirely from the Old Testament prophets. You cannot read it like a textbook or a modern historical narrative.

  • Cosmic Imagery (stars falling, sun darkening) describes political and covenantal upheaval, not literal astronomy.
  • Beasts represent oppressive kingdoms, empires, and power structures.
  • Cities represent covenant identity (the unfaithful Harlot City vs. the faithful Bride/New Jerusalem).

Literalism is applied only where the text explicitly demands it. Symbolism based on Old Testament prophetic idiom is the default operating mode.

4. The Covenant Framework

Revelation is not a book about the end of the physical planet. It portrays the final, violent confrontation between two distinct covenant worlds:

  • The Old Covenant System: Apostate, unfaithful, persecuting the early church, and ultimately judged by God.
  • The New Covenant Reality: Faithful, persecuted but ultimately vindicated, and permanently established.

Judgment language in Revelation refers to the historical removal of the old order. Victory language refers to the public, undeniable unveiling of Christ‚Äôs established reign over the nations.

5. The Purpose of this Commentary

This Revelation module exists to:

  • Clarify the meaning of ancient apocalyptic imagery using the rest of Scripture.
  • Anchor the interpretation firmly in first-century historical fulfillment.
  • Prevent futurist drift and end-times anxiety.
  • Present Revelation exactly as it was intended: as a book of absolute victory, assurance, and comfort for the believer, not a book of fear.

6. Reader Responsibility

Readers are expected to approach Revelation on its own terms. Importing modern assumptions, speculative futuristic timelines, or fear-based interpretations undermines the authority of the text and completely obscures its message.

Revelation does not end in escape, planetary destruction, or a 2,000-year delay. It ends in presence, restoration, and the unshakable reality of New Creation life.
