1 Peter 4

The Imminent End and the House of God Judgment

Horizon: Is at Hand Event: Judgment of the House

Scripture (WEB)

7But the end of all things is at hand. Therefore be of sound mind, self-controlled, and sober in prayer. 12Beloved, don’t be astonished at the fiery trial which has come upon you, to test you, as though a strange thing happened to you. 13But because you are partakers of Christ’s sufferings, rejoice; that at the revelation of his glory you may rejoice with exceeding joy. 17For the time has come for judgment to begin at the house of God. If it begins first with us, what will happen to those who don’t obey the Good News of God? 18"If it is hard for the righteous to be saved, what will happen to the ungodly and the sinner?"

Fulfillment Commentary

The End is "At Hand" (v. 7)

Peter makes one of the most direct time-statements in the New Testament: "The end of all things is at hand." In the Greek (engiken), this means the event had drawn near and was virtually present.

Peter was not predicting the end of the physical universe, but the "end of all things" pertaining to the Old Covenant age—the Temple, the priesthood, and the sacrificial system. If "at hand" meant "2,000+ years away," Peter would have been a false prophet. From a fulfilled perspective, Peter was accurately warning his readers that the AD 70 destruction of Jerusalem was imminent.

The Fiery Trial (v. 12)

The "fiery trial" was the intense persecution breaking out in the mid-60s AD (specifically under Nero). Peter tells his readers not to be surprised; they were living in the transitional "birth pangs" that Jesus predicted in the Olivet Discourse. This testing was the precursor to the "revelation of his glory"—the public vindication of the Christian faith and the visible judgment of its opposers.

Judgment at the House of God (v. 17-18)

Peter declares that "the time has come" (present tense in the mid-60s) for judgment to begin at the house of God. In a covenantal context, the "house of God" referred primarily to the Temple in Jerusalem and the people of Israel.

The "saved" in verse 18 refers to the physical escape and spiritual vindication of the faithful remnant during the Roman siege. Jesus promised that "not a hair of your head shall perish" (Luke 21:18) for those who followed His instructions to flee. The "ungodly and the sinner" faced the full weight of the covenantal wrath that ended the Mosaic age.
