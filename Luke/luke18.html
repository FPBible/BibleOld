Luke 18

Justice for the Chosen • The Humility of the Tax Collector • The Blind Man’s Sight

1. Immediate Context

Luke 18 serves as the legal preparation for the King’s final entry into Jerusalem. During the AD 30–70 transition, the "little flock" was facing increasing pressure and persecution from the old religious establishment. The Son addresses this by teaching on the persistent widow, whose appeal for justice mirrors the remnant’s cry for the finality of the New Creation. This chapter defines the character of those who inhabit the New World: they are not the self-justified performers of the Law, but the humble who recognize their need for sovereign mercy.

The chapter documents a series of critical "entrance" requirements for the Kingdom. From the welcoming of children to the warning for the rich ruler, the Messiah makes it clear that the New Creation is entered by those who abandon their old-world assets and trust. As the AD 70 horizon approached, the Son's healing of the blind man at Jericho acted as a final manifest sign—giving sight to those who truly saw His identity as the Son of David. This chapter marks the closing of the jurisdictional gap between the old system and the King who was about to finalize the transition through His own death and resurrection.

2. Scripture (WEB)

Source (verbatim): https://ebible.org/web/LUK18.htm

1 He also spoke a parable to them that they must always pray, and not give up, 2 saying, “There was a judge in a certain city who didn’t fear God, and didn’t respect man. 3 A widow was in that city, and she kept coming to him, saying, ‘Avenge me of my adversary!’ 4 He wouldn’t for a while, but afterward he said within himself, ‘Though I don’t fear God, nor respect man, 5 yet because this widow bothers me, I will avenge her, lest she wear me out by her continual coming.’”
6 The Lord said, “Listen to what the unrighteous judge says. 7 Won’t God avenge his chosen ones, who are crying out to him day and night, and yet he exercises patience with them? 8 I tell you that he will avenge them quickly. Nevertheless, when the Son of Man comes, will he find faith on the earth?”
9 He also spoke this parable to certain people who were convinced of their own righteousness, and who despised all others. 10 “Two men went up into the temple to pray; one was a Pharisee, and the other was a tax collector. 11 The Pharisee stood and prayed to himself like this: ‘God, I thank you, that I am not like the rest of men, extortioners, unrighteous, adulterers, or even like this tax collector. 12 I fast twice a week. I give tithes of all that I get.’ 13 But the tax collector, standing far away, wouldn’t even lift up his eyes to heaven, but beat his breast, saying, ‘God, be merciful to me, a sinner!’ 14 I tell you, this man went down to his house justified rather than the other; for everyone who exalts himself will be humbled, but he who humbles himself will be exalted.”
15 They were also bringing their babies to him, that he might touch them. But when the disciples saw it, they rebuked them. 16 Jesus summoned them, saying, “Allow the little children to come to me, and don’t hinder them, for God’s Kingdom belongs to such as these. 17 Most certainly, I tell you, whoever doesn’t receive God’s Kingdom like a little child, he will in no way enter into it.”
18 A certain ruler asked him, saying, “Good Teacher, what shall I do to inherit eternal life?” 19 Jesus said to him, “Why do you call me good? No one is good, except one—God. 20 You know the commandments: ‘Don’t commit adultery,’ ‘Don’t murder,’ ‘Don’t steal,’ ‘Don’t give false testimony,’ ‘Honor your father and your mother.’”
21 He said, “I have observed all these things from my youth up.” 22 When Jesus heard it, he said to him, “One thing you still lack. Sell all that you have, and distribute it to the poor. You will have treasure in heaven. Come, follow me.” 23 But when he heard these things, he became very sad, for he was very rich.
24 Jesus, seeing that he became very sad, said, “How difficult it is for those who have riches to enter into God’s Kingdom! 25 For it is easier for a camel to enter in through a needle’s eye, than for a rich man to enter into God’s Kingdom.” 26 Those who heard it said, “Then who can be saved?” 27 But he said, “The things which are impossible with men are possible with God.”
28 Peter said, “Behold, we have left everything and followed you.” 29 He said to them, “Most certainly I tell you, there is no one who has left house, or wife, or brothers, or parents, or children, for God’s Kingdom’s sake, 30 who will not receive many times more in this time, and in the world to come, eternal life.”
31 He took the twelve aside, and said to them, “Behold, we are going up to Jerusalem, and all the things that are written through the prophets concerning the Son of Man will be completed. 32 For he will be delivered up to the Gentiles, will be mocked, shamefully treated, and spit on. 33 They will scourge and kill him. On the third day, he will rise again.” 34 They understood none of these things. This saying was hidden from them, and they didn’t perceive the things that were said.
35 It happened, as he drew near to Jericho, that a certain blind man sat by the wayside, begging. 36 Hearing a multitude going by, he asked what this meant. 37 They told him that Jesus of Nazareth was passing by. 38 He cried out, saying, “Jesus, you son of David, have mercy on me!” 39 Those who went before rebuked him, that he should be quiet, but he cried out all the more, “You son of David, have mercy on me!” 40 Standing still, Jesus commanded him to be brought to him. When he had come near, he asked him, 41 “What do you want me to do for you?” He said, “Lord, that I may see again.” 42 Jesus said to him, “Receive your sight. Your faith has made you well.” 43 Immediately he received his sight, and followed him, glorifying God. All the people, when they saw it, praised God.

3. Fulfillment Notes

  • Avenge Them Quickly (v. 8): A prophetic reference to the AD 70 judgment. The cry for justice from the transition remnant was answered "quickly" (within their generation) through the dissolution of the old covenant system.
  • Faith on the Earth (v. 8): Refers to the "earth" (Land) of Israel. Would the remnant remain faithful to the King’s finished work as the clouds of judgment gathered over the Land?
  • This Time / World to Come (v. 30): en tō kairō toutō. The transition generation was receiving the "many times more" during their time, leading into the "world to come" (the New Covenant age) after AD 70.
  • All Things Completed (v. 31): The legal exhaustion of the prophets. Every prophecy regarding the Messiah’s suffering and entry into glory was being finalized during this specific transition.

4. Annotations

  • Avenge me of my Adversary (v. 3) — The "adversary" was the old world system that persecuted the New Creation remnant. The King’s judgment provides the legal "avenging."
  • The Temple (v. 10) — The setting for the contrast between the old "Performance" and the new "Mercy." The tax collector’s justification occurred *in* the very place the Pharisee assumed he owned.
  • Needle’s Eye (v. 25) — A symbol of the impossibility of entering the Kingdom based on old-world assets. Only through the "possible" sovereign act of God can the transition be completed.
  • Son of David (v. 38) — The specific royal title of the Messiah. The blind man’s use of this title was a legal recognition of the King’s sovereign authority over the House of Israel.

5. Fulfillment Commentary

1) The Manifestation of Sovereign Justice

The parable of the persistent widow (Luke 18:1-8) unveils the Son’s glory through the promise of certain justice. The widow represents the vulnerable remnant of the transition generation, crying out against the "adversary" of the religious and political establishment. Unlike the unrighteous judge, the Father is moved by His own nature to "avenge His chosen ones." This was not a promise of distant cosmic justice, but of a historical "quick" relief. The AD 70 destruction was the legal answer to the blood of the martyrs, finalizing the foreclosure of the system that had oppressed the New Creation. The Son’s glory is seen in His commitment to His chosen people, ensuring that their cry for the "Kingdom to come" was answered with the historical reality of the old world’s removal.

2) Justification Through Mercy: The End of Performance

The contrast between the Pharisee and the tax collector (Luke 18:9-14) is the legal summary of the two worldviews colliding during the transition. The Pharisee stood in the Temple and "prayed to himself," cataloging his "Performance" of the Law (tithing, fasting, social status). He believed his status was earned. The tax collector, however, appealed only to the "Mercy" of the Sovereign. Jesus’s verdict—that the tax collector went home "justified"—was a radical jurisdictional shift. It proved that in the New Creation, standing is not a result of human effort but of divine pardon. The glory of the New World is its accessibility to the humble. The Pharisee remained trapped in his own record, while the tax collector inhabited the rest of the King’s favor.

3) Counting the Impossible Cost of the Kingdom

The encounter with the rich ruler (Luke 18:18-27) exposes the legal barrier of old-world security. The ruler believed he had "observed" the commandments, yet he lacked the one thing necessary for the New Creation: total dependence on the Son. His "riches" were the anchor that kept him from entering the transition. Jesus's warning about the "needle’s eye" was a historical reality: entering the Kingdom meant abandoning the biological and economic assets of the dying Mosaic order. For the rich, this was humanly "impossible." But the glory of the Son is seen in His declaration that the "impossible" is "possible with God." The New Creation is a world birthed by a sovereign act, where the "treasure in heaven" replaces the fading coins of the old age, ensuring a supply that judgment cannot touch.

4) The Sight of the Royal Son

The healing of the blind man at Jericho (Luke 18:35-43) is the exultant finale of this chapter. As the King approached Jerusalem to "complete all things," He stopped to give sight to one who saw His royal identity by faith. While the multitudes saw only "Jesus of Nazareth," the blind man cried out to the "Son of David." This recognition was the "faith" the Son was looking for on the Land. The man’s immediate restoration and his subsequent "following" of the King act as the blueprint for the New Creation believer. We were once blind to the finished reality of the transition, but through the Son’s word, we now "see again." The glory of the King is His power to open eyes to the reality of His present reign, causing all the people to praise God for the light of the New World.

8. New Creation Reality

In Luke 18, the glory of the Son is revealed as the power that justifies the humble and gives sight to the blind. As a citizen of the New Creation, you are the "Tax Collector" who has gone home justified and the "Blind Man" who now sees the King. You do not live in the "Exhausting Performance" of trying to prove your righteousness through fasting or tithing; you live in the "Mercy" that has already been spoken over you. The "Thief" of religious pride has been humbled. You possess the "Treasure in Heaven" that is your established status in the Father's house. You inhabit a world where the King has already "avenged" His chosen ones, and your only requirement is to "follow Him" in the light of His finished victory. The "impossible" has been made "possible" for you.

9. Reflection

Question: If the Son declared that the humble tax collector was "justified" by mercy alone (Luke 18:14), why do we still allow modern "Pharisees" to try and judge our standing in the Kingdom based on our "performance" of rituals or our social status?
