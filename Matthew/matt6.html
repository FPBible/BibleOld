Matthew 6 — True Righteousness in the Father’s Presence

Immediate Context

Matthew 6 continues the Sermon on the Mount by exposing performative “righteousness” inside the temple-age world and relocating devotion from public status systems to direct Father-relationship—preparing disciples to live as New Covenant people while the old order nears its historical removal.

Scripture (WEB)

1“Be careful that you don’t do your charitable giving‡ NU reads “acts of righteousness” instead of “charitable giving” before men, to be seen by them, or else you have no reward from your Father who is in heaven. 2Therefore, when you do merciful deeds, don’t sound a trumpet before yourself, as the hypocrites do in the synagogues and in the streets, that they may get glory from men. Most certainly I tell you, they have received their reward. 3But when you do merciful deeds, don’t let your left hand know what your right hand does, 4so that your merciful deeds may be in secret, then your Father who sees in secret will reward you openly.

5“When you pray, you shall not be as the hypocrites, for they love to stand and pray in the synagogues and in the corners of the streets, that they may be seen by men. Most certainly, I tell you, they have received their reward. 6But you, when you pray, enter into your inner room, and having shut your door, pray to your Father who is in secret; and your Father who sees in secret will reward you openly. 7In praying, don’t use vain repetitions as the Gentiles do; for they think that they will be heard for their much speaking. 8Therefore don’t be like them, for your Father knows what things you need before you ask him. 9Pray like this:

10Let your Kingdom come.

11Give us today our daily bread.

12Forgive us our debts,

13Bring us not into temptation,

13For yours is the Kingdom, the power, and the glory forever. Amen.’‡ NU omits “For yours is the Kingdom, the power, and the glory forever. Amen.”

14“For if you forgive men their trespasses, your heavenly Father will also forgive you. 15But if you don’t forgive men their trespasses, neither will your Father forgive your trespasses.

16“Moreover when you fast, don’t be like the hypocrites, with sad faces. For they disfigure their faces that they may be seen by men to be fasting. Most certainly I tell you, they have received their reward. 17But you, when you fast, anoint your head and wash your face, 18so that you are not seen by men to be fasting, but by your Father who is in secret; and your Father who sees in secret will reward you.

19“Don’t lay up treasures for yourselves on the earth, where moth and rust consume, and where thieves break through and steal; 20but lay up for yourselves treasures in heaven, where neither moth nor rust consume, and where thieves don’t break through and steal; 21for where your treasure is, there your heart will be also.

22“The lamp of the body is the eye. If therefore your eye is sound, your whole body will be full of light. 23But if your eye is evil, your whole body will be full of darkness. If therefore the light that is in you is darkness, how great is the darkness!

24“No one can serve two masters, for either he will hate the one and love the other, or else he will be devoted to one and despise the other. You can’t serve both God and Mammon. 25Therefore I tell you, don’t be anxious for your life: what you will eat, or what you will drink; nor yet for your body, what you will wear. Isn’t life more than food, and the body more than clothing? 26See the birds of the sky, that they don’t sow, neither do they reap, nor gather into barns. Your heavenly Father feeds them. Aren’t you of much more value than they? 27“Which of you by being anxious, can add one moment§ literally, cubit to his lifespan? 28Why are you anxious about clothing? Consider the lilies of the field, how they grow. They don’t toil, neither do they spin, 29yet I tell you that even Solomon in all his glory was not dressed like one of these. 30But if God so clothes the grass of the field, which today exists and tomorrow is thrown into the oven, won’t he much more clothe you, you of little faith?

31“Therefore don’t be anxious, saying, ‘What will we eat?’, ‘What will we drink?’ or, ‘With what will we be clothed?’ 32For the Gentiles seek after all these things; for your heavenly Father knows that you need all these things. 33But seek first God’s Kingdom and his righteousness; and all these things will be given to you as well. 34Therefore don’t be anxious for tomorrow, for tomorrow will be anxious for itself. Each day’s own evil is sufficient.

Fulfillment Notes

1) Jesus confronts the public honor economy of the temple-age world and roots righteousness in Father-relationship rather than religious visibility.

2) “Let your Kingdom come” belongs to the first-century arrival and manifestation of the reign of God as the old covenant order reaches its end.

3) The prayer and forgiveness frame “debts” covenantally: reconciliation is the posture of the New Covenant people during the transition.

4) “Treasures in heaven” redirects Israel from fading, theft-prone earthly systems to an unshakable covenant inheritance.

5) “Don’t be anxious” forms a settled people who live from the Father’s care, not from the instability of a world about to be shaken.

Annotations

your Father who sees in secret will reward you openly: The Father is not accessed through display; true reward flows from hidden union, not public performance.
your Father who is in secret: Jesus relocates “holy place” logic from temple-space to Father-nearness—direct access replaces mediated status.
Let your Kingdom come.: First-century covenant transition language—God’s reign arriving in power as the old age closes (AD 30–70).
You can’t serve both God and Mammon.: A loyalty test between covenant allegiance and wealth-power systems that rival God as “master.”
But seek first God’s Kingdom and his righteousness; and all these things will be given to you as well.: Kingdom-first stability in the midst of shaking; righteousness as covenant membership and life under God’s rule.
debts: More than money—relational and covenant obligations; New Covenant life expresses forgiveness because the accusation system is being removed.
treasures in heaven: Covenant inheritance that cannot be stolen, corrupted, or collapsed when earthly structures fail.

Fulfillment Commentary

1) From Temple-Visibility to Father-Union

Matthew 6 is not a generic “be humble” talk; it’s a direct confrontation with how the temple-age world produced righteousness—through visibility, reputation, and approval inside the religious-social machine. Jesus names the “hypocrites” not as random mean people, but as representatives of a system that trained Israel to perform covenant identity in public. Alms, prayers, fasting—these were real acts, but they were easily weaponized into status. In that environment, righteousness could become theater, and the “reward” was immediate: applause, honor, influence, control. Jesus cuts through that entire economy by anchoring devotion in the Father who is already present and who sees what the public cannot see. This is covenantal movement: the center of gravity is shifting away from temple space and priestly gatekeeping toward direct Father-relationship. That shift is the seed of the New Creation life: access is not earned by performance, and holiness is not managed by spectators; it flows from union.

2) The “Secret Place” Is Not Hiding—It’s New Covenant Access

“Your Father who is in secret” is not Jesus promoting spiritual introversion; it’s Jesus announcing the end of a mediated presence model. Under the old order, the “secret” place was bound up with veils and restricted access—holy space for a limited priesthood on a limited schedule. Jesus now tells ordinary disciples to go into an inner room and pray to the Father as Father. That is explosive in covenant terms. It anticipates what will become normal when the old system is retired: the Father is not far, not locked behind ritual machinery, not parceled out by institutional control. The “reward” language is also covenantal: not a prosperity formula, but the Father’s open vindication and provision for those aligned with his kingdom way—especially as the transition intensifies toward AD 70 and the old world’s claims begin to collapse.

3) “Let Your Kingdom Come” and the First-Century Horizon

The Lord’s Prayer sits inside the Sermon on the Mount, addressed to disciples living in the overlap of ages. “Let your Kingdom come” is not a vague request for a distant end-of-history event; it belongs to the first-century arrival and manifestation of God’s reign as the Mosaic age reaches its conclusion. The prayer trains disciples to desire what God is doing in their time: the unveiling of the King, the gathering of a renewed people, and the removal of the covenant barriers that had defined Israel’s distance. Read this in covenant transition terms and it becomes coherent: the name of God is being honored in the Son; the will of God is being enacted in the new covenant; daily bread and deliverance are prayed by a people who will face real pressures as the old world shakes; forgiveness is demanded because New Covenant life cannot be lived while clinging to accusation and retaliation—the very spirit of the old order.

4) Debts, Forgiveness, and the End of Accusation

Jesus ties prayer to forgiveness with teeth: if you forgive, you’re forgiven; if you don’t, you aren’t. That’s not Jesus re-installing a new legal treadmill; it’s Jesus describing covenant posture during a covenant transition. The old system was powered by accusation—law-defined trespass, debt imagery, and relational fracture. New Covenant life, by contrast, embodies release. If the Messiah is bringing “save his people from their sins,” then his people must live as released people: releasing others, refusing to weaponize trespass, refusing to live by the old economy of scorekeeping. This is why Matthew 6 matters for “fulfilled” theology: it shows what the new world feels like. It is not fear-driven, not performance-driven, not accusation-driven. It is Father-driven—living from the inside out with mercy that matches the covenant you claim to be in.

5) Treasures, Mammon, and the Shaking of an Old World

“Don’t lay up treasures on the earth” is commonly individualized into “don’t be materialistic,” but in Matthew’s flow it also functions as survival wisdom for a world nearing upheaval. Israel’s temple-centered society was not stable; it was heading toward judgment and collapse. Treasures could be seized, stolen, burned, confiscated, or rendered useless. Jesus redirects loyalty: invest in what cannot be shaken—“treasures in heaven,” covenant inheritance, kingdom life, Father approval. That’s why the “eye” imagery follows: perception determines what fills you. If your “eye” is evil—if your vision is governed by greed, fear, status, and scarcity—your inner world becomes darkness. Then Jesus states the dividing line: you can’t serve both God and Mammon. Mammon is not simply “money”; it’s wealth as master, wealth as security, wealth as identity. In a covenant transition, serving Mammon ties you to the failing structures of the old world, while serving God anchors you to the unshakable kingdom.

6) “Don’t Be Anxious” as a Kingdom-Stabilized People

Anxiety is not merely a mental health topic here; it’s a discipleship marker. Jesus is forming a people who can live calmly while an age is shaking. Birds, lilies, daily provision—these images are not naive. They train a new covenant nervous system: the Father is reliable. The old system made people manage survival through control—status, purity performance, public reputation, hoarding, leverage. Jesus replaces control with trust: seek first the kingdom and righteousness, and the Father supplies what is needed. This is not a promise that nothing difficult ever happens; it’s a promise that the Father’s care is not interrupted by external instability. In first-century terms, this prepares disciples for pressures, persecutions, displacement, and loss. In New Creation terms today, it means your life is not held together by “tomorrow” plans and fear cycles; it is held together by the Father’s presence now.

7) The Net Result: Righteousness Without Performance, Prayer Without Distance

Matthew 6 is kingdom formation. It dismantles temple-age righteousness as public theater and installs New Covenant righteousness as relational reality. It trains prayer as communion, not manipulation. It redefines forgiveness as the natural language of a released people. It severs loyalty to Mammon as master and anchors identity in the Father who provides. And because we read this through a fulfilled, first-century resolution, we can say plainly: the transition reached its historical conclusion; the old world that depended on public status, temple leverage, and law-powered accusation is not the governing covenant reality anymore. What remains is the Father’s nearness and the kingdom life of those who live from it.
