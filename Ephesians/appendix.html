Appendix

Key Terms in Ephesians (Fulfilled / New Creation Lens)

Original text (for reference): Ephesians 1, Ephesians 2, Ephesians 3, Ephesians 4, Ephesians 5, Ephesians 6

Why This Appendix Exists

Ephesians is one of the clearest New Creation letters in the New Testament. It is not written as a prediction of distant events. It is written as a proclamation of a finished covenant transition and the settled identity of God’s people in Christ.

This appendix exists to keep the letter readable and stable. It defines the major phrases Paul uses so the reader is not forced into futurist assumptions or fear-based interpretations.

1) “In Christ”

“In Christ” is not religious language for “trying to be a better person.” It is covenant location. It describes where life is now found: in union with the risen and enthroned Messiah.

  • Not: self-improvement
  • But: participation — shared life, access, identity

Ephesians treats “in Christ” as present reality, not future reward. If you read it as postponed, the entire letter collapses.

2) “Heavenly Places”

“Heavenly places” does not mean “where you go when you die.” It refers to present authority and access: a shared position in Christ’s reign.

Paul’s focus is not geography. It is status: Christ is seated above every rule and power, and His people share His position.

  • Not: a future map of the afterlife
  • But: present covenant access and authority

Prophecy background: Son of Man authority (Daniel 7)

3) “Dead” and “Alive”

In Ephesians 2, “dead” is not physical death. It is covenant death: life under sin and condemnation, shaped by an age that was passing away.

“Alive” is covenant resurrection: life in Christ, in the Spirit, with access to the Father.

  • Dead: alienated, condemned, outside covenant life
  • Alive: reconciled, included, sharing Christ’s life

Resurrection framework: Resurrection Explained — 1 Corinthians 15

4) “The Mystery”

A biblical “mystery” is not something still waiting to happen. It is something once hidden that is now revealed. Paul says it plainly: the mystery “has now been revealed.”

The mystery is not a secret rapture. It is the creation of one new covenant people: Gentiles included as fellow heirs, members of the same body, without Torah boundary markers.

  • Not: future prophecy code
  • But: the now-revealed New Humanity in Christ

5) “Ages” and Time Language

Ephesians is saturated with time markers: “formerly… but now,” “now revealed,” “ages,” “the day,” “until.”

These markers refer to a real covenant transition that occurred within the first century: the old covenant order reached its end, and the New Covenant world was established in Christ.

This does not require charts. It requires honesty about Paul’s tense and the New Testament’s own time-bound language.

6) “Rulers, Authorities, Powers”

Ephesians speaks of rulers and powers because the old covenant world operated through authority structures that enforced separation, accusation, and condemnation.

Paul does not tell believers to chase spirits. He tells them to stand firm in truth, righteousness, peace, and faith. The conflict is allegiance and stability, not supernatural combat theatre.

  • Not: demon-hunting instructions
  • But: standing against accusation and oppressive authority

See also: Colossians 2 — disarming the powers

7) “The Law” and “The Dividing Wall”

Ephesians 2 states the dividing wall was abolished: the “law of commandments expressed in ordinances.” This is not poetic. It is decisive.

The Law functioned as a covenant boundary system. Its removal is the basis of peace and the creation of one new humanity.

  • What ended: Torah as covenant boundary and condemning power
  • What remains: life in the Spirit expressed through love

Ephesians does not reintroduce the Law in chapters 4–6. It describes what love looks like in a reconciled people.

8) “Temple” and “Dwelling Place”

Ephesians 2 calls believers a temple and a dwelling place of God. This is the New Covenant replacement of the old temple system.

God’s presence is no longer housed in stone and priesthood. It is present in a people.

  • Not: future rebuilding
  • But: present indwelling by the Spirit

9) “Submission” and Household Instructions

Paul addresses real first-century social structures without endorsing domination. His ethic is shaped by Christ: self-giving love, service, and non-partiality.

The controlling principle is mutual submission: “submitting to one another out of reverence for Christ.”

Ephesians uses household relationships as practical ground for New Creation life: truth, kindness, restraint, love, and dignity.

10) What Ephesians Is Actually Doing

Ephesians is not trying to motivate believers through fear. It is not trying to keep them waiting for what God might do one day.

It is announcing:

  • a finished reconciliation (one body, one Spirit, one access)
  • a settled identity (in Christ, chosen, adopted, sealed)
  • a present authority (seated with Christ, standing firm)
  • a visible way of life (truth, love, peace, maturity)

Ephesians only reads cleanly when you stop postponing it. It is New Creation reality, written as now.
