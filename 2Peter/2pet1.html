2 Peter 1

Partakers of the Divine Nature

Status: Divine Nature Hope: The Day Dawns

Scripture (WEB)

3seeing that his divine power has granted to us all things that pertain to life and godliness, through the knowledge of him who called us by his own glory and virtue; 4by which he has granted to us his precious and exceedingly great promises; that through these you may become partakers of the divine nature, having escaped from the corruption that is in the world by lust. 10Therefore, brothers, be more diligent to make your calling and election sure. For if you do these things, you will never stumble. 11For thus you will be richly supplied with the entrance into the eternal Kingdom of our Lord and Savior, Jesus Christ. 16For we didn’t follow cunningly devised fables when we made known to you the power and coming of our Lord Jesus Christ, but we were eyewitnesses of his majesty. 19We have the more sure prophetic word; and you do well that you heed it, as to a lamp shining in a dark place, until the day dawns, and the morning star arises in your hearts.

Fulfillment Commentary

Partakers of the Divine Nature (v. 4)

Peter reminds the transition generation that they had already been granted "all things that pertain to life and godliness." By escaping the corruption of the Old World, they were becoming "partakers of the divine nature." This isn't a promise of biological immortality, but of covenantal union with God—a restoration of the "Presence" that Adam lost.

The Eternal Kingdom Entrance (v. 11)

Peter speaks of a "richly supplied" entrance into the "eternal Kingdom." In the first-century context, this entrance was the final, formal establishment of the New Covenant Kingdom at the destruction of the Old Covenant Temple. They were standing at the threshold of the "Everlasting Kingdom" which would never be shaken (Hebrews 12:28).

Power and Coming (v. 16)

Peter defends the reality of the "power and coming" (parousia) of Christ. He anchors this not in myths, but in his own experience at the Transfiguration. The Transfiguration was a "preview" of the glory that was about to be revealed in the fall of Jerusalem. Peter’s point is that the *same* glory he saw on the mountain was about to break through into history.

Until the Day Dawns (v. 19)

The "prophetic word" (the Old Testament) was like a lamp in a dark place for the early church. They were to follow it "until the day dawns." This "day" was the Day of the Lord, and the "morning star" was Christ’s full manifestation in the hearts of the believers as the Old Covenant darkness finally vanished. To Peter, this was not a distant dawn; it was the light breaking over the horizon of their specific generation.
