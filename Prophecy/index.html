Prophecy Hub

Fulfilled Judgment · Covenant Transition · New Creation

Biblical prophecy does not describe the end of the physical universe. It announces covenant judgment, the removal of an old religious order, and the establishment of a new way of life in Christ.

This hub exists to show how Scripture itself uses prophetic language — and how the New Testament declares its fulfilment.

How to Read This Hub

The prophets spoke in symbolic, poetic, and covenantal language. Heaven and earth, sun and moon, clouds and fire, coming and shaking are not modern scientific terms. They describe authority, worship systems, judgment, and transition.

The New Testament does not reinterpret these symbols into something new. It applies them — decisively — to the first-century transition from the Old Covenant world to the New.

I. Covenant Judgment Texts

These texts announce judgment on the Old Covenant system. They are time-bound, audience-relevant, and fulfilled within history.

  • Matthew 24 — Judgment on Jerusalem
    Jesus interprets Israel’s prophetic tradition and announces the end of the temple world.
  • Luke 21 — Days of Vengeance
    Luke makes explicit that these events fall within that generation.
  • Daniel — Kingdom, Son of Man, and Time
    The framework behind “coming,” authority, and kingdom language.
  • Isaiah — New Heavens, New Earth, New Humanity
    Creation language used to describe covenant renewal and restoration.
  • Ezekiel 37 — Resurrection as Restoration
    Resurrection imagery applied to national and covenant renewal.
  • Psalms — Enthronement and Rule
    Royal and temple imagery shaping New Testament claims about Christ.
  • 2 Peter 3 — Heavens and Earth
    The passing of a covenant world, not the burning of the cosmos.
  • Revelation — Symbol, Judgment, Victory
    Apocalyptic imagery revealing judgment, vindication, and victory.

II. Presence and “Coming” Clarified

These pages explain how Scripture uses “coming” language — not as delayed physical travel, but as royal presence and exercised authority.

  • Son of Man — Covenant Authority and Vindication

  • 1 Thessalonians 4 — Presence, Not Escape
    Comfort language rooted in resurrection hope, not evacuation theology.

III. Resurrection Explained

These writings are interpretive, not predictive. They explain what resurrection means in light of Christ’s finished work.

  • 1 Corinthians 15 — Resurrection and Victory
    Resurrection defined as the defeat of death and the completion of covenant transition.

IV. Judgment, Satan, and Hell

These pages address commonly misunderstood terms without importing later mythology or fear-based frameworks.

  • Satan — The Adversary in Covenant Context
    “The satan” as accuser and opposer within Israel’s covenant story.
  • Hell — Origins and Meaning
    Gehenna, Hades, and judgment language explained within Scripture’s own horizon.

Summary

Prophecy in Scripture is not about predicting the distant future. It is about God setting things right — judging what is corrupt, vindicating what is faithful, and establishing life in Christ.

The New Testament declares that this transition has occurred. The invitation now is not to wait, but to live within what has been fulfilled.
