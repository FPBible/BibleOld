1 Peter 1

The Living Hope • The Last Time • The Ready Salvation

1. Immediate Context

Peter writes in the mid-60s AD, addressing Jewish Christians scattered across the Roman provinces of Asia Minor — Pontus, Galatia, Cappadocia, Asia, and Bithynia. The Neronian persecution has begun. The Temple in Jerusalem still stands, but its days are numbered. Peter writes knowing that the Old Covenant age is in its final hours.

Chapter 1 establishes the eschatological horizon of the entire letter. Peter's language is saturated with time-anchored phrases: "ready to be revealed," "the last time," "the end of times," "a little while." These are not vague spiritual expressions. They are precise covenant signals to a generation living at the seam between the ages. The prophets of Israel, Peter says, were ministering not to themselves but to this generation.

2. Scripture (WEB)

Source (verbatim): https://ebible.org/web/1PE01.htm

1 Peter, an apostle of Jesus Christ, to the chosen ones who are living as foreigners in the Dispersion in Pontus, Galatia, Cappadocia, Asia, and Bithynia,
2 according to the foreknowledge of God the Father, in sanctification of the Spirit, that you may obey Jesus Christ and be sprinkled with his blood: Grace to you and peace be multiplied.
3 Blessed be the God and Father of our Lord Jesus Christ, who according to his great mercy caused us to be born again to a living hope through the resurrection of Jesus Christ from the dead,
4 to an incorruptible and undefiled inheritance that doesn't fade away, reserved in Heaven for you,
5 who by the power of God are guarded through faith for a salvation ready to be revealed in the last time.
6 In this you greatly rejoice, though now for a little while, if need be, you have been put to grief in various trials,
7 that the proof of your faith, which is more precious than gold that perishes even though it is tested by fire, may be found to result in praise, glory, and honor at the revelation of Jesus Christ—
8 whom, not having known, you love. In him, though now you don't see him, yet believing, you rejoice with joy unspeakable and full of glory,
9 receiving the result of your faith, the salvation of your souls.
10 Concerning this salvation, the prophets sought and searched diligently, who prophesied of the grace that would come to you,
11 searching for what or what kind of time the Spirit of Christ, which was in them, pointed to, when he predicted the sufferings of Christ, and the glories that would follow them.
12 To them it was revealed, that not to themselves, but to you, they ministered these things, which now have been announced to you through those who preached the Good News to you by the Holy Spirit sent out from heaven; which things angels desire to look into.
13 Therefore, prepare your minds for action, be sober, and set your hope fully on the grace that will be brought to you at the revelation of Jesus Christ—
14 as obedient children, not conforming yourselves according to your former lusts as in your ignorance,
15 but just as he who called you is holy, you yourselves also be holy in all of your behavior;
16 because it is written, "You shall be holy; for I am holy."
17 If you call on him as Father, who without respect of persons judges according to each man's work, pass the time of your stay here in fear:
18 knowing that you were redeemed, not with corruptible things, with silver or gold, from your useless way of life handed down from your fathers,
19 but with precious blood, as of a lamb without blemish and without spot, the blood of Christ;
20 who was foreknown before the foundation of the world, but was revealed at the end of times for your sake,
21 who through him are believers in God, who raised him from the dead, and gave him glory; so that your faith and hope might be in God.
22 Seeing you have purified your souls in your obedience to the truth through the Spirit in sincere brotherly affection, love one another from the heart fervently:
23 having been born again, not of corruptible seed, but of incorruptible, through the word of God, which lives and remains forever.
24 For, "All flesh is like grass, and all of man's glory like the flower in the grass. The grass withers, and its flower falls;
25 but the Lord's word endures forever." This is the word of Good News which was preached to you.

3. Fulfillment Notes

  • Ready to be Revealed (v. 5): The Greek hetoimos means immediately prepared, at the ready. Peter was not describing a salvation postponed thousands of years. He was telling his readers it was on the verge of full disclosure — the public vindication of the saints at the end of the Old Covenant age.
  • The Last Time (v. 5): In New Testament usage, "the last time/days/hour" consistently refers to the final period of the Old Covenant age — the AD 30–70 generation. See also Hebrews 1:2, Acts 2:17, 1 John 2:18. Peter locates his readers squarely within it.
  • Not to Themselves, but to You (v. 12): This is one of the definitive audience-relevance statements of the New Testament. The Old Testament prophets were ministering to Peter's first-century readers — not to 21st-century Christians. The "glories that would follow" Christ's sufferings were the realities that generation was about to inherit.
  • Revealed at the End of Times (v. 20): Peter does not say the end of times is coming someday. He locates Christ's incarnation at the end of the Old Covenant age — and his readers at its consummation.

4. Annotations

  • Dispersion — The Diaspora: Jewish Christians scattered through Rome's eastern provinces, heirs to the tradition of exile-and-return that shaped Israelite identity from Babylon onwards.
  • sprinkled with his blood — Exodus 24:8 language. Moses sprinkled the people to ratify the Mosaic Covenant. Peter applies the same ritual to the New Covenant, ratified by Christ's blood.
  • reserved in Heaven for you — "Heaven" here is the divine realm that guarantees and holds the inheritance secure — not a physical destination. The inheritance is the fullness of New Covenant life.
  • the revelation of Jesus Christ — Apokalypsis — the unveiling. Used three times in this letter (1:7, 1:13, 4:13). Each time it refers to the imminent manifestation of Christ's kingship in judgment and vindication.
  • useless way of life handed down from your fathers — The empty performance of the Mosaic system in its apostate, late-Second-Temple form. Not a generic comment on paganism, but a specific covenantal indictment.
  • corruptible / incorruptible seed — The contrast of the two ages: the biological, Adamic, Torah-defined lineage (corruptible) versus the new-creation life born of the Word (incorruptible).

5. Fulfillment Commentary

1) The Time-Stamp: "Ready to be Revealed in the Last Time" (v. 5)

This verse is the eschatological key to the entire letter. Peter does not say the salvation will eventually come, or that it will come at some unspecified future point. He says it is ready to be revealed — positioned, armed, on the edge of disclosure — in the last time. If Peter's readers were genuinely living in the last time, and the salvation was genuinely ready, then the context of the letter is the final decades of the Old Covenant age, not the 21st century.

This framing governs every subsequent verse. The "various trials" of v. 6, the "proof of faith by fire" of v. 7, the "revelation of Jesus Christ" of v. 7 — all belong to that specific generation's specific moment.

2) The Prophets Ministered to That Generation (v. 12)

Peter's statement in verse 12 is perhaps the most decisive audience-relevance claim in the New Testament. The holy prophets of Israel — Isaiah, Daniel, Jeremiah, Ezekiel — were not writing for themselves, and they were not writing for us in the 21st century. They were writing for the first-century church. The "glories that would follow" the sufferings of Christ were the established realities that generation was on the verge of fully inheriting as the Old Covenant world collapsed.

This does not mean those scriptures have no application today. It means their primary, intentional, authorial audience was Peter's generation. We read over their shoulder.

3) The Holiness Call as Covenant Transfer (vv. 14–16)

The quotation from Leviticus 19:2 — "You shall be holy, for I am holy" — was the foundational identity statement of national Israel under Moses. Peter applies it without qualification to his multi-ethnic church. This is a massive covenant transfer. The calling once reserved for a single biological nation has been extended to everyone in Christ. The practical implication is equally massive: the behavior of the New Covenant community is the lived evidence of the covenant shift.

4) Redeemed from the Fathers' Way of Life (vv. 17–19)

The "useless way of life handed down from your fathers" is the hollow, performance-based religious culture of late Second Temple Judaism — a system increasingly emptied of covenant faithfulness while meticulously maintaining its external forms. Peter's readers are not being told to leave paganism (they already have). They are being told that Christ has redeemed them from a religious tradition that had become a dead end. The Passover-lamb imagery of v. 19 makes the point precisely: the whole sacrificial system pointed to this moment and is now superseded.

5) "Revealed at the End of Times for Your Sake" (v. 20)

This may be the most time-anchoring phrase in the letter. Christ was "revealed at the end of times for your sake." Peter does not say the end of times is a future event. He says his readers are its beneficiaries — the generation for whose sake the whole long arc of covenant history had been moving. The end of times is not physical eschatology; it is the end of the Mosaic covenant age. And that generation stood at its consummation.

6) The Incorruptible Word Among the Ruins (vv. 23–25)

The quotation from Isaiah 40:6–8 closes the chapter with covenant contrast. Flesh withers; kingdoms fall; the Temple will fall. But the Word of God — now proclaimed in the gospel and embodied in the new-creation community — endures forever. Isaiah originally addressed this word to exiles in Babylon, promising that God's purposes would outlast their captors. Peter applies the same promise to his exiles: the Rome that is persecuting them will pass; the Word built into them will not.

7. Cross-References

  • Hebrews 1:2 — "In these last days he has spoken to us by his Son." Hebrews uses identical "last days" language, confirming the first-century horizon.
  • Acts 2:17 — Peter at Pentecost quotes Joel 2: "In the last days, I will pour out my Spirit." He identifies Pentecost as the last-days event.
  • Isaiah 40:6–8 — The source of Peter's "grass withers" quotation, originally addressed to Babylonian exiles as a promise of coming redemption.
  • Leviticus 19:2 — "You shall be holy, for I the Lord your God am holy." The Mosaic identity statement now transferred to the New Covenant community.

8. New Creation Reality

Peter's letter was written into crisis. His readers faced persecution, cultural exile, and the terrifying collapse of the world they had known. Yet the letter is saturated with joy — joy "unspeakable and full of glory" (v. 8). That joy was not wishful thinking; it was the settled confidence of people who knew what was happening and why. As a believer in the New Creation, you inherit the same foundation. The salvation is not pending — it has been revealed. The inheritance is not uncertain — it is incorruptible. The Word that held that generation through the storm is the same Word that holds you. You are not waiting for the story to end well. It already has.

9. Reflection

Question: Peter tells his readers to "set your hope fully" on the grace being brought to them. What does it look like to hold your hope fully, rather than hedging it against circumstances — and what does the phrase "ready to be revealed" say about where that confidence should be anchored?
