Acts 3

1. Immediate Context

In Acts 3, the power of the New Covenant Kingdom is demonstrated through a miraculous healing at the physical heart of the Old Covenant system—the Temple. This miracle serves as a judicial sign to Israel, validating the apostles as the authorized successors to the prophets. Peter’s subsequent sermon is one of the most eschatologically significant addresses in the New Testament, explicitly defining the "times of restoration" and the urgent necessity for the first-century generation to repent before the "Prophet like Moses" executes final judgment on the covenant-breakers.

2. Scripture (WEB)

1 Peter and John were going up together into the temple at the hour of prayer, the ninth hour. 2 A certain man who was lame from his mother’s womb was being carried, whom they laid daily at the door of the temple which is called Beautiful, to ask gifts for the needy of those who entered into the temple. 3 Seeing Peter and John about to go into the temple, he asked to receive a gift.

4 Peter, fastening his eyes on him, with John, said, “Look at us.” 5 He listened to them, expecting to receive something from them. 6 But Peter said, “Silver and gold I have none, but what I have, that I give you. In the name of Jesus Christ of Nazareth, get up and walk!” 7 He took him by the right hand and raised him up. Immediately his feet and his ankle bones received strength. 8 Leaping up, he stood and began to walk. He entered with them into the temple, walking, leaping, and praising God.

... 19 “Repent therefore, and turn again, that your sins may be blotted out, so that there may come times of refreshing from the presence of the Lord, 20 and that he may send the Christ who has been appointed for you, even Jesus, 21 whom heaven must receive until the times of restoration of all things, which God spoke by the mouth of all his holy prophets since the world began.

22 For Moses indeed said to the fathers, ‘The Lord God will raise up a prophet for you from among your brothers, like me. You shall listen to him in all things whatever he says to you. 23 It will be that every soul that will not listen to that prophet will be utterly destroyed from among the people.’ 24 Yes, and all the prophets from Samuel and those who followed after, as many as have spoken, they also told of these days.

Source: ebible.org

3. Fulfillment Notes

  • The Hour of Prayer (v. 1): The miracle occurs at the exact time of the evening sacrifice, showing the New Covenant power superseding the Old Covenant ritual.
  • The Restoration of All Things (v. 21): Peter identifies that the prophets spoke of "these days"—the transitional period ending in AD 70 when the Old system was fully replaced by the New.
  • Utterly Destroyed (v. 23): A direct warning of the impending national judgment (AD 70) for those who refused to hear the Prophet Jesus.
  • All the Prophets (v. 24): Peter explicitly states that the entire prophetic tradition pointed to "these days," the very time in which he was standing.

4. Annotations

  • Beautiful Gate (v. 2): Symbolic of the transition; the lame man is healed at the entrance to the Old Temple, signaling he is now fit for the New Temple.
  • Times of Refreshing (v. 19): The Greek anapsyxis refers to a recovery of breath; the spiritual relief brought by the New Covenant.
  • Prophet Like Moses (v. 22): A reference to Deuteronomy 18:15-19, identifying Jesus as the ultimate Lawgiver and Judge.
  • These Days (v. 24): A technical term for the apostolic age, the period between Christ's first coming and His judicial return in AD 70.

5. Fulfillment Commentary

The Sign at the Gate: Healing the Lame Israel (v. 1-10)

The healing of the man lame from his mother's womb is a living parable of the nation of Israel. Lame from her "birth" at Sinai, the nation was unable to walk in the commandments of the Law. By placing this miracle at the Beautiful Gate during the hour of sacrifice, God was demonstrating that what the Temple and the Law could not do (perfect the man), the Name of Jesus had accomplished. The man leaping and entering the Temple signifies that the true "restoration" is found in Christ, who makes the "lame" fit for the presence of God. This sign was a judicial summons to the priesthood: the authority had shifted from the gold of the Temple to the Name of the King.

The blotting out of Sins and Refreshing (v. 19)

Peter calls for repentance so that "sins may be blotted out." In the Old Covenant, sins were "covered" by the blood of bulls and goats, but the conscience was never fully cleared. The "times of refreshing" refers to the spiritual liberation that comes when the heavy yoke of the Law is removed and the Spirit is given. This refreshing was the immediate benefit of the New Covenant, intended to sustain the remnant through the "great and terrible day" that was fast approaching. It was not a future millennial hope, but a first-century spiritual reality.

The Restoration of All Things (v. 21)

Verse 21 is a pillar of fulfilled eschatology. Peter states that Jesus must remain in heaven "until the times of restoration of all things." Many futurists push this to the end of time, but Peter identifies the scope by saying it was what the "holy prophets" spoke of. What did the prophets speak of? They spoke of the restoration of the Kingdom, the regathering of the remnant, and the passing of the Old Covenant (Hebrews 8:13). This "restoration" was the process of transitioning the people of God from the physical shadows to the spiritual substance. This process was completed when the Temple was removed in AD 70, leaving only the "things that cannot be shaken" (Hebrews 12:27).

The Prophet Like Moses and the Destruction Clause (v. 22-23)

Peter quotes Moses’ prophecy from Deuteronomy 18 to highlight the judicial nature of Christ’s ministry. Moses was the mediator of the Old Covenant; Jesus is the mediator of the New. Moses warned that a failure to listen to this coming Prophet would result in being "utterly destroyed from among the people." This was not a warning of hell in the afterlife, but a warning of covenantal excommunication and physical destruction. Those who remained in the Old system after the Prophet had spoken were legally cut off. This warning found its historical fulfillment in the Siege of Jerusalem, where the "unbelieving" were indeed destroyed, while the "listening" remnant escaped.

"All the Prophets... Told of These Days" (v. 24)

Peter’s conclusion is definitive: "all the prophets... told of these days." If "these days" refers to the time of Peter and his audience, then the prophetic focus of Samuel, David, and the rest was the first-century climax of the ages. The prophets were not looking at the 21st century; they were looking at the arrival of the Messiah, His sacrifice, and the subsequent "Day of the Lord" that would wrap up the Jewish age. To push these prophecies into our future is to ignore Peter’s direct statement of contemporary fulfillment.

The Blessing of the Firstfruits (v. 25-26)

Peter ends by reminding the audience that they are the "sons of the prophets" and of the covenant. The blessing of the New Covenant was offered "first" to them. Jesus was sent to "turn away every one of you from your iniquities." This shows that the priority of the transition was the salvation of the Jewish remnant. God was being faithful to His promises to Abraham before the Old house was demolished. The "blessing" was the opportunity to enter the New Creation before the judgment fire fell on the old world.

8. New Creation Reality

Because the "restoration of all things" has been accomplished through the completion of the New Covenant, we live in a world where the spiritual access to God is fully open. We do not need a Beautiful Gate or a physical Temple; we are the Temple. The "times of refreshing" are our daily experience as we walk in the Spirit. The power that healed the lame man is the same power that has legally and spiritually restored our standing before God, making us active participants in the eternal New Creation.

9. Reflection
