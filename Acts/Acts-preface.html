Preface — Acts

The Transition Generation

Acts is the story of the overlap.

The new covenant community emerges while the Temple still stands. The gospel goes to the nations while Jerusalem remains the center. The Spirit is poured out while the old covenant structures linger. This is not the beginning of an indefinite church age. It is the documented account of the transition generation—the final decades between Pentecost and the destruction of the Temple in AD 70.

Luke writes Acts after Jerusalem has fallen. He knows how the story ends. Yet the narrative stops before AD 70, creating a dramatic irony. The reader knows what the characters do not yet see: the old world is about to burn, and everything the apostles are doing is preparing the new community to survive the collapse.

Luke-Acts as a Two-Volume Work

Acts is the second volume of Luke's historical project. The Gospel traces the Kingdom arriving and the old order condemned. Acts shows the new covenant community emerging and the gospel spreading to the nations—all before the Temple falls.

The two volumes together tell the complete story of covenant transition. Luke ends with the resurrection and the opened Scriptures. Acts begins with the ascension and the promise of the Spirit. Between these two points—resurrection and Pentecost—the hinge of history turns.

Pentecost and the Last Days

Acts 2 is the theological center of the book. Peter stands up on the day of Pentecost and declares, "This is what was spoken by the prophet Joel: 'In the last days, God says, I will pour out my Spirit on all people.'"

The last days are not our present moment looking backward. The last days are the final phase of the old covenant age—the terminal generation before the Temple's destruction. Pentecost inaugurates the New Covenant, but the old covenant structures remain operative for another forty years. This is the overlap. Two ages run simultaneously. The new is here, but the old has not yet fully passed away.

Peter does not say, "This will happen in the distant future." He says, "This is that." The last days begin at Pentecost. They end when the old age ends—in AD 70.

The Urgency of the Mission

Acts is written with urgency because the timeline is short. The apostles operate with a sense of compressed time. Paul's missionary journeys are not leisurely. They are driven. The gospel must reach the nations before the end comes.

This urgency is not generic evangelical zeal. It is mission within a specific window—the final generation before the covenantal judgment falls on Jerusalem. The gospel goes out to prepare a people who will survive the collapse, who will stand as the vindicated remnant when the old house is torn down.

The Temple Still Standing

Throughout Acts, the Temple remains the physical center of worship. The apostles go there to pray. They preach in Solomon's colonnade. They participate in purification rituals. The Temple has not yet been destroyed, but it is already obsolete in covenantal terms.

Stephen's speech in Acts 7 makes this explicit. He traces Israel's history of rejecting God's messengers, culminating in the rejection of Jesus. He declares that "the Most High does not dwell in houses made by hands," quoting Isaiah to show that the Temple was never intended to be permanent. For this, Stephen is stoned. The old order does not tolerate the claim that it is ending.

Yet Stephen's death marks the beginning of the gospel's spread beyond Jerusalem. Persecution scatters the believers, and wherever they go, they preach the word. The old center—Jerusalem and the Temple—loses its monopoly. The new community is portable, Spirit-filled, not tied to a location or a building.

The Gentile Inclusion

One of the central dramas of Acts is the inclusion of the Gentiles. This is not incidental. It is covenantal transformation. The old covenant was centered on ethnic Israel, tied to the land, mediated through the Temple. The new covenant explodes those boundaries.

Acts 10 is the hinge. Peter receives a vision: what God has made clean, do not call common. He goes to the house of Cornelius, a Gentile, and the Spirit falls on them before they are even baptized. Peter is forced to recognize that God is doing something new—something that does not fit the old covenant categories.

The Jerusalem Council in Acts 15 formalizes this shift. The question is whether Gentile believers must be circumcised and keep the Law of Moses. The answer is no. They are saved by grace, not by the works of the Law. This is not a minor adjustment. It is the dismantling of the entire Mosaic system's jurisdiction. The Law was until John. The gospel of the Kingdom has arrived.

From Jerusalem to Rome

Acts begins in Jerusalem and ends in Rome. This is not arbitrary geography. It is covenantal trajectory. The gospel moves from the old center to the new—from the city that will be destroyed to the city that represents the nations.

Paul's journey to Rome is the symbolic completion of the mission Jesus announced in Acts 1:8: "You will be my witnesses in Jerusalem and in all Judea and Samaria, and to the end of the earth." By the time Acts ends, the gospel has reached the heart of the empire. The new covenant community is no longer dependent on Jerusalem. When the city falls, the church will not fall with it. The transition is complete.

Signs and Wonders as Covenant Authentication

Acts is filled with signs and wonders—healings, exorcisms, resurrections, earthquakes, miraculous escapes. These are not random displays of power. They are covenant authentication, the visible proof that the new age has arrived and the old is passing away.

The miracles in Acts serve the same function as the signs Moses performed before Pharaoh or the plagues that validated the Exodus. They authenticate the apostles as messengers of the new covenant. They demonstrate that the Spirit has been poured out, that the last days have begun, and that God is doing something unprecedented.

The concentration of miracles in Acts is not a model for all church history. It is the signature of the transition generation—the final validation before the old order is removed.

The Overlap and the Tension

Acts documents a world in tension. The new covenant has been inaugurated, but the old covenant structures have not yet been dismantled. Believers are born again into the new creation, but they still live in the fading shadow of the old.

This tension runs through the entire book. Jewish believers still observe the festivals, still go to the Temple, still feel the pull of the old covenant identity. Gentile believers are told they are free from the Law, yet they must navigate a world where the Law still has social and legal force.

The tension is not resolved by theological argument. It is resolved by historical event. When the Temple falls in AD 70, the old covenant structures are physically removed. The overlap ends. The new creation stands alone. Acts does not narrate this event, but it is written knowing it has already happened.

Paul's Urgency

Paul dominates the second half of Acts, and his ministry is marked by relentless urgency. He moves from city to city, planting churches, appointing elders, writing letters, and pressing forward. There is no leisure in his mission. There is only the driving sense that time is short.

Paul knows he will not live to see old age in peaceful retirement. He knows suffering awaits him in every city. He knows chains and afflictions are coming. Yet he also knows the end is near—not the end of the world, but the end of the age. He must finish the work before the judgment falls.

When Paul says in his letters, "The time is short" or "The night is far gone, the day is at hand," he is not speaking metaphorically. He is describing the actual timeline of the transition generation. Acts shows this urgency in motion—the race against time to establish the new covenant community before the old world collapses.

The Abrupt Ending

Acts ends abruptly. Paul is under house arrest in Rome, preaching the Kingdom of God and teaching about the Lord Jesus Christ with all boldness and without hindrance. And then the narrative stops.

We do not get Paul's trial, his release, his further travels, or his martyrdom. We do not get the destruction of Jerusalem or the fall of the Temple. Luke knows these events have happened—he writes after AD 70—but he does not narrate them.

Why? Because Acts is not the story of what happened after the transition. It is the story of the transition itself. The narrative ends when the gospel has reached Rome, when the mission to the nations is established, when the new covenant community is no longer dependent on Jerusalem. The rest—the fall of the city, the vindication of the faithful, the final removal of the old order—is assumed knowledge for Luke's readers.

How to Read Acts

  • Read it as the second volume of Luke's historical project, continuing the story from the Gospel
  • Locate it in the overlap period—the final generation between Pentecost and AD 70
  • Take the "last days" language in Acts 2 literally—the last days of the old covenant age
  • See the Gentile inclusion as covenantal transformation, not merely ethnic expansion
  • Recognize the urgency as mission within a compressed timeline, not general zeal
  • Read the miracles as covenant authentication for the transition generation
  • Understand the abrupt ending as deliberate—the story ends when the mission is complete, before the judgment falls

Why Acts Matters Now

We do not live in the overlap. The Temple has fallen. The old covenant age has ended. The transition generation passed through the furnace and established the new community. We inherit what they secured.

Acts is not a blueprint for what the church should always be doing. It is the historical record of what the church did in the unique moment of covenant transition. The signs and wonders, the urgency, the mission to the nations before the end—these belong to that generation, not to every generation.

What we learn from Acts is how the first believers navigated the most disorienting moment in redemptive history—the collapse of the world they had known and the emergence of something entirely new. Their courage, their faithfulness, and their refusal to cling to the old when the new was breaking in—this is the testimony that runs before us.

The overlap has ended. The new creation stands as the sole operative reality. Acts shows us how we got here.
