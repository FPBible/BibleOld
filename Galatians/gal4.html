Galatians 4

1. Immediate Context

Building on the "tutor" analogy from Chapter 3, Paul now explains the timing of God's redemptive plan. He moves from the legal status of an heir to the domestic reality of a son. Using the poignant contrast between an infant heir and a mature son, Paul exposes the tragedy of the Galatians' desire to return to "elemental principles." The chapter culminates in a radical allegory that identifies the earthly Jerusalem of the first century as the "slave woman" Hagar, destined for removal to make way for the free Jerusalem from above.

2. Scripture (WEB)

Source (verbatim): https://ebible.org/web/GAL04.htm

1 But I say that so long as the heir is a child, he is no different from a bondservant, though he is lord of all;
2 but is under guardians and stewards until the day appointed by the father.
3 So we also, when we were children, were held in bondage under the elemental principles of the world.
4 But when the fullness of the time came, God sent out his Son, born to a woman, born under the law,
5 that he might redeem those who were under the law, that we might receive the adoption of children.
6 And because you are children, God sent out the Spirit of his Son into your hearts, crying, “Abba, Father!”
7 So you are no longer a bondservant, but a son; and if a son, then an heir of God through Christ.
8 However at that time, not knowing God, you were in bondage to those who by nature are not gods.
9 But now that you have come to know God, or rather to be known by God, why do you turn back again to the weak and miserable elemental principles, to which you desire to be in bondage all over again?
10 You observe days, months, seasons, and years.
11 I am afraid for you, that I might have wasted my labor for you.
12 I beg you, brothers, become as I am, for I also have become as you are. You did me no wrong,
13 but you know that because of weakness of the flesh I preached the Good News to you the first time.
14 That which was a temptation to you in my flesh, you didn’t despise nor reject; but you received me as an angel of God, even as Christ Jesus.
15 What was the blessing you enjoyed? For I testify to you that, if possible, you would have plucked out your eyes and given them to me.
16 So then, have I become your enemy by telling you the truth?
17 They zealously seek you in no good way. No, they desire to alienate you, that you may seek them.
18 But it is always good to be zealous in a good cause, and not only when I am present with you.
19 My little children, of whom I am again in travail until Christ is formed in you—
20 but I could wish to be present with you now, and to change my tone, for I am perplexed about you.
21 Tell me, you that desire to be under the law, don’t you listen to the law?
22 For it is written that Abraham had two sons, one by the handmaid, and one by the free woman.
23 However, the son by the handmaid was born according to the flesh, but the son by the free woman was born through promise.
24 These things contain an allegory, for these are two covenants. One is from Mount Sinai, bearing children to bondage, which is Hagar.
25 For this Hagar is Mount Sinai in Arabia, and answers to the Jerusalem that exists now, for she is in bondage with her children.
26 But the Jerusalem that is above is free, which is the mother of us all.
27 For it is written, “Rejoice, you barren who don’t bear. Break out and shout, you who don’t travail. For more are the children of the desolate than of her who has a husband.”
28 Now we, brothers, as Isaac was, are children of promise.
29 But as then, he who was born according to the flesh persecuted him who was born according to the Spirit, so also it is now.
30 However what does the Scripture say? “Throw out the handmaid and her son, for the son of the handmaid will not inherit with the son of the free woman.”
31 So then, brothers, we are not children of a handmaid, but of the free woman.

3. Fulfillment Notes

  • Historical Fulcrum: "The fullness of the time" (v. 4) marks the first-century climax of the Old Covenant age.
  • Judicial Removal: The command to "Throw out the handmaid" (v. 30) is the prophetic verdict against the earthly Temple system.
  • Covenant Overlap: Paul identifies "the Jerusalem that exists now" as currently in bondage with her children (v. 25).
  • Spiritual Seal: The indwelling Spirit crying "Abba" is the internal proof of the completed transition from slave to son (v. 6).

4. Annotations

  • Elemental Principles (Stoicheia): Verse 3, 9. The basic "ABC" components of the old religious world—ceremonies, calendars, and boundary laws.
  • Abba: Verse 6. An Aramaic term for Father, signifying intimate familial belonging that replaces legal distance.
  • Jerusalem that exists now: Verse 25. The literal, earthly city of Jerusalem in the first century, serving as the center of the fading Old Covenant.
  • Jerusalem that is above: Verse 26. The spiritual reality of the New Covenant people; the "Mother" city of the New Creation.

5. Fulfillment Commentary

The Fullness of Time and the Arrival of the Son (v. 4-5)

Paul introduces the "fullness of time" as a historical appointment. In fulfilled eschatology, this isn't just a convenient date in history; it is the expiration date of the "guardian" system. God sent His Son precisely when the Old Covenant era had reached its terminal point. By being "born under the law," Christ was positioned to fulfill the specific legal demands of that age and "redeem those who were under the law." This was the Great Exchange: the Son entered the system of bondage to legally pull the heirs out of it and into "adoption."

Returning to the "Weak and Miserable" Principles (v. 8-11)

Paul makes a stunning comparison: he equates the Galatians' desire for Torah-observance with their former pagan idolatry. Both are "elemental principles" (stoicheia) of the old world. Whether a person is bound by pagan "gods" or by "days, months, seasons, and years" (the Jewish liturgical calendar), they are still in bondage to the physical world. Paul views the adoption of the Mosaic calendar not as spiritual progress, but as a regression into the kindergarten of the human race. In the New Creation, righteousness is not mapped out on a calendar; it is found in the Person of Christ.

The Allegory of Two Jerusalems (v. 21-27)

Paul utilizes the "law" itself to dismantle the law. He identifies Hagar (the slave woman) with Mount Sinai and—most provocatively—with "the Jerusalem that exists now." This was a theological bombshell for the first century. Paul is stating that the earthly, physical center of Judaism is actually a place of slavery. In contrast, "the Jerusalem that is above" is the mother of the free. This is the New Covenant community, the "Israel of God" that is not bound by geographic borders or ethnic lineage. The "desolate" woman (the Gentiles) now has more children than the one with a "husband" (ethnic Israel under the law).

The Persecution of the Spirit by the Flesh (v. 28-29)

Paul notes a historical pattern: just as Ishmael (born of the flesh) persecuted Isaac (born of the Spirit), so the "Jerusalem that exists now" was currently persecuting the New Covenant believers. This was the lived reality of the AD 30-70 period. The Old Covenant establishment was violently resisting its obsolescence. For Paul, this persecution was proof that the Galatians were on the right side of history—they were children of promise, suffering at the hands of those who still clung to the dying era of the flesh.

The Prophetic Eviction: Throw Out the Bondwoman (v. 30-31)

Paul concludes with the ultimate prophetic verdict: "Throw out the handmaid and her son." In the framework of fulfilled prophecy, this is the judicial announcement of the AD 70 destruction. The Old Covenant system and the New Covenant inheritance cannot coexist. The "son of the handmaid" (those clinging to the Law) will not inherit with the "son of the free woman." The transition is absolute. Paul tells the Galatians—and us—that we are not children of the slave-system, but of the Free Woman. The old world has been evicted; we live in the house of the Father as full, free sons.

8. New Creation Reality

Galatians 4 is your certificate of maturity. You are no longer an infant who needs a "guardian" to tell you when to eat, how to dress, or what days are holy. The Spirit of the Son is within you, crying "Abba!" If you find yourself gravitating toward religious rules to feel "secure" with God, you are returning to the nursery. New Creation life means living as a child of the Free Woman, dwelling in the spiritual Jerusalem, and refusing to let any "elemental principle" of this world dictate your belonging. You are already in the house; stop acting like a slave.

9. Reflection

In what ways am I still trying to live by "days and seasons" rather than by the indwelling Spirit of the Son?
