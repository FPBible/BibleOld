Colossians 3

1. Immediate Context

Colossians 3 marks the transition from theological defense to covenantal application. Paul has established that believers are transferred, made full, and released from the judgment of shadows. Now, he anchors their daily ethics in the completed reality of their union with Christ. The focus is the replacement of the "Old Human" (the Adamic/Old Covenant order) with the "New Human" (the Christic/New Covenant order). It is a call to live from a secure identity rather than striving for a distant one.

2. Scripture (WEB)

Source (verbatim): https://ebible.org/web/COL03.htm

1 If then you were raised together with Christ, seek the things that are above, where Christ is, seated on the right hand of God.
2 Set your mind on the things that are above, not on the things that are on the earth.
3 For you died, and your life is hidden with Christ in God.
4 When Christ, our life, is revealed, then you will also be revealed with him in glory.
5 Put to death therefore your members which are on the earth: sexual immorality, uncleanness, depraved passion, evil desire, and covetousness, which is idolatry;
6 for which things’ sake the wrath of God comes on the children of disobedience.
7 You also walked in these in the past, when you lived in them.
8 But now you also put them all away: anger, wrath, malice, slander, and shameful speaking out of your mouth.
9 Don’t lie to one another, seeing that you have put off the old man with his doings,
10 and have put on the new man, who is being renewed in knowledge after the image of his Creator,
11 where there cannot be Greek and Jew, circumcision and uncircumcision, barbarian, Scythian, bondservant, freeman; but Christ is all, and in all.
12 Put on therefore, as God’s chosen ones, holy and beloved, a heart of compassion, kindness, lowliness, humility, and perseverance;
13 bearing with one another, and forgiving each other, if any man has a complaint against any; even as Christ forgave you, so you also do.
14 Above all these things, walk in love, which is the bond of perfection.
15 And let the peace of God rule in your hearts, to which also you were called in one body; and be thankful.
16 Let the word of Christ dwell in you richly; in all wisdom teaching and admonishing one another with psalms, hymns, and spiritual songs, singing with grace in your hearts to the Lord.
17 Whatever you do, in word or in deed, do all in the name of the Lord Jesus, giving thanks to God the Father, through him.
18 Wives, be in subjection to your husbands, as is fitting in the Lord.
19 Husbands, love your wives, and don’t be bitter against them.
20 Children, obey your parents in all things, for this is well pleasing to the Lord.
21 Fathers, don’t provoke your children, so that they won’t be discouraged.
22 Servants, obey in all things those who are your masters according to the flesh, not just when they are looking, as men pleasers, but in singleness of heart, fearing God.
23 And whatever you do, work heartily, as for the Lord, and not for men,
24 knowing that from the Lord you will receive the reward of the inheritance; for you serve the Lord Christ.
25 But he who does wrong will receive again for the wrong that he has done, and there is no partiality.

3. Fulfillment Notes

  • Time-Frame Statements: "Wrath... comes" (v. 6) reflects the imminent covenantal judgment horizon of the first century, specifically the Day of the Lord against the old order.
  • Audience Indicators: The abolition of "circumcision and uncircumcision" as identity markers (v. 11) is a primary indicator of the first-century covenant transition.
  • Covenant Transition Markers: The "Old Man" and "New Man" contrast serves as the definitive anthropological shift between the age of Adam/Law and the age of Christ/Spirit.
  • First-Century Contextual Anchors: "Greek and Jew" and "Barbarian, Scythian" were the primary social and covenantal divisions defining the pre-AD 70 world.

4. Annotations

  • Above (Ano): The realm of Christ’s current authority and reign; not a geographic location in outer space, but the seat of New Covenant power.
  • Wrath (Orge): Covenantal judicial consequences resulting from the rejection of the Messiah and the persistence in the old-world patterns of corruption.
  • Old Man / New Man: Corporate identities. The "Old Man" is humanity under the covenant of Law; the "New Man" is humanity under the covenant of Grace.
  • Image (Eikon): The restoration of humanity to its intended design in Christ, who is the exact representation of God.
  • Barbarian / Scythian: Specific first-century markers of the "uncivilized" and "extreme" outsiders, emphasizing that Christ dissolves every social barrier.

5. Fulfillment Commentary

Living from Christ's Realm of Authority (v. 1-2)

Paul begins with the logic of resurrection. If the first-century saints were "raised with Christ," their orientation must change. Seeking the things "above" is not a command to engage in escapism or to wait for a literal departure from the earth. In fulfilled eschatology, "above" is the realm of Christ's current authority. Since the war is won and the King is seated at the right hand of God, the believer's mindset must be rooted in that victory rather than the fears and shadow-systems of the "earth" (the old covenant order).

Setting the mind on things above means evaluating life through the lens of Christ's finished work. The "earthly" things are those governed by the obsolete regulations, boundary markers, and condemnation of the Mosaic economy. Paul is instructing the Colossians to live as citizens of the New Heavens and New Earth, which was breaking into their reality and would soon be fully established.

Covenantal Security: The Hidden Life (v. 3-4)

The phrase "you died" is profound covenant transfer language. The person who was once defined by Adam and the Law’s condemnation no longer exists in God’s sight. This is not a psychological goal but a legal reality. The life of the believer is "hidden with Christ in God"—a state of absolute covenantal security that is not fragile or performance-based.

The "revelation in glory" refers to the vindication of the saints that coincided with the end of the old age. When the earthly temple system was removed in AD 70, the true identity of the "New Man" was publicly manifested to the world. Their life, which had been secret and persecuted, was proven to be the true inheritance of God's family.

The Imminent Wrath on the Old World (v. 5-7)

Paul warns that "the wrath of God comes" (present tense) upon those who persist in the patterns of the old age. In the first-century context, this wrath is the historical judgment culminating in the destruction of Jerusalem. The list of vices—immorality, covetousness, idolatry—describes the "walking" of the old humanity. To remain in these behaviors was to remain under the impending cloud of the covenant's curse.

This is not a warning of random, end-of-the-world doom, but a specific judicial accountability for those who rejected the reconciliation offered in Christ. Paul’s call to "put to death" these members is a call to migrate ethically into the New Covenant reality before the old world is consumed by its own corruption.

The Migration of the Human Race (v. 8-11)

Paul’s anthropology is based on the transition of the ages. He describes a historical "putting off" of the Old Man and a "putting on" of the New Man. This is the New Creation in operation. This New Humanity is being "renewed in knowledge" according to the image of Christ. It is a restoration of what was lost in the Adamic fall, but enhanced by the indwelling life of the Spirit.

Crucially, in this New Human, the old boundary markers are obliterated. "Greek and Jew" and "circumcision and uncircumcision" no longer have the power to define who belongs. The collapse of these identity systems is a hallmark of fulfilled prophecy. Christ is now "all, and in all," meaning He is the sole definition and life-force of the people of God, regardless of their former tribal or social status.

The Climate of the New Covenant (v. 12-15)

Because the believers are "chosen, holy, and beloved," they are to put on compassion, kindness, and humility. Paul is not giving a new law to earn God's love; he is describing the natural clothing of those who are already loved. Forgiveness is to be practiced "even as Christ forgave you"—an appeal to the completed reconciliation of the cross.

The "peace of God" is commanded to "rule" (act as umpire) in their hearts. In fulfilled eschatology, this peace is the result of the war being over. The hostility between God and man, and between Jew and Gentile, has been resolved. Therefore, the inner climate of the New Creation is not anxiety or performance-driven fear, but the settled peace of a finished reconciliation.

New Creation in Ordinary Places (v. 16-25)

Paul concludes by showing that the New Creation is not confined to "spiritual" or "mystical" zones. It penetrates the most ordinary household relationships: husbands and wives, parents and children, masters and slaves. The life of the "New Human" is visible in how one speaks, works, and relates. Everything is to be done "in the name of the Lord Jesus."

The "reward of the inheritance" was the immediate promise for those first-century saints who persevered through the transition. By working "from the soul" as for the Lord, they demonstrated that their true Master was Christ, not the systems of the flesh. This brings the cosmic reality of the Kingdom down to the practical grit of daily survival, showing that the New Creation is an all-encompassing reality.

8. New Creation Reality

Colossians 3 is the blueprint for a mind renewed by the Spirit, free from the entanglements of religion. You do not become new by self-hatred or external fixing; you become new by living from your completed union with Christ. If Christ is your life, the old pattern of anger, insecurity, and fear no longer has a legal claim on you. You are free to put on the compassion and peace that match your true identity as a full heir of the New Creation.

9. Reflection

What old pattern—anger, insecurity, or the need for human approval—am I still treating as "me," instead of putting it off as something that no longer owns me in Christ?
