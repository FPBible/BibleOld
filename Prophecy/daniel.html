Daniel

Kingdom, Son of Man, and Time — Read From Fulfilment

Reading Frame (Mandatory)
Daniel is not a coded timetable for distant centuries. It is a prophetic narrative describing the rise and fall of empires, the judgment of covenant enemies, and the transfer of kingdom authority to the Son of Man. Jesus and the apostles treat Daniel as fulfilled or fulfilling in their own time.

Overview

  • Daniel frames history in terms of kingdoms, not calendars
  • The Son of Man “comes” to receive authority, not to travel to earth
  • Time statements are bounded and meaningful
  • Judgment language culminates in covenant transition
  • The kingdom is given, not postponed

1) Daniel’s Historical Setting Matters

Daniel is written during exile, under pagan empire. Its visions answer a central question: Who truly rules history — empire or God?

Key Insight
Daniel is not concerned with the end of the planet. It is concerned with the fate of empires, the vindication of God’s people, and the establishment of an unshakeable kingdom.

2) The Statue and the Four Beasts — Empires, Not Eternity

Daniel 2 presents a statue of successive metals. Daniel 7 presents four beasts. Both represent the same reality: successive empires.

  • Babylon
  • Medo-Persia
  • Greece
  • Rome

These kingdoms rise, dominate, and fall. None is eternal. (Dan 2) (Dan 7)

Critical Point
Daniel’s visions are political and covenantal. They describe who holds power and whose rule will endure.

3) The Stone and the Everlasting Kingdom

In Daniel 2, a stone “cut without hands” strikes the statue and grows into a mountain filling the earth. (Dan 2:34–35)

Fulfilled Lens
This is not a delayed future invasion. The stone represents God’s kingdom established during the time of the final empire. Jesus identifies this kingdom as present and advancing.

4) Daniel 7 — The Son of Man

Daniel sees “one like a Son of Man” coming to the Ancient of Days and receiving dominion, glory, and a kingdom. (Dan 7:13–14)

Decisive Clarification
The direction of movement is upward. The Son of Man comes to God, not down to earth. This is enthronement imagery, not descent imagery.

Jesus repeatedly applies this passage to Himself. (Matt 26:64)

5) “Coming on the Clouds” — Authority Language

Cloud-coming language in Daniel is drawn from Old Testament judgment texts. It signifies divine authority and vindication.

Important
When Jesus says His hearers will “see the Son of Man coming on the clouds,” He is claiming Danielic authority — not predicting a delayed physical appearance.

6) Time, Times, and Half a Time

Daniel repeatedly uses bounded time phrases: “time, times, and half a time.” (Dan 7:25) (Dan 12:7)

Clarification
These expressions denote limited periods of oppression, not open-ended epochs. They culminate in judgment and vindication.

7) The Seventy Weeks — Purposeful Countdown

Daniel 9 describes seventy weeks decreed: to end transgression, to atone for sin, and to bring in everlasting righteousness. (Dan 9:24)

Fulfilled Lens
The stated purposes of the seventy weeks are accomplished in Messiah. The text does not anticipate an indefinite pause. It culminates in covenant transition.

8) Resurrection Language in Daniel

Daniel 12 speaks of many who “awake” to life or contempt. (Dan 12:2)

Covenantal Resurrection
This language reflects vindication and judgment within Israel’s covenant history. It anticipates New Testament resurrection language describing transition from old covenant death to new covenant life.

9) “The End” in Daniel

Daniel is told to seal the book “until the time of the end.” (Dan 12:4)

Clarification
“The end” is the end of Daniel’s prophetic horizon — the conclusion of the covenant era his visions addressed. Jesus explicitly locates that end within His generation.

10) Daniel in the New Testament

Daniel is the most cited prophetic framework in the New Testament:

  • Matthew 24 — abomination, tribulation, coming
  • Luke 21 — judgment on Jerusalem
  • Philippians 2 — exaltation of the Son
  • Hebrews — kingdom that cannot be shaken
  • Revelation — beasts, judgment, victory
Bottom Line
The New Testament does not treat Daniel as unfulfilled speculation. It treats Daniel as the script now being enacted.

Summary

  • Daniel describes empires and their overthrow
  • The Son of Man receives authority through enthronement
  • Cloud-coming language signals judgment and rule
  • Time statements are bounded and purposeful
  • The kingdom is established, not postponed
  • Daniel undergirds Jesus’ fulfilled eschatology
