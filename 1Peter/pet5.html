1 Peter 5

Shepherding the Flock • The Roaring Lion • The Little While

1. Immediate Context

Peter closes his letter with direct address to elders — the leaders charged with shepherding the communities through the most dangerous period of early church history. The eschatological horizon of the whole letter reaches its personal, pastoral conclusion here. The "glory about to be revealed" (v. 1), the "crown that will not fade" (v. 4), the "little while" of suffering (v. 10) — all point to the same imminent event that has driven the entire letter.

Peter writes as a man who knows he will not survive to see the AD 70 consummation himself (2 Peter 1:14). He is commissioning the elders to hold the communities together through the final window of pressure until the appearing of the Chief Shepherd vindicates them.

2. Scripture (WEB)

Source (verbatim): https://ebible.org/web/1PE05.htm

1 Therefore I exhort the elders among you, as a fellow elder, and a witness of the sufferings of Christ, and who will also share in the glory that will be revealed:
2 shepherd the flock of God which is among you, exercising oversight, not by compulsion, but willingly; not for dishonest gain, but eagerly;
3 neither as lording it over those entrusted to you, but making yourselves examples to the flock.
4 When the chief Shepherd is revealed, you will receive the crown of glory that doesn't fade away.
5 Likewise, you younger ones, be subject to the elder. Yes, all of you clothe yourselves with humility, to subject yourselves to one another; for "God resists the proud, but gives grace to the humble."
6 Humble yourselves therefore under the mighty hand of God, that he may exalt you in due time;
7 casting all your worries on him, because he cares for you.
8 Be sober and self-controlled. Be watchful. Your adversary, the devil, walks around like a roaring lion, seeking whom he may devour.
9 Withstand him steadfast in your faith, knowing that your brothers who are in the world are undergoing the same sufferings.
10 But may the God of all grace, who called you to his eternal glory in Christ Jesus, after you have suffered a little while, perfect, establish, strengthen, and settle you.
11 To him be the dominion forever and ever. Amen.
12 Through Silvanus, our faithful brother, as I consider him, I have written to you briefly, exhorting, and testifying that this is the true grace of God. Stand firm in it.
13 She who is in Babylon, chosen together with you, greets you; and so does Mark, my son.
14 Greet one another with a kiss of love. Peace be to you all who are in Christ Jesus. Amen.

3. Fulfillment Notes

  • The Glory About to be Revealed (v. 1): The Greek mellousēs apokalyptesthai — "about to be revealed," the same mellō construction used throughout the NT for imminent events (Matthew 16:27; Romans 8:18). Peter is a "partaker" of this coming glory — not a distant hope but the next event on his eschatological calendar.
  • The Chief Shepherd's Appearing (v. 4): The crown offered to the elders was specifically linked to the phanerōthēntos — the appearing — of the Chief Shepherd. If this appearing were two millennia away, the incentive to those specific first-century elders would have been meaningless. The promise was for their generation, their service, their vindication.
  • Due Time / Appointed Time (v. 6): En kairō — at the right covenantal moment, the appointed time of visitation and vindication. This is the same kairos language used throughout the NT for the AD 70 horizon.
  • A Little While (v. 10): See Hebrews 10:37 ("He who is coming will come, and will not wait") and Revelation 6:11 ("a little while longer"). This is a consistent NT formula for the brief window between the writing and the parousia — measured in years, not millennia.
  • Babylon (v. 13): Nearly universally understood by scholars as a cryptonym for Rome — the empire that, like ancient Babylon, had taken the covenant people captive. Peter writing from "Babylon" mirrors Jeremiah writing to the Babylonian exiles.

4. Annotations

  • fellow elder (v. 1) — Peter does not lead from above but from alongside. His authority is the eyewitness authority of one who was present at the sufferings of Christ — not an office but a testimony.
  • dishonest gain (v. 2) — The counterfeit shepherd leading for financial benefit — a problem as old as Ezekiel 34. The elder who serves from that motive has already disqualified himself from the crown.
  • the proud / the humble (v. 5) — From Proverbs 3:34. The humble are those aligned with the direction of the covenant: downward before God, upward in Christ. The proud are those grasping for the old world's power structures that are about to be overthrown.
  • perfect, establish, strengthen, settle (v. 10) — Four Greek verbs, each describing a dimension of the consolidation that would come after the storm: completeness, foundation, power, and stability. These are the characteristics of the New Creation community on the far side of the AD 70 transition.
  • peace be to you all who are in Christ (v. 14) — Shalom pronounced over those in the Messiah. The New Creation is the reign of peace — not peace as absence of conflict but peace as the established order of the age that cannot be shaken.

5. Fulfillment Commentary

1) The Imminent Appearing as Leadership Incentive (vv. 1–4)

Peter addresses the elders as a "fellow elder" — collapsing any hierarchical distance — and then offers them a specific, time-anchored incentive: when the Chief Shepherd is revealed, you will receive an unfading crown. This was not a carrot on an infinitely long stick. It was a genuine near-term promise to men who were risking their lives to shepherd persecuted communities. The "glory about to be revealed" (mellousēs) was the next item on the eschatological agenda: the AD 70 manifestation of Christ's reign. Their faithful service through the final window would be publicly vindicated at that appearing.

2) Humility as Eschatological Posture (vv. 5–7)

The call to "clothe yourselves with humility" and to humble yourselves under "the mighty hand of God" is eschatological at its core. The "mighty hand" is Exodus language — the same hand that brought Israel out of Egypt, that judged Pharaoh. Peter's readers were living through a new Exodus, and the mighty hand was again moving. To humble themselves under it was to align with the direction of the covenant. God would exalt them "in due time" — in the kairos, the appointed covenantal moment — which was the AD 70 vindication.

Verse 7 — "casting all your worries on him, because he cares for you" — is the personal pastoral application of the whole letter's eschatology. Their anxieties were real: persecution, social displacement, economic pressure, the approaching collapse of the world they had known. Peter's answer: the God who is moving in history with mighty-hand power is the same God who cares for you individually. Let him carry the weight.

3) The Roaring Lion in Its First-Century Form (vv. 8–9)

The "roaring lion seeking whom he may devour" was not a generic spiritual bogeyman. In the AD 60s, it had a specific embodiment: the combined force of Roman state persecution and Jewish synagogue opposition, both of which were "seeking" to destroy the Christian communities. The word "devour" (katapien) is the same word used in Revelation 12:4 for the dragon seeking to devour the child. The first-century cosmic conflict between the kingdom of Christ and the powers of the dying age is the frame.

Verse 9 grounds the resistance in solidarity: "your brothers in the world are undergoing the same sufferings." The scattered communities of Asia Minor were not alone; the whole church across the Roman world was in the same fire. This solidarity — the awareness of being part of a worldwide community of witnesses — was itself a resource of resistance.

4) The Little While (v. 10)

The promise of v. 10 is the pastoral heart of the letter. "After you have suffered a little while" — Peter does not say "someday" or "eventually." He says a little while. The same phrase appears in Hebrews 10:37, quoting Habakkuk: "Yet a little while, and he who is coming will come and will not wait." In Revelation 6:11, the martyrs are told to rest "a little while longer." Across the NT this is a consistent formula for the brief window between writing and parousia.

After that little while: perfect, establish, strengthen, and settle. Four verbs of consolidation. The storm was real and brief; the New Creation on the other side was permanent. Peter commissioned the elders to hold the communities together until that settlement came.

5) Babylon and the Closing Peace (vv. 12–14)

Peter's closing identifies his location as "Babylon" — Rome — and sends greetings from the church there and from Mark. The use of "Babylon" as a cryptonym for Rome appears also in Revelation 17–18, where the harlot city is identified with the seven hills and with the empire that spills the blood of prophets. Peter writing from "Babylon" to the diaspora perfectly mirrors Jeremiah writing to the Babylonian exiles: stand firm in the grace of God; the king who thinks he owns you will fall. The letter closes with "peace" — the shalom of the New Creation, the established order of the age that cannot be shaken, pronounced over all who are in Christ.

  • Revelation 17:9, 18 — "Babylon" as the city on seven hills, confirming that Peter's "Babylon" = Rome.

8. New Creation Reality

The "little while" is over. The storm that Peter's readers braced for came, did its work, and passed. The New Creation they were being prepared for is the order in which you now live. The four verbs of v. 10 — perfect, establish, strengthen, settle — describe your inheritance, not your aspiration. You are settled in the New Creation. The Chief Shepherd has appeared. The crown that does not fade is the covenant life already given. The task now is to shepherd those entrusted to you with the same willingness, eagerness, and example that Peter commissioned from his elders — because the King who will vindicate that service already reigns.

9. Reflection

Question: Peter's pastoral instructions were shaped entirely by the eschatological horizon — the imminent appearing of the Chief Shepherd gave the elders both their incentive and their model. How does knowing that the appearing has occurred and the vindication is complete change the way you approach leadership or service in your community?
