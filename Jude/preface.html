Preface — Jude

The Letter That Was Never Planned

Jude opens with one of the most revealing confessions in the New Testament epistles. He had intended to write about the common salvation — a positive treatment of the shared life in Christ. Instead, an infiltration of apostates forced a different letter entirely.

This is not general church correspondence. It is a targeted, urgent dispatch written into a specific crisis in the AD 60s, addressed to communities being corrupted from within.

How Jude Is Commonly Misread

Jude is frequently treated as:

  • a general warning about false teachers in every era of church history
  • a source of exotic angelology and apocalyptic speculation
  • a stern moral lecture disconnected from its historical occasion

All of these readings flatten the letter's precision. Jude is not writing to the church in general. He is writing to a specific community facing a specific and imminent judgment on specific people.

The Actual Situation Jude Addresses

Jude writes to communities that have been infiltrated by people who:

  • turned grace into a licence for moral chaos
  • denied the Lordship of Christ while maintaining religious form
  • were present at the love feasts — eating with the community while corrupting it
  • despised authority and slandered the things they did not understand

These were not outsiders attacking the church. They were insiders corrupting it. Their danger was precisely their proximity.

The Three Historical Types

Jude's argument is typological. He lines up three Old Testament precedents to prove a single principle: God has a consistent, twice-confirmed-and-more track record of judging apostasy and preserving the faithful.

  • The Exodus generation — rescued from Egypt, then destroyed in the wilderness for unbelief
  • The fallen angels — who abandoned their proper domain and are held in darkness for judgment
  • Sodom and Gomorrah — who gave themselves to immorality and stand as a permanent example

All three types point to the same conclusion: the apostates Jude is describing have walked into a pattern with a known outcome. Their judgment is not uncertain or distant. It is as certain as the Flood.

The "Once for All" Faith

Jude's call to "contend earnestly for the faith once for all delivered to the saints" is often read as a mandate to defend doctrinal correctness in the abstract.

In context, it is more specific: the faith has been completely and finally delivered to the first-century saints. There is no version 2.0 coming. The apostates were not proposing additions to an incomplete revelation — they were corrupting a complete one.

The word translated "once for all" (hapax) carries the force of finality and closure. The deposit is sealed. The task is to hold it, live from it, and resist those who distort it.

The Doxology as the Final Word

Jude's letter ends not with anxiety but with one of the great doxologies of the New Testament. "To him who is able to keep you from stumbling, and to present you faultless before the presence of his glory with exceeding joy."

This is the pastoral resolution of the whole crisis. The apostates will be judged. The faithful will be kept and presented. The God who holds the outcome is the God to whom all glory, majesty, dominion, and power belong — now and forever.

Jude writes in crisis but closes in settled confidence. That movement — from alarm to doxology — is the shape of the whole letter, and the shape of New Creation life.

How to Read Jude

  • Read it as a first-century document addressed to a first-century crisis
  • Let the three types do their typological work — they establish certainty, not speculation
  • Watch for the pastoral movement from warning to doxology
  • Resist the temptation to turn every verse into a reference to present-day false teachers
  • Read the doxology as the letter's real conclusion — not an add-on but the whole point

Why Jude Matters Now

The specific crisis Jude addressed was resolved in AD 70. The apostates he described were consumed in the same judgment they denied was coming.

What remains is the doxology, the once-for-all faith, and the three-type pattern that assures every reader: God knows how to deliver the godly out of temptation. He has done it before. He did it in the generation Jude was writing to. He does it still.
