Luke 23

The Verdict of Innocence • The Tearing of the Veil • The Opening of Paradise

1. Immediate Context

Luke 23 presents the legal and historical climax of the Messiah’s earthly journey. Within the AD 30–70 transition, the trial and crucifixion were not a "failure" of the Kingdom but its definitive jurisdictional victory. Standing before Pilate and Herod, the Son is subjected to the scrutiny of the highest earthly powers, only to be declared innocent. This lack of fault legally established Him as the "Unblemished Lamb" capable of exhausting the Adamic debt. The cross became the seat of His sovereign judgment upon the old world.

The chapter documents the specific "Day of the Lord" in microcosm. As the Sun was darkened and the Temple veil was torn, the Old Covenant structures were legally and physically disrupted. The entry of the "Thief" into Paradise by a single word from the Son proved that the New Creation is a realm of direct, unhindered access. As the AD 70 horizon loomed, these events signaled to the first-century generation that the old house was being closed and the new, eternal sanctuary was being opened. The burial by Joseph of Arimathea marks the end of the "Old Week," setting the stage for the dawn of the New World in the resurrection.

2. Scripture (WEB)

Source (verbatim): https://ebible.org/web/LUK23.htm

1 The whole company of them rose up and brought him before Pilate. 2 They began to accuse him, saying, “We found this man perverting the nation, forbidding paying taxes to Caesar, and saying that he himself is Christ, a king.”
3 Pilate asked him, “Are you the King of the Jews?” He answered him, “So you say.”
4 Pilate said to the chief priests and the multitudes, “I find no basis for a charge against this man.”
5 But they insisted, saying, “He stirs up the people, teaching throughout all Judea, beginning from Galilee even to this place.”
6 But when Pilate heard Galilee mentioned, he asked if the man was a Galilean. 7 When he learned that he was from Herod’s jurisdiction, he sent him to Herod, who himself also was in Jerusalem in those days.
8 Now when Herod saw Jesus, he was exceedingly glad, for he had wanted to see him for a long time, because he had heard many things about him. He hoped to see some miracle done by him. 9 He questioned him with many words, but he gave him no answer. 10 The chief priests and the scribes stood, vehemently accusing him. 11 Herod with his soldiers humiliated him and mocked him. Dressing him in luxurious clothing, they sent him back to Pilate. 12 Herod and Pilate became friends with each other that very day, for before they were enemies with each other.
13 Pilate called together the chief priests, the rulers, and the people, 14 and said to them, “You brought this man to me as one that perverts the people, and behold, I, having examined him before you, found no basis for a charge against this man concerning those things of which you accuse him. 15 Neither has Herod, for I sent you to him, and behold, nothing worthy of death has been done by him. 16 I will therefore chastise him and release him.”
17 Now he had to release one prisoner to them at the feast. 18 But they all cried out together, saying, “Away with this man! Release to us Barabbas!” 19 —one who was thrown into prison for a certain revolt in the city, and for murder.
20 Pilate, wanting to release Jesus, spoke to them again; 21 but they shouted, saying, “Crucify! Crucify him!”
22 He said to them the third time, “Why? What evil has this man done? I have found no capital crime in him. I will therefore chastise him and release him.”
23 But they were urgent with loud voices, asking that he might be crucified. Their voices and the voices of the chief priests prevailed. 24 Pilate decreed that their demand should be granted. 25 He released him who had been thrown into prison for insurrection and murder, for whom they asked, but he delivered Jesus up to their will.
26 When they led him away, they seized one Simon of Cyrene, coming from the country, and laid on him the cross, to carry it after Jesus. 27 A great multitude of the people followed him, including women who also mourned and lamented him. 28 But Jesus, turning to them, said, “Daughters of Jerusalem, don’t weep for me, but weep for yourselves and for your children. 29 For behold, the days are coming in which they will say, ‘Blessed are the barren, the wombs that never bore, and the breasts that never nursed.’ 30 Then they will begin to tell the mountains, ‘Fall on us!’ and tell the hills, ‘Cover us!’ 31 For if they do these things in the green tree, what will be done in the dry?”
32 There were also two others, criminals, led with him to be put to death. 33 When they came to the place which is called ‘The Skull,’ there they crucified him and the criminals, one on the right and one on the left.
34 Jesus said, “Father, forgive them, for they don’t know what they are doing.” Dividing his garments among them, they cast lots. 35 The people stood watching. The rulers with them also scoffed at him, saying, “He saved others. Let him save himself, if this is the Christ of God, his chosen one!”
36 The soldiers also mocked him, coming to him and offering him vinegar, 37 and saying, “If you are the King of the Jews, save yourself!”
38 An inscription was also written over him in letters of Greek, Latin, and Hebrew: “THIS IS THE KING OF THE JEWS.”
39 One of the criminals who was hanged insulted him, saying, “If you are the Christ, save yourself and us!”
40 But the other answered, and rebuking him said, “Don’t you even fear God, seeing you are under the same condemnation? 41 And we indeed justly, for we receive the due reward for our deeds, but this man has done nothing wrong.” 42 He said to Jesus, “Lord, remember me when you come into your Kingdom.”
43 Jesus said to him, “Assuredly I tell you, today you will be with me in Paradise.”
44 It was now about the sixth hour, and darkness came over the whole land until the ninth hour. 45 The sun was darkened, and the veil of the temple was torn in two. 46 Jesus, crying with a loud voice, said, “Father, into your hands I commit my spirit!” Having said this, he breathed his last.
47 When the centurion saw what was done, he glorified God, saying, “Certainly this was a righteous man.” 48 All the multitudes that came together to see this, when they saw the things that were done, returned, beating their breasts. 49 All his acquaintances, and the women who followed him from Galilee, stood far off, watching these things.
50 Behold, a man named Joseph, who was a member of the council, a good and righteous man 51 (he had not consented to their counsel and deed), a man of Arimathea, a city of the Jews, who was also waiting for God’s Kingdom: 52 this man went to Pilate, and asked for Jesus’ body. 53 He took it down, and wrapped it in a linen cloth, and laid him in a tomb that was cut in stone, where no one had ever been laid. 54 It was the day of the Preparation, and the Sabbath was drawing near. 55 The women, who had come with him out of Galilee, followed after, and saw the tomb, and how his body was laid. 56 They returned, and prepared spices and ointments. On the Sabbath they rested according to the commandment.

3. Fulfillment Notes

  • I Find No Basis for a Charge (v. 4, 14, 22): Three times the Roman jurisdiction declared the Son innocent. This legally satisfied the requirement for the Spotless Lamb of the New Covenant transition.
  • Fall on Us / Cover Us (v. 30): A direct prophecy of the AD 70 destruction of Jerusalem. Quoting Hosea 10:8, Jesus identifies the city’s impending ruin as a historical judgment.
  • Today in Paradise (v. 43): sēmeron met’ emou esē en tō paradeisō. The immediate opening of the New Creation realm. The Son’s word legally admitted the repentant criminal into rest "Today," bypassing all ritual requirements.
  • Veil Torn in Two (v. 45): The definitive physical sign of the Old Covenant's legal termination. The tearing of the veil signaled the end of localized mediation and the opening of direct access for the Royal Priesthood.

4. Annotations

  • Barabbas (v. 18) — Name meaning "Son of the Father." The nation chose the violent "Son" of revolt over the peaceful "Son" of God, sealing their literal fate in the AD 66–70 war.
  • Daughters of Jerusalem (v. 28) — Refers to the citizens of the Old Covenant capital. The Son's warning concerns the historical catastrophe awaiting that specific generation.
  • Paradise (v. 43) — The garden of the New World. It signifies the restoration of what was lost in Eden, now accessible by sovereign decree.
  • Preparation Day (v. 54) — The final day before the Sabbath. Legally, this was the "End of the Old Week," as the King rested in the tomb before inaugurating the New Creation.

5. Fulfillment Commentary

1) The Manifestation of Sovereign Innocence

The trial of the Son (Luke 23:1-25) unveils His glory as the only valid legal Subject. Despite the vehement accusations of the religious elite, both Pilate (Rome) and Herod (the local ruler) could find "no basis for a charge." This was a jurisdictional victory: the Son was proved righteous under the very systems meant to judge Him. The nation’s choice of Barabbas—a murderer and insurrectionist—over the innocent King was the final legal act of self-condemnation for the old world. This event signaled that the New Creation is founded on an "unimpeachable" record. The glory of the Son is seen in His silent majesty before His accusers, proving that He was not being "punished" for His own deeds, but was voluntarily exhausting the legal requirements of a world that had rejected its Creator.

2) The Prophecy of National Desolation

On the way to the Skull (Luke 23:26-31), the Son delivered a final eschatological briefing to the "Daughters of Jerusalem." He warned them that the tragedy they were witnessing was a microcosm of the "Day of Vengeance" awaiting the city in AD 70. By quoting Hosea, He identified Jerusalem as a "dry tree" destined for the fire of judgment. This commentary affirms that the transition was a move from the physical, earthly city of stone to the spiritual city of the Spirit. The Son's glory is unveiled in His compassion, even as He sets the legal horizon: the collapse of the old covenant system was necessary to preserve the "green tree" of the New Creation. This was the final jurisdictional notice that the "Daughters" must exit the old house to find safety in the King's Kingdom.

3) The Jurisdictional Opening of Paradise

The dialogue between the Son and the repentant criminal (Luke 23:39-43) is the legal summary of the New World. While the other criminal and the rulers mocked His "power," one man recognized His "Kingdom." Jesus's response—"Today you will be with me in Paradise"—was a sovereign decree that bypassed every religious mediator and Temple ritual. This act proved that the New Creation is a realm of direct, immediate restoration. The Son holds the keys to Paradise and uses them by His Word alone. This event informed the transition generation that the "Thief" of distance and shame had been evicted. The glory of the King is seen in His authority to grant a "Today" status to the most unworthy, establishing the rest of Paradise as the permanent home for all who recognize His reign.

4) The Tearing of the Old World Veil

The death of the Son (Luke 23:44-49) marks the physical and legal end of the Mosaic order. The darkening of the sun and the "tearing of the temple veil" were the cosmic signals that the Old Covenant was finished. The veil, which had legally barred humanity from the Presence for centuries, was removed by the Sovereign’s final breath. This was the AD 30 "Opening" that would be historically finalized in the AD 70 "Removal." The Son’s cry—"Into your hands I commit my spirit"—manifested the absolute trust and rest that defines the New Creation. This commentary highlights that our Standing is now an "Open Field." The centurion’s confession that "certainly this was a righteous man" acts as the first Gentile recognition of the New World's foundation. The victory is won, the veil is gone, and the King's work is legally complete.

8. New Creation Reality

In Luke 23, the glory of the Son is revealed as the power that tears the veil and grants you "Today" status in Paradise. As a citizen of the New Creation, you are the criminal who has been remembered and the "centurion" who now sees the Light. You do not live in a world of "basis for a charge," for the King's innocence has been legally applied to your life. The "Thief" of religious exclusion has been silenced forever. You inhabit a world where the veil is gone, and the Father’s hands are wide open to receive your spirit. You are invited to live in the "Rest" of the finished victory, knowing that the "Skull" has been turned into a "Throne" and the old city's ruin has paved the way for your eternal joy. Paradise is no longer a future hope; it is your current jurisdiction.
