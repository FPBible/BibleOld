2 Peter 1

Divine Nature Already Given • The Transfiguration Anchor • The Day Dawning

1. Immediate Context

2 Peter is Peter's final letter, written with explicit awareness of his own impending death (v. 14). The letter addresses a crisis that would deepen into chapter 3: mockers within the community denying the imminence of the parousia. Chapter 1 establishes the foundation for everything that follows: the divine power has already given everything needed for life and godliness, and the parousia is not a myth but an eyewitness-certified reality, anchored in the Transfiguration.

The letter is Peter's testament — the final deposit of apostolic testimony before his death. Its urgency is the urgency of a man who knows the day is breaking but also knows he will not live to see it dawn fully.

2. Scripture (WEB)

Source (verbatim): https://ebible.org/web/2PE01.htm

1 Simon Peter, a servant and apostle of Jesus Christ, to those who have obtained a like precious faith with us in the righteousness of our God and Savior, Jesus Christ:
2 Grace to you and peace be multiplied in the knowledge of God and of Jesus our Lord,
3 seeing that his divine power has granted to us all things that pertain to life and godliness, through the knowledge of him who called us by his own glory and virtue;
4 by which he has granted to us his precious and exceedingly great promises; that through these you may become partakers of the divine nature, having escaped from the corruption that is in the world by lust.
5 Yes, and for this very cause adding on your part all diligence, in your faith supply moral excellence; and in moral excellence, knowledge;
6 and in knowledge, self-control; and in self-control, perseverance; and in perseverance, godliness;
7 and in godliness, brotherly affection; and in brotherly affection, love.
8 For if these things are yours and abound, they make you to be not idle nor unfruitful in the knowledge of our Lord Jesus Christ.
9 For he who lacks these things is blind, seeing only what is near, having forgotten the cleansing from his old sins.
10 Therefore, brothers, be more diligent to make your calling and election sure. For if you do these things, you will never stumble.
11 For thus you will be richly supplied with the entrance into the eternal Kingdom of our Lord and Savior, Jesus Christ.
12 Therefore I will not be negligent to remind you of these things, though you know them, and are established in the present truth.
13 I think it right, as long as I am in this tent, to stir you up by reminding you;
14 knowing that the putting off of my tent is coming shortly, even as our Lord Jesus Christ made clear to me.
15 Yes, I will make every effort that you may always be able to remember these things even after my departure.
16 For we didn't follow cunningly devised fables when we made known to you the power and coming of our Lord Jesus Christ, but we were eyewitnesses of his majesty.
17 For he received from God the Father honor and glory, when the voice came to him from the Majestic Glory, "This is my beloved Son, in whom I am well pleased."
18 We heard this voice come out of heaven when we were with him on the holy mountain.
19 We have the more sure prophetic word; and you do well that you heed it, as to a lamp shining in a dark place, until the day dawns, and the morning star arises in your hearts.
20 knowing this first, that no prophecy of Scripture is of private interpretation.
21 For no prophecy ever came by the will of man: but holy men of God spoke, being moved by the Holy Spirit.

3. Fulfillment Notes

  • All Things Already Given (v. 3): Perfect tense — dedōrēmenēs. God's divine power has already granted everything needed for life and godliness. This is the foundation of 2 Peter's ethics: not "try harder to earn what you need" but "live from what has already been given."
  • Power and Coming (v. 16): Parousia — presence. Peter grounds the parousia not in future speculation but in his eyewitness experience at the Transfiguration. The glory he saw on the mountain was the preview of the glory about to be revealed in the fall of Jerusalem and the full manifestation of Christ's reign.
  • The Transfiguration as Parousia Preview (vv. 17–18): Luke 9:31 records that Moses and Elijah spoke with Jesus at the Transfiguration about his "departure" (exodos) — his death, resurrection, and the judgment it would bring. The Transfiguration was a preview of the parousia — the King revealed in glory.
  • Until the Day Dawns (v. 19): The prophetic word was a lamp for the dark period between the Resurrection and the parousia. The "day dawning" and "morning star arising" are the imagery of the Day of the Lord breaking through — the end of the Old Covenant darkness. For Peter's first-century readers, this was not a distant metaphor; it was a breaking dawn.

4. Annotations

  • partakers of the divine nature (v. 4) — Covenant union with God — the restoration of the Presence lost in Adam. Not biological deification but covenantal intimacy: dwelling in, and being indwelt by, the God who called them.
  • corruption that is in the world by lust (v. 4) — The Old Covenant world-system in its terminal state of spiritual decay. "Having escaped" is aorist — they have already crossed out of it.
  • seeing only what is near (v. 9) — The literal Greek is "short-sighted" (myōpazōn). The one who lacks the virtues is spiritually myopic — fixated on the visible, passing age and unable to see the covenant realities already operative.
  • my tent / putting off of my tent (vv. 13–14) — Physical life as a temporary dwelling. Peter expects to "put it off" shortly. The reference is to John 21:18–19, where Jesus predicted the manner of Peter's death.
  • no private interpretation (v. 20) — Not a ban on personal Bible reading but a hermeneutical warning: the prophetic scriptures must be read within the covenantal and apostolic framework in which they were given, not bent to serve private agendas — specifically the agenda of denying the imminent parousia.

5. Fulfillment Commentary

1) The Foundation: Everything Already Given (vv. 3–4)

The opening theological declaration of 2 Peter is the most important sentence in the letter: God's divine power has already granted all things pertaining to life and godliness. Peter does not say "will grant." He says "has granted." The transition generation was not waiting to receive what they needed for the New Covenant life. They had already been given it — through the knowledge of Christ who called them by his own glory and virtue. This is the foundation that makes all the ethics of the chapter flow naturally rather than as burdensome performance.

2) The Ladder of Virtues (vv. 5–9)

The chain from faith to love is not a works programme. It is a description of covenantal maturity — the progressive expansion into the fullness of what has already been given. Each quality flows from and into the next; love is the apex because it is the complete expression of the new-creation life. To lack these qualities is to be "short-sighted," fixated on the visible and passing. The specific blindness Peter has in mind becomes clear in chapter 3: it is the blindness of those who cannot see the parousia coming because they are looking only at the material, unchanged surface of the world.

3) The Eternal Kingdom Entrance (vv. 10–11)

The "richly supplied entrance into the eternal Kingdom" was the consummation toward which the transition generation was moving. In the first-century context, this entrance was the full, formal establishment of the New Covenant Kingdom at the destruction of the Old Covenant Temple. Those who cultivated the virtues of vv. 5–7 would enter that kingdom not barely but richly — with confidence and abundance rather than with the shame of the compromised.

4) Peter's Death and the Written Testament (vv. 12–15)

The urgency of this chapter is not general spiritual fervour. It is the urgency of a man writing his last will and testament. Peter knows he will not live to see the AD 70 fulfilment (John 21:18–19 had told him how and when he would die). He therefore labours to write down what he knows so that "after my departure" the communities would still have the apostolic witness in written form. This is the context for the entire letter: Peter's final deposit of eschatological clarity before the crisis of chapters 2–3 hits.

5) The Transfiguration as the Parousia's Guarantee (vv. 16–18)

Peter's defence of the parousia against the mockers (addressed directly in chapter 3) begins here with the most solid possible foundation: personal eyewitness testimony. He was on the mountain. He heard the voice. He saw the glory. And that glory — the Majestic Glory of the Transfiguration — was not an isolated event but a preview of the parousia. Luke 9:31 records that Moses and Elijah spoke with Jesus at the Transfiguration about his "departure" (exodos) — specifically his coming death and the judgment/vindication it would unleash. The Transfiguration was Christ "previewing" his parousia glory. When Peter says he was an eyewitness of "his majesty," he is saying: I have already seen, with my own eyes, the glory that is coming. It is not a myth.

6) The Lamp Until the Day Dawns (vv. 19–21)

The Old Testament prophetic word was, for the early church, a lamp in the darkness of the inter-covenantal period — the time between Christ's resurrection and the full establishment of the New Creation order. They were to heed it "until the day dawns" — until the Day of the Lord broke in its full light and the "morning star" (Christ's full manifestation) arose in their hearts. For Peter's readers in the AD 60s, this was not a distant metaphor. They were in the last minutes of darkness before the dawn. The warning against "private interpretation" (v. 20) is specifically aimed at those who would twist the prophetic scriptures to avoid the implications of the imminent parousia — the false teachers whom Peter is about to expose in chapter 2.

8. New Creation Reality

Peter wrote his last letter as a man running out of time — not in despair, but in urgency. The day was dawning; he would not live to see full sunrise. Yet his letter breathes confidence not anxiety, because everything needed had already been given. The divine power that "has granted all things" to Peter's readers has granted them to you. The lamp that guided them through the dark inter-covenantal period has been superseded by the day itself. The morning star has risen. You are not reading prophecy in a lamp-lit room, straining to see when the dawn will come. You are standing in the daylight, reading the record of those who waited for it.

9. Reflection

Question: Peter grounds the reality of the parousia in his own eyewitness experience at the Transfiguration — not in abstract theology but in "we were there; we saw the glory." What is the experiential foundation of your own confidence in the New Creation reality — and does it have the same concrete, historical rootedness that Peter's did?
