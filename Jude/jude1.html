Jude 1

Contending for the Faith • The Apostates Ordained of Old • Enoch's Prophecy

1. Immediate Context

Jude, the brother of James and half-brother of Jesus, writes with compressed urgency. He had intended to write about "our common salvation" but was compelled instead to address an emergency: apostates had crept into the communities, turning grace into licence and denying the Lordship of Christ. His letter is the shortest in the New Testament and one of the most concentrated in eschatological intensity.

The letter is closely parallel to 2 Peter 2 — Jude and Peter are addressing the same crisis from slightly different angles. Written in the AD 60s, the judgment Jude announces is not vague or distant. These apostates were "of old ordained to this condemnation" — their sentence was already written into the prophetic record, and its execution was imminent. Jude lines up three Old Testament types — the Exodus generation, the fallen angels, and Sodom and Gomorrah — as triple precedents for what was about to happen to the infiltrators.

2. Scripture (WEB)

Source (verbatim): https://ebible.org/web/JUD01.htm

1 Jude, a servant of Jesus Christ, and brother of James, to those who are called, sanctified by God the Father, and kept for Jesus Christ:
2 Mercy to you and peace and love be multiplied.
3 Beloved, while I was very eager to write to you about our common salvation, I was constrained to write to you exhorting you to contend earnestly for the faith which was once for all delivered to the saints.
4 For there are certain men who crept in secretly, even those who were of old written of for this condemnation: ungodly men, turning the grace of our God into indecency, and denying our only Master, God, and Lord, Jesus Christ.
5 Now I desire to remind you, though you already know this, that the Lord, having saved a people out of the land of Egypt, afterward destroyed those who didn't believe.
6 Angels who didn't keep their first domain, but deserted their own dwelling place, he has kept in everlasting bonds under darkness for the judgment of the great day.
7 Even as Sodom and Gomorrah, and the cities around them, having, in the same way as these, given themselves over to sexual immorality and gone after strange flesh, are set forth as an example, suffering the punishment of eternal fire.
8 Yet in the same way, these also in their dreaming defile the flesh, despise authority, and slander celestial beings.
9 But Michael, the archangel, when contending with the devil and arguing about the body of Moses, didn't dare bring a railing accusation against him, but said, "May the Lord rebuke you!"
10 But these speak evil of whatever things they don't know. What they understand naturally, like the creatures without reason, they are destroyed in these things.
11 Woe to them! For they went in the way of Cain, and ran riotously in the error of Balaam for hire, and perished in Korah's rebellion.
12 These men are hidden rocky reefs in your love feasts when they feast with you, shepherds who without fear feed themselves; clouds without water, carried along by winds; autumn trees without fruit, twice dead, plucked up by the roots;
13 wild waves of the sea, foaming out their own shame; wandering stars, for whom the blackness of darkness has been reserved forever.
14 About these also Enoch, the seventh from Adam, prophesied, saying, "Behold, the Lord came with ten thousands of his holy ones,
15 to execute judgment on all, and to convict all the ungodly of all their works of ungodliness which they have done in an ungodly way, and of all the hard things which ungodly sinners have spoken against him."
16 These are murmurers and complainers, walking after their lusts (and their mouth speaks proud things), showing respect of persons to gain advantage.
17 But you, beloved, remember the words which have been spoken before by the apostles of our Lord Jesus Christ.
18 They said to you that "In the last time there will be mockers, walking after their own ungodly lusts."
19 These are they who cause divisions, and are sensual, not having the Spirit.
20 But you, beloved, keep building up yourselves on your most holy faith, praying in the Holy Spirit.
21 Keep yourselves in the love of God, looking for the mercy of our Lord Jesus Christ to eternal life.
22 On some have compassion, making a distinction,
23 and some save, snatching them out of the fire; and on some have mercy with fear, hating even the clothing spotted by the flesh.
24 Now to him who is able to keep you from stumbling, and to present you faultless before the presence of his glory with exceeding joy,
25 to God our Savior, who alone is wise, be glory and majesty, dominion and power, both now and forever. Amen.

3. Fulfillment Notes

  • Once for All Delivered (v. 3): The Greek hapax paradotheisē — delivered once, completely, finally. This is a closed deposit. Jude is not defending a faith still being constructed; he is defending a faith already fully given to the first-century saints. The content of the faith is complete and settled.
  • Of Old Ordained to This Condemnation (v. 4): The apostates' judgment was already written into the prophetic record before they appeared. Jude is not issuing a new sentence; he is identifying men who had walked into a sentence already announced. Their condemnation was a matter of covenant record, not personal vendetta.
  • The Last Time (v. 18): "In the last time there will be mockers." The last time is the same phrase as 1 Peter 1:5 and 1 John 2:18 — the final period of the Old Covenant age, AD 30–70. The mockers Jude described were the sign that his generation was living in that final period.
  • Enoch's Prophecy (vv. 14–15): Jude quotes 1 Enoch 1:9, applying it to the apostate teachers of his day. "The Lord came with ten thousands of his holy ones to execute judgment" — the prophetic aorist presents the coming judgment as so certain it can be described as already accomplished. The "ungodly" being judged were the apostate generation of the first century.
  • Sodom as Antitype (v. 7): Revelation 11:8 identifies Jerusalem as "Sodom and Egypt." Jude's use of Sodom as an "example" of the judgment the apostates faced points forward to the same AD 70 event. The "eternal fire" is covenant-judgment language, not a description of ongoing physical combustion.

4. Annotations

  • crept in secretly (v. 4) — pareisedysan — slipped in alongside, infiltrated. These were not outsiders attacking; they were insiders corrupting. Their danger was precisely their proximity to the community.
  • the body of Moses (v. 9) — The dispute between Michael and the devil over Moses' body is drawn from Jewish tradition (The Assumption of Moses). In covenantal terms, the "body of Moses" is the Mosaic system itself — the old covenant administration. The devil's claim on it was the accusatory power of the Law; Michael's restraint signals that its removal was God's prerogative alone, executed by decree at the cross and consummated at AD 70.
  • the way of Cain (v. 11) — Murderous religion — worship on one's own terms that produces hatred rather than love. The apostate teachers were spiritually violent even while maintaining religious form.
  • the error of Balaam (v. 11) — Using prophetic gifting for financial gain while leading the community into moral compromise (Numbers 22–25). The same pattern Peter addresses in 2 Peter 2:15.
  • Korah's rebellion (v. 11) — Numbers 16: challenging divinely appointed authority and claiming equal standing without divine appointment. The three examples (Cain, Balaam, Korah) are a complete typology of apostate behaviour: wrong worship, corrupt prophecy, illegitimate authority-claims.
  • clouds without water (v. 12) — Promising life-giving rain but delivering nothing. The image is of teachers who maintained the appearance of spiritual authority while offering no genuine nourishment.
  • twice dead, plucked up by the roots (v. 12) — Dead in the old covenant, then briefly alive in the New, and now dead again through apostasy — and this time with no root to regenerate from. See 2 Peter 2:20–22.

5. Fulfillment Commentary

1) The Emergency That Redirected the Letter (vv. 3–4)

Jude's opening is one of the most revealing moments in the New Testament epistles. He had a letter planned — a treatment of "our common salvation." Instead, the crisis of apostasy forced a different document. This is not a general theological treatise; it is an emergency dispatch. The infiltrators had already arrived. Their sentence was already written. The task was to mobilise the community to recognise them and resist them before the judgment that was already on its way completed its work.

2) The Three-Type Argument (vv. 5–7)

Jude's typological argument is the mirror of 2 Peter 2. Three historical events demonstrate a single principle: God judges apostasy decisively and preserves the faithful. The Exodus generation — rescued from Egypt, then destroyed in the wilderness for unbelief. The fallen angels — who abandoned their proper domain and are now held in darkness for judgment. Sodom and Gomorrah — who gave themselves to sexual immorality and stand as a permanent "example." All three types converge on the first-century apostates. They have walked into a pattern with a known outcome.

3) The Body of Moses (v. 9)

Michael's refusal to bring a railing accusation against the devil in the dispute over Moses' body is Jude's model for how to handle apostates: not with personal invective but with the authority of the Lord's own word. More significantly, the "body of Moses" in covenantal terms is the Mosaic administration itself — the old covenant system. The devil held a legitimate accusatory claim on it through the Law's power to condemn. Michael's restraint points to the fact that the removal of the Mosaic system was God's action alone, executed through the cross and visibly consummated in AD 70.

4) The Three Woes: Cain, Balaam, Korah (v. 11)

The three Old Testament apostasy archetypes Jude invokes are not random. Cain represents religion on human terms — refusing God's prescribed form of approach and responding to correction with violence. Balaam represents the prostitution of prophetic gifting for personal profit — leading Israel into the very sin that brought the plague of Numbers 25. Korah represents the illegitimate claim to priestly authority — challenging Moses and Aaron with the assertion that all the community is equally holy. Together they form a complete profile of the apostate teacher: wrong worship, corrupt prophecy, and unlawful authority-claims.

5) Enoch's Prophecy and the Certain Coming Judgment (vv. 14–15)

Jude's quotation of 1 Enoch 1:9 is remarkable. He treats it as authoritative prophecy and applies it directly to the first-century apostates. The "Lord coming with ten thousands of his holy ones" is the parousia in judgment — the same event described in Matthew 24:30–31, 1 Thessalonians 3:13, and Revelation 19. For Jude's first-century readers, this was not a distant event. It was the imminent vindication of the saints and the destruction of the apostates who were currently troubling their communities.

6) The Last Time and the Community's Response (vv. 17–23)

The reminder in v. 18 — "in the last time there will be mockers" — is Jude's explicit location of his readers in the end of the Old Covenant age. The apostles had predicted this; now it was happening; therefore, this was the last time. The community's response is not panic or counter-attack but the steady, Spirit-grounded faithfulness of vv. 20–23: building up on the most holy faith, praying in the Spirit, keeping in the love of God, looking for mercy — and simultaneously reaching toward those on the edge, snatching some from the fire with fear and discernment.

7) The Doxology of Settled Security (vv. 24–25)

Jude closes with one of the great doxologies of the New Testament. "To him who is able to keep you from stumbling, and to present you faultless before the presence of his glory with exceeding joy." This is not wishful thinking in the face of crisis. It is the bedrock confidence of those who know who holds them. The apostates will be judged; the faithful will be presented. The doxology is the pastoral resolution of the whole letter: in the middle of the crisis, the God who keeps is the final word.

  • 2 Peter 2 — The parallel treatment of the same apostate crisis with the same three types: angels, Noah/flood, Sodom.
  • Numbers 16:1–35 — Korah's rebellion — one of Jude's three apostasy archetypes (v. 11).
  • Numbers 25:1–9 — Balaam's error at Baal Peor — the pattern behind Jude's "error of Balaam" (v. 11).
  • Revelation 11:8 — Jerusalem called "Sodom and Egypt" — confirming that Jude's Sodom type points to the AD 70 judgment on the apostate city.
  • 1 John 2:18 — "It is the last hour" — the same "last time" as Jude v. 18, confirming the first-century location.

8. New Creation Reality

Jude wrote in an emergency — apostates had infiltrated, judgment was imminent, the community needed to be mobilised. But he closes not with anxiety but with the most settled doxology in the New Testament: the God who keeps, the God who presents faultless, the God to whom belong glory and majesty, dominion and power, now and forever. You are not in that emergency. The judgment Jude announced came. The apostates who denied the Lordship of Christ were consumed in the same AD 70 fire they denied was coming. The faith that was "once for all delivered to the saints" is the faith you inhabit. You are on the far side of the crisis. The task is to live from the same doxology that closed Jude's letter — fully kept, fully secure, fully presented.

9. Reflection

Question: Jude's letter was supposed to be about "our common salvation" but was redirected by crisis into a defence of the faith. What does it look like to hold both simultaneously — the settled joy of the common salvation and the alert discernment required to recognise and resist what distorts it?
